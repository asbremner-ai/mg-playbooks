Daily Mobility Routine

6 minutes. Every single day. This directly addresses the 4 physical areas most responsible for swing faults. Do it before training, before a round, or first thing in the morning.

🧘 6 Minutes, Every Day
Movement 1 — Thoracic Rotation

Unlock Shoulder Turn & Clubhead Speed

  • ▸
    How: Seated on a chair or floor, place hands on opposite shoulders (arms crossed). Rotate your torso fully left, then fully right. Hold each end range for 1 second.
  • ▸
    Reps: 10 rotations each direction. Approximately 90 seconds total.
  • ▸
    Why it matters: A restricted thoracic spine forces the lower back to rotate instead — causing both swing faults and back injury. This is the single most important mobility for golfers.
Movement 2 — Hip 90/90 Stretch

Unlock Hip Rotation for Downswing Power

  • ▸
    How: Sit on the floor with both legs bent at 90° (front shin perpendicular to body, rear shin parallel). Lean slowly forward over the front leg, keeping a straight spine.
  • ▸
    Reps: Hold 30 seconds each side. 2 minutes total.
  • ▸
    Why it matters: Tight hips prevent proper rotation in the backswing, creating a reverse pivot or early extension in the downswing. Both destroy low-point control and clubhead speed.
Movement 3 — Hip Flexor Stretch

Restore Address Position & Pelvic Tilt

  • ▸
    How: Kneeling lunge position — one knee on floor, other foot forward. Tuck your pelvis under and press the hips forward slowly until you feel a stretch in the front of the trailing hip. Keep the torso upright.
  • ▸
    Reps: 30 seconds each side. 1 minute total.
  • ▸
    Why it matters: Tight hip flexors restrict the forward hip tilt at address, causing golfers to stand too upright — which then forces a swing plane that is too upright and produces pulls and over-the-top moves.
Movement 4 — Wrist Mobility

Protect Face Angle at Impact

  • ▸
    How: Extend both arms forward. Make full clockwise and anticlockwise wrist circles, then flex and extend the wrists fully through their complete range.
  • ▸
    Reps: 10 circles each direction + 10 flex/extend. 90 seconds total.
  • ▸
    Why it matters: Lead wrist mobility affects face angle at impact. Restricted wrists create compensatory impact patterns that destroy shot consistency.
💡

Stack it with your morning: Do this routine immediately upon waking — before coffee, before your phone. It takes exactly 6 minutes and sets up your body for the day. After 3 weeks it becomes automatic. After 6 weeks you will notice the difference in your swing.

Monday — TRX Foundation Session

Building the Base

  • A
    TRX Squat: 3 × 12 reps. Feet shoulder-width, hold TRX for balance and depth assistance. Focus on controlled descent and driving through heels. Rest 60 seconds.
  • B
    TRX Row: 3 × 10 reps. Feet forward, body at 45° incline. Pull chest to handles, squeeze shoulder blades at top. Builds the pulling strength that counteracts the forward-hunched posture common in golfers.
  • C
    TRX Chest Press: 3 × 10 reps. Face away from anchor, press handles away from chest. Activates the pressing pattern that contributes to lead arm strength through impact.
  • D
    TRX Hip Hinge (Romanian Deadlift): 3 × 10 reps. Push hips back while maintaining a neutral spine, lower until mild hamstring tension, drive hips forward to stand. The most important movement pattern in golf fitness.
Wednesday — Dumbbell Hip Hinge + Core

Build the Posterior Chain

  • A
    Dumbbell Romanian Deadlift: 3 × 10. Hold a dumbbell in each hand in front of thighs. Hinge at hips pushing them back, lower weights down legs, return to standing. Start with 8–10 kg and increase as strength improves.
  • B
    Dumbbell Hip Thrust (Glute Bridge): 3 × 12. Lie on back, feet flat on floor, dumbbell on hips. Drive hips upward squeezing glutes hard at top, hold 1 second, lower slowly. This directly trains the hip extension power that generates swing speed.
  • C
    Dead Bug: 3 × 8 each side. Lie on back, arms pointing to ceiling, knees bent at 90°. Slowly lower opposite arm and leg toward the floor without letting the lower back leave the ground. Return and repeat. Builds the deep core stability that maintains posture through the swing.
  • D
    Pallof Press: 3 × 10 each side. Standing perpendicular to a band anchor (or cable), press both hands away from chest directly in front — resist the rotational pull. The anti-rotation demand directly trains the core stabilisation required for consistent low point.
Friday — TRX Rotation + Footwork

Golf-Specific Movement Patterns

  • A
    TRX Golf Rotation: 3 × 8 each direction. Face the TRX anchor, extend arms forward. Rotate your torso (not just arms) against the resistance to one side, then the other. Directly trains the rotational power and deceleration control used in the golf swing.
  • B
    Lateral Shuffle (Footwork): 3 × 20 seconds. Quick lateral steps maintaining athletic posture. Builds the lower body coordination and fast-twitch muscle activation that contributes to downswing speed and balance.
  • C
    Single-Leg Romanian Deadlift (bodyweight): 3 × 8 each leg. Stand on one leg, hinge forward at the hip with the free leg extending behind you. Builds single-leg stability critical for the impact position and balance through the swing.

Key Phase 2 Exercises

Increased Intensity

  • A
    TRX Single-Leg Squat (Pistol Prep): 3 × 8 reps each leg. Use TRX for assistance — the goal is depth and control, not unassisted. This builds the single-leg stability that eliminates lateral sway in the swing.
  • B
    Dumbbell Romanian Deadlift (heavier): 3 × 8 reps. Increase to 14–20 kg. Add 2 kg every 2 weeks if 8 reps feels controlled. Track the weight every session.
  • C
    Weighted Hip Thrust: 3 × 10 reps. Add heavier dumbbell (12–20 kg). Drive hips explosively on the way up — power not just strength. The glutes are the primary power generator in the golf swing.
  • D
    Rotational Dumbbell Chop (Wood Chop): 3 × 10 each direction. Hold one dumbbell with both hands, rotate from high to low (or low to high) in a diagonal chopping pattern. Directly trains the diagonal rotational pattern of the golf downswing.
  • E
    Punch Bag Rotational Strikes: 3 × 30 seconds per side. Drive rotational strikes into the bag using full body rotation — hips first, then torso, then arms. Trains explosive rotational power under load. Disguised as boxing, this is one of the best golf fitness exercises available.

Explosive Power Development

Supplemental Exercise Library

Additional golf-specific movements to rotate into your programme from Phase 2 onwards. Add 1–2 of these per week to address weak areas and keep training varied.

📚 Exercise Reference
Core & Stability

Low Point Control & Consistency

  • ▸
    Dumbbell Goblet Squat with Rotation: 3 × 10. Hold dumbbell at chest, squat to parallel, at bottom rotate torso right as far as possible — elbow pointing up — return to centre, then rotate left. Combines hip mobility and thoracic rotation under load.
  • ▸
    Copenhagen Side Plank: 3 × 20 sec each side. Lie on your side, rest upper shin on a bench or chair, lift hips off the floor into a straight line. Builds lateral hip stability that prevents sliding in the backswing.
  • ▸
    Bird Dog: 3 × 8 each side. On hands and knees, extend opposite arm and leg simultaneously, hold 2 seconds, return. Builds the anti-rotation core stability required for maintaining spine angle through the swing.
Rotational Power

Speed Generators

  • ▸
    Medicine Ball Rotational Throw (wall or partner): 3 × 8 each side. Stand side-on to a wall, rotate and throw the ball into the wall using full body rotation — hips first. The most direct simulation of the golf downswing pattern available.
  • ▸
    Cable Rotation (golf plane): 3 × 10 each direction. Set a cable at hip height. Stand perpendicular to the stack, pull the cable across your body using hip and torso rotation — not just arms. Mimics the exact downswing force production pattern.
  • ▸
    Landmine Press + Rotation: 3 × 8. Hold the end of a barbell in one hand, press it in a diagonal arc from hip to shoulder while rotating through the hips. This trains the coordinated power transfer from lower body to upper body that creates clubhead speed.
Flexibility & Recovery

Longevity Movements

  • ▸
    Thread the Needle: On hands and knees, slide one arm under your body along the floor, rotating the thoracic spine — "threading" your arm through the gap. This is one of the most effective thoracic mobility exercises for golfers.
  • ▸
    Figure-4 Glute Stretch: Lie on back, cross one ankle over the opposite knee, pull the bent knee toward your chest until you feel the glute stretch. Hold 30 seconds. Maintains hip mobility as strength training increases hip muscle tension.
  • ▸
    Cat-Cow Spinal Mobility: On hands and knees, alternate between arching the back up (cat) and dropping it toward the floor (cow) × 10 slow repetitions. Maintains lumbar spine mobility and counteracts the compressive effects of the golf swing on the lower back.

Pre-Round Activation

15–20 minutes as soon as you arrive at the course. Purpose: wake up your movement system — not train it. Research shows a proper warm-up adds 1.5–2 mph swing speed in the first 3 holes and prevents cold-start errors.

⏱ 20 Minutes — Never Skip
T-minus 20 Minutes — Arrival

Joint Lubrication Walk

  • 1
    Walk briskly for 2 minutes — from car to clubhouse and back. Swing your arms freely. This initiates circulation and begins joint lubrication before any specific movements.
  • 2
    5 ankle circles each direction. 10 leg swings front-to-back and side-to-side (hold onto a cart or fence). These prime the lower body kinetic chain from the ground up.
T-minus 18 Minutes — Dynamic Mobility

Golf-Specific Activation

  • 1
    Thoracic rotation × 10 each way: Seated on bag trolley, hands on shoulders. This directly primes the shoulder turn you'll use on every full swing.
  • 2
    Hip 90/90 × 20 sec each side: Sit on the ground (or approximate standing version with a club). Unlocks the hip mobility needed for a proper downswing sequence.
  • 3
    Standing hip circles × 10 each direction: Hands on hips, feet shoulder-width. Large slow circles. Warms the hip capsule which is typically compressed after a car journey to the course.
  • 4
    Wrist circles × 10 each direction: Both wrists simultaneously. Lead wrist specifically — restricted wrist mobility creates compensatory impact patterns that show up immediately in the first few holes.
T-minus 12 Minutes — Swing Activation

Neural Priming

  • 1
    Two-club slow swing × 10: Cross two clubs behind your head, rotate slowly through the full backswing and follow-through. The resistance warms golf-specific muscles without fatiguing them. Primes the kinematic sequence.
  • 2
    One-arm shadow swing × 5 each arm: Trail arm only, then lead arm only — slow practice swings through full range. Identifies any restriction or tightness before you're on the range.
  • 3
    Single-leg balance × 20 sec (lead leg): Stand on your lead foot. This activates the stabilising muscles of the lead leg — critical for consistent impact position and balance through the finish.
⭐

Tour standard: Tour players arrive 45–60 minutes before their tee time. 15–20 minutes of this is physical preparation. Never arrive with less than 30 minutes to tee time. The physical arousal from rushing takes 8–12 minutes to subside — long enough to ruin your first 2 holes.

Post-Round Recovery

What you do in the 20 minutes after your round determines how your body feels for the next 48 hours — and how quickly you can play again at full capability. Most amateur golfers skip this entirely. That is a mistake.

🔄 20 Minutes — Builds Longevity
Why This Matters

The Post-Round State of Your Body

After 4–5 hours of walking, rotating, and repetitive loading: your hip flexors are shortened, your thoracic spine is compressed, your glutes have switched off, and your nervous system is fatigued. Without active recovery, these imbalances accumulate round after round — leading to the stiffness, back pain, and deteriorating swing mechanics that end amateur golf careers early.

Minutes 1–5 — Immediately After

Walk & Breathe — Don't Sit Down Yet

  • 1
    Walk at a gentle pace for 2 minutes — around the 18th green or back toward the clubhouse. Swing your arms freely. Do not immediately sit down — your blood is pooled in your legs and your spine is compressed.
  • 2
    Take 5 long, slow deep breaths: 4 seconds in through the nose, hold 2 seconds, 6 seconds out through the mouth. This activates your parasympathetic nervous system, beginning the transition from performance to recovery mode.
Minutes 5–12 — Targeted Stretching

Address What the Swing Compresses

  • 1
    Hip flexor stretch × 45 sec each side: Kneeling lunge. The hip flexors are dramatically shortened after 18 holes and will remain so unless stretched immediately. This also decompresses the lumbar spine.
  • 2
    Figure-4 glute stretch × 30 sec each side: Lie on back. The glutes switch off after hours of walking — this stretch maintains their length and prepares them to re-engage for the next session.
  • 3
    Thoracic rotation × 10 each way: Seated, hands on shoulders. The thoracic spine absorbs rotational forces over 70+ swings — gentle rotation decompresses it and maintains mobility for your next round.
  • 4
    Cat-cow × 10 slow reps: On hands and knees if possible, or substitute with standing hip circles. Restores lumbar spine mobility after the repetitive rotational loading of a full round.
Minutes 12–20 — Recovery Nutrition

The Recovery Window

Within 60 minutes of finishing your round, consume a recovery meal or shake: 40–50g protein (chicken, salmon, Greek yogurt, or a whey shake), 200g sweet potato or 150g wholegrain rice (replenish glycogen), and 500ml water with electrolytes if you walked the course. This window is when muscle protein synthesis is highest — missing it significantly slows recovery for the next 24 hours.

The Day After — Active Recovery

Keeping the System Moving

  • ▸
    Daily mobility routine (6 min): Same as every day. Particularly important the day after a round when stiffness is most likely.
  • ▸
    20-minute gentle walk: Maintains circulation and prevents the "post-round stiffness day" that most amateur golfers experience. Brisk enough to feel like exercise, not so hard as to add load.
  • ▸
    No strength training the day after a round: The swing is a high-force, high-repetition activity. Your musculoskeletal system needs one full day before being loaded again. Respect this or accumulate the injuries that cut handicap progress short.

Ground Reaction Force Training

Ground Reaction Force (GRF) is the largest single source of clubhead speed (30–35%) — and the most undertrained physical variable in amateur golf. The Speed Training playbook identifies GRF as the #1 speed source; this section provides the dedicated technical and physical protocol to develop it.

⚡ GRF — The Physics of Golf Speed
What Ground Reaction Force Is — and Why It Dominates Speed

Newton's Third Law Applied to the Golf Swing

When you push into the ground with your feet, the ground pushes back with equal and opposite force. This ground reaction force travels up through the legs, hips, and core — initiating the kinematic chain that produces clubhead speed. The more force you produce into the ground, and the better your timing of that force, the faster the chain moves.

🔄 The Squat-Jump Sequence — Elite Transition Mechanics
The Squat into Jump — The Most Powerful Transition Move

How Tour Players Load and Explode

High-speed force plate data from tour players consistently shows a vertical downward force spike in early transition (the "squat"), immediately followed by a powerful upward force production (the "jump"). This squat-jump sequence is absent in most amateur swings — replaced by a level rotation that produces significantly less GRF and therefore less speed.

  • 1
    The squat (transition): As the backswing completes, the hips drop slightly downward before initiating rotation. This is a subtle movement — 1–3 inches of downward motion — not a visible knee bend. The sensation is "sitting into the ground" at the top before driving through. Force plate data shows this squat creates 1.2–1.5× bodyweight of loading force before any rotation begins.
  • 2
    The jump (downswing): From the loaded squat position, the legs drive upward and toward the target simultaneously. The sensation is "pushing the ground away" — not "rotating the hips." This vertical drive is what creates the upward GRF peak (1.8–2.2× bodyweight at tour level) that initiates the kinematic chain. The rotation is a consequence of this push, not the primary input.
  • 3
    Lead foot drives down through impact: At and through impact, the lead foot presses firmly into the ground. The lead heel rises only after impact — rising prematurely before impact signals a loss of GRF. Feel the lead heel "pressing into the ground" through the hitting zone.
  • 4
    Pressure transfer sequence — feel it: Trail foot heavy at address → trail foot heavier at top of backswing → lead foot heavier at impact → full weight on lead side at finish. The transfer should feel progressive, not sudden. A sudden lunge (early extension) short-circuits the GRF sequence and produces the "stall" pattern — hips stop rotating and the hands flip through.
🏋️ GRF Training Exercises
Phase 1 — GRF Awareness Drills (Weeks 1–4)

Building the Neural Pattern Before Adding Load

  • 1
    Trail Heel Rise Drill: Take your address position without a club. Make a backswing to the top, then drive your lead foot into the ground while allowing the trail heel to rise naturally. Hold the impact position for 2 seconds. Focus entirely on the lead foot pressure. 3 sets × 15 reps, 3× per week. This isolates the pressure transfer without any speed or rotation complexity.
  • 2
    Jump and Land Drill: Stand in golf address posture. Jump vertically (both feet leave the ground simultaneously) and land in impact posture — weight on lead side, hips slightly open. Feel the ground reaction force on landing. This develops the neuromuscular awareness of what GRF feels like as a sensation. 3 sets × 10 reps.
  • 3
    Step Drill (Dynamic): Start with feet together. As you initiate the downswing, step your lead foot into its normal position and drive it into the ground. This exaggerates the lateral pressure shift and forces a correct GRF sequence. Used extensively by TPI coaches to teach pressure transfer. Hit 20 shots with the step drill before 20 normal shots to transfer the feeling.
Phase 2 — GRF Strength Development (Weeks 5–12)

Building the Physical Capacity to Generate Force

  • 1
    Single-Leg Squat and Press (Lateral): Stand on your lead leg. Squat to 90° knee flexion, then drive upward and slightly outward (toward target direction). 4 sets × 8 reps per leg. This trains the hip abductors and glutes to produce horizontal GRF — exactly the force component that drives hip rotation through impact. Progress from bodyweight to dumbbell held at chest.
  • 2
    Hex Bar Deadlift — Vertical Force Production: The hex bar deadlift is the most sport-specific GRF exercise for golf. It trains the exact muscles (glutes, hamstrings, lower back) responsible for producing vertical ground force. 3 sets × 5 reps at 80% 1RM. Start with Romanian Deadlift in Phase 1 if hex bar is unavailable.
  • 3
    Lateral Bounds (Plyometric): Stand on one leg, bound laterally to the other leg, landing with control. Focuses on horizontal force production and the landing stability that mirrors the lead side at impact. 3 sets × 8 bounds per direction. Progress from short bounds to maximum distance bounds as strength and control improve.
  • 4
    Med Ball Squat-to-Slam: Hold a medicine ball (4–6 kg). Squat into a loaded position, then drive upward explosively, slamming the ball downward at full arm extension. The squat-to-drive pattern mirrors the GRF sequence in the golf transition. 3 sets × 10 reps. This builds power in the exact movement direction required.
Phase 3 — GRF Power Integration (Weeks 13–24)

Transferring GRF Strength to Clubhead Speed

  • 1
    Jump Swing with SuperSpeed: Use the lightest SuperSpeed stick. Begin each swing with a deliberate "squat" at the top, then drive upward as you initiate the downswing. The lighter implement allows you to feel the GRF-initiated speed without technical interference. 3 sets × 10 swings. Compare Mevo speed readings vs. normal SuperSpeed sessions to quantify GRF contribution.
  • 2
    Box Jump to Address Position: Stand facing a plyo box (30–40 cm height). Jump to the top of the box, land in golf address posture, and immediately drive back down into a simulated impact posture. The landing and immediate re-drive trains the stretch-shortening cycle that mirrors the squat-jump transition sequence. 4 sets × 6 reps.
  • 3
    Mevo Speed Tracking — GRF Sessions vs. Standard Sessions: Track driver speed separately on sessions where you deliberately focus on the squat-jump sequence vs. standard sessions. Most players see 2–5 mph additional speed when GRF is consciously sequenced. This quantifies the contribution and motivates continued focus on the movement.

HRV & Physical Readiness

Heart Rate Variability (HRV) is the most validated, non-invasive marker of nervous system recovery available. Training hard on a low-HRV day produces injury risk and poor neural adaptation. This section provides the full protocol for integrating HRV into your training and competition schedule.

📊 What HRV Actually Measures
HRV — The Science

Why Beat-to-Beat Variation Predicts Training Readiness

Heart rate variability measures the millisecond differences between consecutive heartbeats. Counter-intuitively, higher variability is better — it indicates the autonomic nervous system is responsive and adaptive. Low HRV indicates the sympathetic nervous system is dominant (stress, fatigue, inflammation) and the body is prioritising recovery over performance adaptation.

💡

Critical interaction — overspeed training: Speed training (SuperSpeed, Rypstick) is a neurological training stimulus. It requires a recovered nervous system to produce adaptation. Running a full speed protocol on a low-HRV day produces fatigue without speed gain — and increases injury risk. High-HRV days are when speed work should be scheduled. This single adjustment can accelerate speed gains by 20–30%.

📱 Measurement Protocol
How to Measure HRV — Tools and Timing

Accurate Measurement Without Expensive Equipment

  • 1
    When to measure: Immediately upon waking — before getting out of bed, before coffee, before checking your phone. HRV is most stable and reproducible at this moment. Any activity, caffeine, or emotional stimulation within 30 minutes before measurement reduces accuracy significantly.
  • 2
    Measurement duration: A minimum of 60 seconds is required for a reliable reading. Most apps use a 60–120 second measurement. Shorter readings are unreliable; longer readings add precision but are rarely necessary.
  • 3
    Body position — always consistent: Measure in the same body position every day. Lying down produces the highest and most stable HRV readings. Sitting produces slightly lower readings. Standing produces the lowest. Changing position between days makes your baseline comparison unreliable.

📈 Building and Reading Your Baseline
Establishing Your Personal Baseline — Weeks 1–4

Why Individual Baselines Matter More Than Population Norms

HRV is highly individual. A reading of 65 ms rMSSD may indicate peak readiness for one person and significant fatigue for another. What matters is your personal baseline — the average of your HRV over 4–6 weeks of consistent measurement in a stable training period. All training decisions are made relative to your baseline, not population averages.

  • 1
    Weeks 1–4 — baseline only: Measure every morning but make no training adjustments based on HRV. Simply observe. Note patterns: does HRV drop after poor sleep? After alcohol? After consecutive hard training days? After competition? These patterns become your personal readiness dictionary.
  • 2
    Week 5 onwards — apply the data: Begin adjusting training intensity based on HRV relative to your 7-day rolling average (most apps calculate this automatically). High reading → push. Normal → standard. Low → back off.
  • 3
    Track alongside training load and subjective feel: Note your Perceived Exertion (RPE) after each session alongside your HRV reading. Over time you will see patterns: sessions at high RPE consistently drop HRV the next morning; poor sleep drops it more than hard training. These insights let you make proactive recovery decisions.

😴 Sleep — The Primary HRV Driver
Sleep Quality — The Most Impactful Recovery Variable

Targets and Optimisation Protocol

Sleep is the single most powerful recovery intervention available. A single night of poor sleep reduces HRV by an average of 15–25%, which cascades into reduced motor learning, reduced speed adaptation, and impaired decision-making on course. Optimising sleep is the highest-ROI recovery investment in the programme.

Sleep MetricTarget (Athlete in Training)Effect on Performance

  • 1
    Pre-sleep protocol (60 min before bed): No screens or dim blue-light filter. Room temperature 17–19°C. No alcohol (alcohol reduces REM sleep by 20–25% even if it aids sleep onset — the sleep quality is significantly worse). Consistent wind-down routine signals the brain to begin melatonin production.
  • 2
    Morning protocol for HRV accuracy: Measure HRV before coffee or any stimulation. Note total hours slept alongside the HRV reading. Within 2–3 weeks you will see a direct correlation between sleep duration/quality and next-morning HRV — the most motivating data for sleep behaviour change.
  • 3
    Pre-competition sleep: The night before a competition matters less than the two nights before. Competition anxiety often disrupts final-night sleep — pre-plan for this by banking extra sleep the two previous nights. A night of poor sleep before competition does not significantly impair performance if the preceding nights were well-rested.

The compound effect: HRV-guided training is not about doing less — it is about doing the right intensity at the right time. Players who train at maximum intensity every day regardless of HRV plateau earlier and accumulate more injuries. Players who use HRV data to push hard on recovery days and back off on low-HRV days progress faster over 6–12 months. The evidence is consistent across sports science — readiness-guided training outperforms fixed-schedule training for long-term athletic development.
