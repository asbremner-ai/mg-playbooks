Daily Mobility Routine

12 minutes. Every single day. Expanded following your TPI screen to address all red and amber findings. Do it before training, before a round, or first thing in the morning. This is now your single highest-leverage daily investment.

🔴 TPI Screen — Updated Routine · 12 Minutes
⚠️

TPI Screen Results — Aug 2025: Your screen flagged 4 RED items (Setup Posture, Single Leg Balance, Seated Trunk Rotation, Wrist Flexion/Extension) and 6 AMBER items (Pelvic Tilt, Torso Rotation, Overhead Deep Squat, 90/90 Hip, Lat Test, Bridge with Leg Extension). All corrective work has been integrated into this routine and your strength sessions below.

🔴 RED — Movement 1 · Setup Posture

Cat-Cow + Wall Posture Drill

  • ▸
    Cat-Cow × 15 reps (slow): On hands and knees, alternate arching the back upward (cat) and dropping it toward the floor (cow). Move through the full range deliberately — this builds lumbar/thoracic body awareness that directly improves address posture.
  • ▸
    Wall Posture Drill × 60 sec: Stand with heels, glutes, shoulders, and head all touching a wall. Hold this position and memorise the feeling. This is your correct address posture. Replicate it every time you step over the ball.
  • ▸
    Duration: ~2 minutes total.
  • ▸
    Swing fault addressed: C-posture or S-posture at address; loss of posture through the swing.
🔴 RED — Movement 2 · Seated Trunk / Torso Rotation

Thoracic Rotation with Overpressure + Thread-the-Needle

  • ▸
    Seated thoracic rotation with overpressure × 15 each side: Sit tall with arms crossed on chest. Rotate maximally and hold the end range for 2 full seconds before returning. Do not allow the hips to rotate — isolate the thoracic spine.
  • ▸
    Thread-the-needle × 10 each side: On hands and knees, slide one arm under your body along the floor, rotating the thoracic spine as far as possible. Return and repeat. One of the most effective thoracic mobility drills for golfers.
  • ▸
    Duration: ~3 minutes total. This is your most critical red finding — prioritise quality.
  • ▸
    Swing fault addressed: Restricted shoulder turn, over-the-top path, loss of width in backswing, reduced clubhead speed.
🟡 AMBER — Movement 3 · 90/90 Hip Rotation

Hip 90/90 Stretch — Extended Hold

  • ▸
    How: Sit on the floor with both legs bent at 90° (front shin perpendicular to body, rear shin parallel). Lean slowly forward over the front leg with a straight spine.
  • ▸
    Reps: Hold 60 seconds each side (increased from 30 sec based on amber result). 2 minutes total.
  • ▸
    Swing fault addressed: Hip internal rotation restriction causes reduced pelvic rotation, loss of power, early extension in downswing.
Movement 4 — Hip Flexor + Pelvic Tilt Awareness

Restore Address Position & Pelvic Control

  • ▸
    Kneeling hip flexor stretch × 45 sec each side: Lunge position — one knee on floor, other foot forward. Tuck pelvis under and press hips forward until you feel a stretch in the front of the trailing hip. Keep torso upright.
  • ▸
    Pelvic tilt drill × 15 reps: Lying on back, feet flat. Alternate deliberately between anterior tilt (arch the lower back) and posterior tilt (press lower back into floor). Build conscious awareness of pelvic position — essential for setup posture and downswing sequencing.
  • ▸
    Duration: ~2.5 minutes total.
  • ▸
    Swing faults addressed: Pelvic tilt amber — inability to separate upper/lower body; early extension; loss of posture.
🔴 RED — Movement 5 · Wrist Flexion/Extension

Wrist Mobility + Controlled Articular Rotations

  • ▸
    Wrist flexion stretch × 30 sec each hand: Arm extended, palm facing up. Use the opposite hand to gently pull fingers back toward you. Hold the end range — do not pulse.
  • ▸
    Wrist extension stretch × 30 sec each hand: Arm extended, palm facing down. Use the opposite hand to gently pull fingers back. Hold.
  • ▸
    Wrist CARs × 5 slow circles each direction, each hand: Controlled articular rotations — move the wrist through its absolute maximum range in a slow, deliberate circle. This is distinct from a casual wrist roll; move at the speed of 3 seconds per revolution.
  • ▸
    Duration: ~2 minutes total.
  • ▸
    Swing fault addressed: Loss of wrist hinge, flipping through impact, inconsistent face angle. The lead wrist red flag directly correlates with inconsistent ball-striking.
🟡 AMBER — Movement 6 · Lat Test + Overhead Deep Squat

Lat + Ankle Mobility

  • ▸
    Lat doorframe stretch × 30 sec each side: Stand in a doorframe, grip the frame at shoulder height with one hand. Let your body drop away, feeling a stretch down the side of the back and into the lat. Restricted lats pull the lead arm down early in the backswing.
  • ▸
    Child's pose with lateral reach × 30 sec each side: From child's pose, walk both hands to one side and hold. Full lat and thoracic stretch simultaneously.
  • ▸
    Ankle dorsiflexion stretch × 30 sec each side: Place foot on a step, lean your knee forward over the toes. This is often the primary limiter in the Overhead Deep Squat test.
  • ▸
    Duration: ~2 minutes total.
🟡 AMBER — Movement 7 · Single Leg Balance

Stability Activation — Close Every Routine

  • ▸
    Single-leg stance × 45 sec each side, eyes open: Stand on one leg with a soft knee. Focus on a fixed point. When stable, progress to eyes closed (much harder — this is the meaningful challenge).
  • ▸
    Eyes closed progression: Target 30 seconds each side without touching down. This is the standard your swing requires at impact.
  • ▸
    Duration: ~1.5 minutes total.
  • ▸
    Swing fault addressed: Sway, slide, loss of balance at finish, early extension.
💡

Stack it with your morning: Do this routine immediately upon waking — before coffee, before your phone. It takes exactly 12 minutes and directly addresses your TPI findings. Reassess with your TPI practitioner at the 8-week mark — expect all amber items to improve and red items to progress to amber.

🔴

TPI Corrective Warm-Up — All 3 Sessions (5 min): Begin every strength session with: Single-leg balance × 45 sec each side (eyes closed target) → Goblet squat with pause at bottom × 10 reps (3-sec hold, addresses Overhead Deep Squat amber) → Pelvic tilt drill × 15 reps (lying on back, alternate anterior/posterior tilt). Only then proceed to the main session.

Monday — TRX Foundation Session

Building the Base

  • A
    TRX Squat: 3 × 12 reps. Feet shoulder-width, hold TRX for balance and depth assistance. Focus on controlled descent and driving through heels. Rest 60 seconds.
  • B
    TRX Row: 3 × 10 reps. Feet forward, body at 45° incline. Pull chest to handles, squeeze shoulder blades at top. Builds the pulling strength that counteracts the forward-hunched posture common in golfers.
  • C
    TRX Chest Press: 3 × 10 reps. Face away from anchor, press handles away from chest. Activates the pressing pattern that contributes to lead arm strength through impact.
  • D
    TRX Hip Hinge (Romanian Deadlift): 3 × 10 reps. Push hips back while maintaining a neutral spine, lower until mild hamstring tension, drive hips forward to stand. The most important movement pattern in golf fitness.
Wednesday — Dumbbell Hip Hinge + Core

Build the Posterior Chain

  • A
    Dumbbell Romanian Deadlift: 3 × 10. Hold a dumbbell in each hand in front of thighs. Hinge at hips pushing them back, lower weights down legs, return to standing. Start with 8–10 kg and increase as strength improves.
  • B
    Bridge Progression — TPI Corrective (Bridge with Leg Extension amber): Week 1–2: Standard glute bridge 3 × 12. Week 3–4: Single-leg bridge 3 × 10 each leg. Week 5–8: Bridge with leg extension 3 × 10 each side (2 sec up, 1 sec hold, 2 sec down). Do not skip stages. Glute weakness directly causes early extension — your most common 10-HCP fault.
  • C
    Dead Bug: 3 × 8 each side. Lie on back, arms pointing to ceiling, knees bent at 90°. Slowly lower opposite arm and leg toward the floor without letting the lower back leave the ground. Return and repeat. Builds the deep core stability that maintains posture through the swing.
  • D
    Pallof Press: 3 × 10 each side. Standing perpendicular to a band anchor (or cable), press both hands away from chest directly in front — resist the rotational pull. The anti-rotation demand directly trains the core stabilisation required for consistent low point.
  • E
    Half-Kneeling Band Rotation — TPI Corrective (Torso Rotation amber): 3 × 10 each side. In a half-kneeling position, hold a resistance band anchored at hip height. Rotate away from the anchor using the torso — not the arms. Trains rotational control in a golf-posture position. Add from Week 3.
Friday — TRX Rotation + Footwork

Golf-Specific Movement Patterns

  • A
    TRX Golf Rotation: 3 × 8 each direction. Face the TRX anchor, extend arms forward. Rotate your torso (not just arms) against the resistance to one side, then the other. Directly trains the rotational power and deceleration control used in the golf swing.
  • B
    Lateral Shuffle (Footwork): 3 × 20 seconds. Quick lateral steps maintaining athletic posture. Builds the lower body coordination and fast-twitch muscle activation that contributes to downswing speed and balance.
  • C
    Single-Leg Romanian Deadlift (bodyweight) — TPI Priority (Single Leg Balance red): 3 × 8 each leg. Stand on one leg, hinge forward at the hip with the free leg extending behind you. This is the primary loaded corrective for your single-leg balance failure. Progress to a light dumbbell (4–6 kg) from Week 4 when form is solid.
  • D
    Goblet Squat with Pause — TPI Corrective (Overhead Deep Squat amber): 3 × 10. Hold a dumbbell at chest, squat slowly to parallel or below, hold 3 seconds at the bottom. This simultaneously mobilises hips, ankles, and trains the deep squat pattern that your ODS test flagged. Use 8–12 kg.

Key Phase 2 Exercises

Increased Intensity

  • A
    TRX Single-Leg Squat (Pistol Prep): 3 × 8 reps each leg. Use TRX for assistance — the goal is depth and control, not unassisted. This builds the single-leg stability that eliminates lateral sway in the swing.
  • B
    Dumbbell Romanian Deadlift (heavier): 3 × 8 reps. Increase to 14–20 kg. Add 2 kg every 2 weeks if 8 reps feels controlled. Track the weight every session.
  • C
    Weighted Hip Thrust: 3 × 10 reps. Add heavier dumbbell (12–20 kg). Drive hips explosively on the way up — power not just strength. The glutes are the primary power generator in the golf swing.
  • D
    Rotational Dumbbell Chop (Wood Chop): 3 × 10 each direction. Hold one dumbbell with both hands, rotate from high to low (or low to high) in a diagonal chopping pattern. Directly trains the diagonal rotational pattern of the golf downswing.
  • E
    Punch Bag Rotational Strikes: 3 × 30 seconds per side. Drive rotational strikes into the bag using full body rotation — hips first, then torso, then arms. Trains explosive rotational power under load. Disguised as boxing, this is one of the best golf fitness exercises available.
💡

Begin SuperSpeed in Phase 2: Add overspeed training to 2 non-training days per week. Light stick × 10, medium stick × 10, driver × 10 at maximum effort — all one direction, then repeat mirrored. Takes 10 minutes. Speed gains typically begin appearing in weeks 3–4.

Phase 3 Key Exercises

Explosive Power Development

  • A
    Dumbbell Jump Squat: 4 × 6 reps with light dumbbells (4–8 kg). Squat to parallel then explode off the ground — land softly and absorb. Builds fast-twitch muscle and neurological speed that transfers directly to clubhead speed.
  • B
    Punch Bag HIIT (Rotational Strikes): 4 rounds × 30 seconds maximum effort / 30 seconds rest. Full rotational body strikes from both sides. This is the highest-intensity golf-specific conditioning available outside of actual speed training.
  • C
    Single-Leg Hip Thrust (explosive): 3 × 8 each leg. Drive through one heel only, pushing hips up explosively. Builds the unilateral glute power that drives the downswing from the lead foot.
  • D
    Golf Conditioning Circuit (4 exercises, 3 rounds): Lateral shuffle 20 sec → TRX rotation 10 each side → Jump squat × 5 → Dead bug × 8 each side. No rest between exercises; 90 seconds between rounds. Builds golf-specific endurance for back-nine performance.
⭐

The back-nine dividend: Tour research shows amateur scores rise 0.4 strokes per hole on holes 14–18 from fatigue. Completing Phase 3 builds the physical and cardiovascular base that eliminates this pattern. Your opponents fade; you don't.

Supplemental Exercise Library

Additional golf-specific movements to rotate into your programme from Phase 2 onwards. Add 1–2 of these per week to address weak areas and keep training varied.

📚 Exercise Reference
Core & Stability

Low Point Control & Consistency

  • ▸
    Dumbbell Goblet Squat with Rotation: 3 × 10. Hold dumbbell at chest, squat to parallel, at bottom rotate torso right as far as possible — elbow pointing up — return to centre, then rotate left. Combines hip mobility and thoracic rotation under load.
  • ▸
    Copenhagen Side Plank: 3 × 20 sec each side. Lie on your side, rest upper shin on a bench or chair, lift hips off the floor into a straight line. Builds lateral hip stability that prevents sliding in the backswing.
  • ▸
    Bird Dog: 3 × 8 each side. On hands and knees, extend opposite arm and leg simultaneously, hold 2 seconds, return. Builds the anti-rotation core stability required for maintaining spine angle through the swing.
Rotational Power

Speed Generators

  • ▸
    Medicine Ball Rotational Throw (wall or partner): 3 × 8 each side. Stand side-on to a wall, rotate and throw the ball into the wall using full body rotation — hips first. The most direct simulation of the golf downswing pattern available.
  • ▸
    Cable Rotation (golf plane): 3 × 10 each direction. Set a cable at hip height. Stand perpendicular to the stack, pull the cable across your body using hip and torso rotation — not just arms. Mimics the exact downswing force production pattern.
  • ▸
    Landmine Press + Rotation: 3 × 8. Hold the end of a barbell in one hand, press it in a diagonal arc from hip to shoulder while rotating through the hips. This trains the coordinated power transfer from lower body to upper body that creates clubhead speed.
Flexibility & Recovery

Longevity Movements

  • ▸
    Thread the Needle: On hands and knees, slide one arm under your body along the floor, rotating the thoracic spine — "threading" your arm through the gap. This is one of the most effective thoracic mobility exercises for golfers.
  • ▸
    Figure-4 Glute Stretch: Lie on back, cross one ankle over the opposite knee, pull the bent knee toward your chest until you feel the glute stretch. Hold 30 seconds. Maintains hip mobility as strength training increases hip muscle tension.
  • ▸
    Cat-Cow Spinal Mobility: On hands and knees, alternate between arching the back up (cat) and dropping it toward the floor (cow) × 10 slow repetitions. Maintains lumbar spine mobility and counteracts the compressive effects of the golf swing on the lower back.

Pre-Round Activation

15–20 minutes as soon as you arrive at the course. Purpose: wake up your movement system — not train it. Research shows a proper warm-up adds 1.5–2 mph swing speed in the first 3 holes and prevents cold-start errors.

⏱ 20 Minutes — Never Skip
T-minus 20 Minutes — Arrival

Joint Lubrication Walk

  • 1
    Walk briskly for 2 minutes — from car to clubhouse and back. Swing your arms freely. This initiates circulation and begins joint lubrication before any specific movements.
  • 2
    5 ankle circles each direction. 10 leg swings front-to-back and side-to-side (hold onto a cart or fence). These prime the lower body kinetic chain from the ground up.
T-minus 18 Minutes — Dynamic Mobility

Golf-Specific Activation (TPI Updated)

  • 1
    Thoracic rotation × 10 each way: Seated on bag trolley, hands on shoulders. This directly primes the shoulder turn you'll use on every full swing.
  • 2
    Hip 90/90 × 45 sec each side: Sit on the ground (or approximate standing version with a club). Extended hold given your amber hip result. Unlocks the hip mobility needed for a proper downswing sequence.
  • 3
    Standing hip circles × 10 each direction: Hands on hips, feet shoulder-width. Large slow circles. Warms the hip capsule which is typically compressed after a car journey to the course.
  • 4
    Wrist flexion/extension + CARs × 5 circles each hand (TPI Red): Wrist flexion stretch 15 sec each hand → extension stretch 15 sec each hand → slow CARs × 5 each direction. Your wrist red flag means this is non-negotiable before every round — restricted wrists affect face angle from the very first hole.
  • 5
    Thread-the-needle × 5 each side: A quick thoracic rotation reinforcer on the ground. 60 seconds total. Reinforces your daily mobility work before competitive demand.
T-minus 12 Minutes — Swing Activation

Neural Priming

  • 1
    Two-club slow swing × 10: Cross two clubs behind your head, rotate slowly through the full backswing and follow-through. The resistance warms golf-specific muscles without fatiguing them. Primes the kinematic sequence.
  • 2
    One-arm shadow swing × 5 each arm: Trail arm only, then lead arm only — slow practice swings through full range. Identifies any restriction or tightness before you're on the range.
  • 3
    Single-leg balance × 20 sec (lead leg): Stand on your lead foot. This activates the stabilising muscles of the lead leg — critical for consistent impact position and balance through the finish.
⭐

Tour standard: Tour players arrive 45–60 minutes before their tee time. 15–20 minutes of this is physical preparation. Never arrive with less than 30 minutes to tee time. The physical arousal from rushing takes 8–12 minutes to subside — long enough to ruin your first 2 holes.

Post-Round Recovery

What you do in the 20 minutes after your round determines how your body feels for the next 48 hours — and how quickly you can play again at full capability. Most amateur golfers skip this entirely. That is a mistake.

🔄 20 Minutes — Builds Longevity
Why This Matters

The Post-Round State of Your Body

After 4–5 hours of walking, rotating, and repetitive loading: your hip flexors are shortened, your thoracic spine is compressed, your glutes have switched off, and your nervous system is fatigued. Without active recovery, these imbalances accumulate round after round — leading to the stiffness, back pain, and deteriorating swing mechanics that end amateur golf careers early.

Minutes 1–5 — Immediately After

Walk & Breathe — Don't Sit Down Yet

  • 1
    Walk at a gentle pace for 2 minutes — around the 18th green or back toward the clubhouse. Swing your arms freely. Do not immediately sit down — your blood is pooled in your legs and your spine is compressed.
  • 2
    Take 5 long, slow deep breaths: 4 seconds in through the nose, hold 2 seconds, 6 seconds out through the mouth. This activates your parasympathetic nervous system, beginning the transition from performance to recovery mode.
Minutes 5–12 — Targeted Stretching (TPI Updated)

Address What the Swing Compresses

  • 1
    Hip flexor stretch × 45 sec each side: Kneeling lunge. The hip flexors are dramatically shortened after 18 holes and will remain so unless stretched immediately. This also decompresses the lumbar spine.
  • 2
    Figure-4 glute stretch × 45 sec each side (extended — Bridge with Leg Extension amber): Lie on back, cross ankle over opposite knee. The glutes switch off after hours of walking — this stretch maintains their length and prepares them to re-engage. Extended hold given your glute weakness finding.
  • 3
    Thread-the-needle × 5 each side (Seated Trunk Rotation red): On hands and knees post-round thoracic decompression. The thoracic spine absorbs 70+ rotational loads per round — this is critical recovery for your primary red finding.
  • 4
    Cat-cow × 10 slow reps: On hands and knees if possible. Restores lumbar spine mobility after the repetitive rotational loading of a full round.
  • 5
    Wrist flexion/extension stretch × 30 sec each hand (Wrist red): Both directions. The wrists accumulate tension through the grip and impact forces of a full round. This prevents the restriction from compounding overnight.
Minutes 12–20 — Recovery Nutrition

The Recovery Window

Within 60 minutes of finishing your round, consume a recovery meal or shake: 40–50g protein (chicken, salmon, Greek yogurt, or a whey shake), 200g sweet potato or 150g wholegrain rice (replenish glycogen), and 500ml water with electrolytes if you walked the course. This window is when muscle protein synthesis is highest — missing it significantly slows recovery for the next 24 hours.

The Day After — Active Recovery

Keeping the System Moving

  • ▸
    Daily mobility routine (12 min): Same as every day. Particularly important the day after a round when stiffness is most likely. Your TPI findings mean this is especially critical post-round.
  • ▸
    20-minute gentle walk: Maintains circulation and prevents the "post-round stiffness day" that most amateur golfers experience. Brisk enough to feel like exercise, not so hard as to add load.
  • ▸
    No strength training the day after a round: The swing is a high-force, high-repetition activity. Your musculoskeletal system needs one full day before being loaded again. Respect this or accumulate the injuries that cut handicap progress short.
