The Stats Framework

On-course stat tracking converts subjective "I think I need to work on X" into objective "the data shows I lose 1.2 strokes per round on approaches from 150–175 yards." The difference between those two statements is the difference between purposeful practice and comfortable practice.

📊 Data-Driven Improvement
Why Traditional Stats Miss the Point

Fairways Hit and GIR Are the Wrong Metrics

Traditional golf statistics — fairways hit, greens in regulation, putts per round — are flawed as improvement guides. They conflate quality and context. A 15-foot putt holed counts the same as a 3-foot putt. Missing the green from 220 yards is treated the same as missing from 140 yards. Strokes Gained solves both problems by measuring performance relative to expectation.

Why Traditional Stats Mislead
Putts per round: 28 putts could mean excellent putting
OR missing 8 greens by 1 foot (easy chips, short putts).
Same number — completely different meaning.

GIR: 10 greens hit from average of 185 yards
OR 10 greens hit from average of 142 yards.
Same GIR — completely different ball-striking quality.
Strokes Gained normalises for distance and situation. Every shot is compared to the Tour average for that exact situation — distance, lie, and scoring context. This produces a true measure of how you perform relative to expectation, independent of course difficulty or context.

Strokes Gained — Deep Dive

Each SG category measures a distinct part of the game and requires a different interpretation approach. Understanding what the numbers mean — and what they don't — prevents misdiagnosis and misallocated practice time.

🔬 SG Mechanics
SG: Approach — The Most Impactful Category

Where Most of the Game is Won or Lost

SG Approach is consistently the largest differentiator between handicap levels. Research across 200,000+ tracked rounds shows approach play accounts for approximately 40% of the scoring difference between a 10 HCP and scratch — more than any other category.

  • 1
    What it measures: The expected strokes to hole out from your approach shot position compared to where you finish. If you are 150 yards out (expected 2.9 strokes to hole) and you leave yourself 25 feet (expected 2.1 strokes to hole) — you gained 0.8 strokes on the approach.
  • 2
    Distance bands matter: Break SG: APP by distance band — 100–125 yards, 125–150, 150–175, 175–200, 200+. A player who loses 0.3 SG from 150–175 yards but gains 0.1 SG from 125–150 yards has a very specific practice target. The overall number hides this.
  • 3
    Direction of miss matters: Arccos and Shot Scope both show where your approach misses land. If 68% of your short-side misses are left, your tendency is systematic — adjust your aim or check your shot shape consistency.
SG: Putting — The Most Misread Category

Why Putting SG Requires Careful Interpretation

  • 1
    Distance distribution is critical context: A player who consistently hits approaches to 45 feet will have worse SG: PUTT than a player hitting to 18 feet — even with identical putting technique. Poor SG: PUTT can indicate approach shot problems, not putting problems. Always cross-reference with SG: APP.
  • 2
    Separate long and short putting: Break putting into two zones: over 20 feet (lag putting — pace and direction) and under 20 feet (scoring zone — make percentage). A player who lags perfectly but misses 6-footers has a different problem than one who 3-putts from 40 feet.
  • 3
    Make percentage by distance: The most actionable putting metric. Compare your make percentage at 4, 6, 8, 10, 15, and 20 feet to Tour average. Where does yours fall off most steeply? That distance is your putting practice priority.

SG: Off the Tee

What Tee Shot Data Actually Shows

  • 1
    Distance vs. accuracy trade-off: SG: OTT captures both. A short, accurate drive and a long, slightly offline drive can produce the same SG value if the expected strokes from each position are equal. The data tells you whether your trade-off is optimal — or whether you are giving up accuracy without gaining enough distance.
  • 2
    Penalty frequency: OB, water, and unplayable lies are the most damaging tee shot outcomes. Your penalty frequency per round is the first number to address in SG: OTT analysis. Even one penalty shot per round costs more than any other single tee shot metric.
  • 3
    Average approach distance after tee shot: This is the bridge between SG: OTT and SG: APP. If your average approach is 168 yards, what does that translate to in expected GIR? If adding 20 yards of driving distance reduces average approach to 148 yards, the GIR gain from that distance increase is quantifiable.
SG: Around the Green

The Most Undervalued Category at 10 HCP

SG: ARG covers all shots within 30 yards of the green that are not on the putting surface. This category is often dismissed as "just chipping" — but research shows it accounts for 20–25% of the scoring gap between 10 HCP and scratch. A player who converts more chips to tap-ins gains significantly without any change to ball-striking.

Prioritising Your Weaknesses

Having data is only valuable if it leads to correct prioritisation. The most common mistake is practising what feels comfortable, or practising the category with the worst absolute number rather than the category with the highest improvement potential.

🎯 Where to Focus
The Prioritisation Framework

Not All Weaknesses Are Equal

  • 1
    Biggest SG loss first — not worst absolute stat: A player who loses 1.8 SG on approach and 0.5 SG on putting should practise approaches first — even if their putting feel worse. The numbers determine priority, not perception.
  • 2
    Controllable vs. uncontrollable: Some losses are hard to improve quickly (SG: OTT for a player with limited swing speed). Others respond rapidly to targeted practice (SG: PUTT for a player with poor pace control). Prioritise areas where rapid improvement is achievable over areas requiring months of physical development.
  • 3
    Consistency before peak: A player who occasionally makes great approach shots but also makes many poor ones should focus on tightening the dispersion before trying to improve average proximity. Consistency (reducing worst-case outcomes) is worth more than raising the best-case ceiling.
  • 4
    The elimination principle: Identify your most costly single shot type — the specific miss that produces the most double bogeys. This is your priority target. A player who loses 80% of their doubles from greenside bunkers should practise greenside bunker play above all else until that rate changes.

Stats to Practice Plan

The final step in the stats workflow is translating data into a specific, time-allocated practice plan. A data-driven practice plan specifies not just what to work on, but how much time, in what format, and what success looks like.

📋 Building Your Plan
The Translation Process

From Number to Drill in Four Steps

  • 1
    Identify the specific loss pattern: "I lose 1.4 SG on approaches" is not actionable. "I lose 0.6 SG on approaches from 150–175 yards — my proximity average is 52 feet vs. Tour benchmark of 38 feet" is actionable. Drill into distance bands, miss directions, and specific scenarios.
  • 2
    Convert to a practice target: Using the identified loss, set a 4-week improvement target. "Reduce 150–175 yard proximity average from 52 feet to 44 feet." This is measurable, specific, and achievable within a practice block.
  • 3
    Allocate time proportionally: Assign practice time in proportion to SG loss weight. If approach is 40% of your total SG loss, approximately 40% of practice time goes to approach work. Not 90% on putting because putting "feels off today."
  • 4
    Measure after 4 weeks: After a 4-week focused block, review the data. Did the target metric improve? If yes — move to next priority. If not — reassess whether the practice method addressed the actual cause.

The most dangerous trap in stats-based analysis is drawing conclusions from too few rounds. Small samples produce dramatic-looking numbers that evaporate when more data is collected. Understanding statistical validity prevents misdirected practice based on noise, not signal.

Common False Patterns

Traps That Produce Wrong Practice Priorities

  • 1
    The three-round putting slump: Three rounds with 34, 36, and 35 putts feels like a putting crisis. It is almost certainly random variation. Putting is the highest-variance SG category — it requires 20–30 rounds to establish a reliable baseline. Three bad putting rounds prove nothing about your putting skill.
  • 2
    The course-specific distortion: A course with exceptionally fast greens, severe rough, or unusual wind exposure will skew every SG category for the rounds played there. If 8 of your 15 tracked rounds are from the same difficult course, your data over-represents that course's challenges. Track across multiple courses.
  • 3
    The self-fulfilling focus trap: Once you identify a perceived weakness and start thinking about it on-course, performance in that area typically worsens temporarily — the explicit processing effect. A data-driven weakness may appear to confirm itself not because it is genuinely a weakness but because awareness increases anxiety in that specific situation.
  • 4
    Confusing correlation with cause: A player who loses SG: PUTT may actually have an approach problem. Poor approach shots leave longer first putts — which produce more 3-putts and worse SG: PUTT. The putting stat looks bad but the root cause is approach proximity. Always check whether a stat reflects a genuine skill weakness or is the downstream consequence of another weakness.
The Rolling Average Rule

The Only Number Worth Trusting

The 10-Round Rolling Average
Never make practice decisions from single-round data.
Never make practice decisions from fewer than 10 rounds.

Use the 10-round rolling average as your baseline.
Update it after every round.
Only act on trends that persist across 3+ consecutive 10-round windows.

Signal: metric moves consistently in the same direction across 3 months.
Noise: metric fluctuates widely round to round with no clear trend.
Both Arccos and Shot Scope have rolling average views built into their dashboards. Set this as your default view. Resist the temptation to analyse single-round spikes — they almost always revert to the mean by the following round.
⭐

The data discipline: The players who improve fastest with tracking technology are not those who analyse every round obsessively. They are those who track consistently, review monthly, act on confirmed trends, and resist reacting to noise. Treat your data like a coach treats a season of film — patterns over time, not individual plays.

🤖 Arccos Caddie AI
In-Round Club Recommendations

What the AI Accounts For — and How to Use It

The Arccos Caddie AI recommendation engine accounts for your personal Smart Distances, live wind speed and direction, elevation change, temperature, and the specific hole layout. It is not a generic recommendation — it is personalised to your actual shot data.

  • 1
    Wind adjustment: Caddie applies the tour caddie wind percentage formula automatically — into: +10% per 10 mph; downwind: −5% per 10 mph. It adjusts for crosswind carry loss as well. Trust the wind adjustment on approaches over 10 mph; most amateurs significantly under-club into the wind.
  • 2
    Elevation correction: Caddie adjusts for hole elevation change using the course's GPS contour data. A 10-metre uphill approach adds approximately 3–4 yards to required carry — Caddie applies this automatically. On hilly courses this feature alone can save 2–3 strokes per round from incorrect elevation adjustments.
  • 3
    Flag position vs. centre: Caddie recommends targeting the fat part of the green by default — not the flag. If your dispersion shows a right miss tendency, Caddie will adjust the aim point left of the flag accordingly. This is course-management intelligence built into every recommendation.
  • 4
    When to override Caddie: Override when you have specific local knowledge (firm/wet conditions), when your form on a specific club is unusually good or bad today, or when the recommended club is one you have low confidence in. Track your overrides — if you consistently outperform Caddie on a specific club, your Smart Distance for that club may need more data.

📋 Post-Round Arccos Protocol
The 15-Minute Review That Drives Everything

What to Do With Your Data After Every Round

  • 1
    Minutes 0–5 — Shot verification: Open the round in Arccos. Walk through the hole-by-hole map view. Correct any missed detections or false positives (particularly important for chip shots and short pitches near the green, which are occasionally mis-categorised). Never skip this step — garbage data produces garbage insights.
  • 2
    Minutes 5–8 — Identify the day's single biggest SG loss: Go to Analytics → This Round → SG Breakdown. Which category lost the most? What was the specific shot type? Note it. This is your only post-round takeaway — do not try to fix everything from one round.
  • 3
    Minutes 8–12 — Check rolling average movement: Open the 10-round rolling average. Did any category move by 0.2+ SG in either direction vs. last review? A consistent 0.2 SG/round improvement in any category across 3+ months is meaningful — record it in your Progress Journal as a confirmed gain.
  • 4
    Minutes 12–15 — Set one practice target: Based on today's data, set one specific practice metric for your next session. "Approach from 150–175 yds — proximity target under 42 feet for 10+ shots on Mevo." One target, measurable, directly linked to the Arccos data that identified the gap.
📆 Monthly Arccos Review Protocol
Monthly Review — The Strategic Layer

What to Examine Once Per Month to Drive Your Training Block

  • 1
    10-round rolling average — all five SG categories: Write down the current rolling average for each. Compare to last month. Which categories improved, held steady, or worsened? Only categories that have changed by 0.15+ SG/round consistently represent a genuine trend — not noise.
  • 2
    Approach distance band analysis: Identify your worst-performing 25-yard approach band this month. Has it changed from last month? If the same band has been your weakest for 3 consecutive months, it is a confirmed technical weakness — book a Mevo session targeting that specific distance and cross-reference the data.
  • 3
    Smart Distance calibration check: After every 5+ rounds, Arccos updates your Smart Distances. Check whether any club has shifted by more than 5 yards. A gain of 5+ yards in driver Smart Distance that has held for 10 rounds = confirmed speed or contact improvement. Log it.
  • 4
    Penalty frequency trend: Calculate your penalty shots per round average for the month. If above 1.5, identify which holes and shot types are producing the penalties. This is a course management problem as much as a technique problem — adjust your strategy card for those specific holes.
  • 5
    Set next month's single focus metric: Based on the full monthly review, select one SG category and one specific sub-metric within it as next month's primary focus. Allocate practice time accordingly. Do not split focus across all four categories — targeted practice on one confirmed weakness produces faster improvement than diluted effort across all of them.
⚠️ Common Arccos Misinterpretations
Traps That Produce Wrong Conclusions

How to Avoid Misreading Your Own Data

  • 1
    "My SG: Putting is terrible — I need to practise putting." Check your approach proximity first. If your average first putt is from 45+ feet, your putting SG will look poor even with excellent putting technique. Proximity above 45 feet at your approach distances = approach problem disguised as a putting problem. Fix approach first.
  • 2
    "My Smart Distances are wrong — I hit it further than that." Smart Distances include all your on-course shots, including mishits, awkward lies, and punch shots. They are not wrong — they are accurate. Your perceived distance is what is inflated. The discomfort of seeing lower numbers than expected is exactly the information you need to club up consistently.
  • 3
    "One great round improved my SG: APP by 0.6 — I've fixed it." A single round shift of any magnitude is noise. Require 3 consecutive 10-round rolling average improvements before declaring any SG category genuinely improved. Premature celebration leads to abandoning the practice that was working.
  • 4
    "The Caddie AI said 8-iron but I felt like 7-iron was right." Track your overrides. If you override Caddie and consistently finish closer to the flag — your Smart Distances for that club need more data or your feel is capturing something (recent warmup, wind feel) that the app cannot. If you override and finish short/long — trust Caddie. The data wins over feel in the long run.
  • 5
    "My SG: OTT is fine so I don't need to practise driving." SG: OTT being neutral (0.0) relative to Tour is a scratch-level standard. At 10 HCP, a neutral OTT reading likely means you are playing conservatively with irons off tees. Check average approach distance — if it is above 165 yards, there may be distance gains available that would significantly improve your approach SG.
