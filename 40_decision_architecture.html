The Fundamental Shift

From Aspirational Thinking to Expected Value Thinking

Most amateur golfers make shot decisions based on their best-case outcome: "If I hit a perfect 7-iron, I can get it to 15 feet." Decision Architecture replaces this with expected value thinking: "Given my dispersion at this distance, with this wind, from this lie — where does aiming at the flag produce the lowest expected number of strokes to hole out?"

The Core Equation
EV(aim point) = Σ P(landing zone i) × E(strokes from zone i) Where: P(landing zone i) = probability of landing in each zone (derived from personal dispersion data) E(strokes from zone i) = expected strokes from each position (derived from Broadie's SG tables) The aim point with the LOWEST EV is ALWAYS the correct play. Lower EV = fewer expected strokes = better decision.
The Decade system (Scott Fawcett) popularised EV-based course management at tour level. This guide applies the same framework specifically to the 10 HCP player profile — using 10 HCP dispersion data, 10 HCP scoring rates, and 10 HCP-specific decision contexts.
The Five-Step Framework

How to Apply Decision Architecture on Every Shot

  • 1
    Map the dispersion cone: From Guide 11 data, know your lateral dispersion at the relevant distance. Apply the 20% on-course widening factor. This is your probability distribution.
  • 2
    Map the consequences: Identify what is in each zone of the dispersion cone. Assign expected strokes to each landing zone using Broadie's tables.
  • 3
    Calculate EV for each candidate aim point: Run the EV calculation for 2–3 candidate aim points (flag, centre-of-green, conservative). Select the lowest.
  • 4
    Apply situation adjustments: Modify the baseline EV for conditions (wind, pressure, lie quality, fatigue). See Dynamic Dispersion in Guide 11.
  • 5
    Commit and execute: Once the aim point is selected, close the analytical phase and execute from System 1. No revision during the shot.

Dispersion → Probability Distribution

Your Guide 11 dispersion data gives you cone width. This tab converts it to a probability distribution — the foundation of every EV calculation.

Data Conversion
The Conversion Method

From ± Yards to Zone Probabilities

Your dispersion data says "±25 yards lateral at 150 yards." To use this in an EV calculation, you need to know what percentage of shots land in each 5-yard zone left and right of your aim line. The distribution is approximately normal (bell-curve shaped) — most shots cluster near the aim line, with decreasing frequency toward the edges.

Zone Probability Table (±25 yard baseline)
Distance from aim line → % of shots landing in that zone: Within ±5 yards: ~38% of all shots ±5 to ±10 yards: ~27% (13.5% each side) ±10 to ±15 yards: ~21% (10.5% each side) ±15 to ±20 yards: ~9% (4.5% each side) ±20 to ±25 yards: ~5% (2.5% each side) Note: Also account for your personal bias. If 65% of your misses go left, shift the entire distribution left. Your AIM LINE should be right of your target to account for bias.

⚠️

Always apply the 20% on-course widening factor: These are range-based dispersion numbers. On-course, add 20% to every lateral dispersion figure before calculating zone probabilities. A ±25 yard range dispersion becomes ±30 yards on-course. Failure to apply this factor systematically underestimates how wide your real cone is in competition.

Asymmetric Consequences

Why the Left Bunker and the Right Rough Are Not Equivalent

The most important principle in consequence mapping: consequences are rarely symmetric. A bunker on the left and rough on the right of a green may both be "misses" — but the greenside bunker costs 3.00 expected strokes while the rough costs 2.75. If you aim at the flag (between them), you should aim slightly away from the bunker because the asymmetric consequence justifies the asymmetric aim.

Asymmetric Consequence Rule
When consequences are asymmetric: Shift aim point AWAY from the more costly consequence By an amount proportional to the difference in cost Example: Bunker left (EV=3.0) vs Rough right (EV=2.75) Difference: 0.25 expected strokes Shift aim: 5–8 yards toward the rough (away from bunker) This reduces P(bunker) at a small cost in P(rough) Net EV reduction: ~0.08 strokes per shot
⭐

The cumulative effect: Correctly accounting for asymmetric consequences on 8–10 shots per round where it applies produces 0.5–0.8 cumulative EV improvement per round. Over a season, this is equivalent to 2–3 handicap shots of decision-quality improvement — with no change in ball-striking ability.

Aim Point Calculation

The complete step-by-step EV calculation for any approach shot. Work through this fully on paper for your 10 most common scenarios, then use the pre-calculated results on the course.

The Full Calculation
Step-by-Step Calculation

The Aim Point Formula in Practice

  • 1
    Identify the shot parameters: Distance (e.g. 155 yards), lie (fairway), conditions (slight headwind — widen dispersion 10%). Personal dispersion at 155 yards: ±27 yards (range) × 1.20 (on-course) × 1.10 (wind) = ±35 yards effective.
  • 2
    Map the green: Flag is 8 yards from left edge. Right side is 22 yards wide with rough. Bunker is left of the green, 15 yards left of the flag. Sketch this on your yardage book or mentally.
  • 3
    Calculate zone probabilities for 3 candidate aim points:
    — Aim at flag
    — Aim at centre of green (10 yards right of flag)
    — Aim at right-centre (18 yards right of flag)
    Use your probability table to assign P(bunker), P(green left), P(green right), P(rough right) for each aim point.
  • 4
    Multiply probabilities by expected strokes and sum: EV = P(bunker)×3.0 + P(green left)×2.1 + P(green right)×2.1 + P(rough right)×2.75 for each aim point.
  • 5
    Select the lowest EV aim point: This is your target. If it feels aggressively conservative — it is supposed to. The maths are usually more conservative than instinct by 8–15 yards.
Worked Example

155 Yards — Flag Tight Left, Bunker Left, Rough Right

Aim PointP(bunker)P(green)P(rough right)EV

Centre of green (+10 yds)10%58%32%2.33

⭐

Result: Right-centre aim saves 0.21 expected strokes vs. aiming at the flag. Applied to 8 similar shots per round, this is 1.68 strokes per round of decision quality improvement — without hitting the ball any differently.

Tee Shot EV — Driver vs. Iron

The break-even framework for tee shot club selection. When is driver EV-positive? What OB rate makes iron the correct play? The calculation is simple and eliminates guesswork.

Tee Decision Framework
The Break-Even OB Rate

The Only Number You Need

Break-Even Calculation
Define: EV(driver, fairway) = expected strokes from good driver position EV(driver, OB) = expected strokes after OB (stroke + distance: ~4.5) EV(iron, fairway) = expected strokes from iron position OB_rate = probability of going OB with driver Break-even where EV(driver) = EV(iron): EV(driver) = (1-OB_rate) × EV(driver,fw) + OB_rate × EV(driver,OB) Set equal to EV(iron) Solving for OB_rate: OB_rate = [EV(iron) - EV(driver,fw)] / [EV(driver,OB) - EV(driver,fw)]
For a typical 420-yard par 4 at 10 HCP: EV(driver, fairway) = 4.05, EV(iron, fairway) = 4.35, EV(driver, OB) = 5.50. Break-even OB rate = (4.35 - 4.05) / (5.50 - 4.05) = 0.30 / 1.45 = 21%. If your OB rate with driver on this hole exceeds 21%, iron is the better play.

Par 5 Go/Layup Decision

The carry confidence threshold that makes going for it EV-positive — calculated specifically for the hazard profile of the hole you are playing.

Par 5 EV Framework
The Go/Layup Break-Even Formula

When Is Attempting the Green EV-Positive?

Break-Even Carry Confidence
Define: p = probability of successful carry (your carry confidence) EV(go, success) = expected strokes when successfully on green (~2.0–2.3) EV(go, fail) = expected strokes from hazard or bad miss (~3.5–4.5) EV(layup) = expected strokes from optimal layup position (~2.5–2.8) Break-even where EV(go) = EV(layup): p × EV(go,success) + (1-p) × EV(go,fail) = EV(layup) Solving for p: p = [EV(layup) - EV(go,fail)] / [EV(go,success) - EV(go,fail)]

💡

Calibrating your carry confidence: Most players overestimate carry confidence by 15–25%. Before assigning a confidence number, ask: "Of the last 10 times I've faced this exact carry distance in similar conditions, how many times have I made it?" That tracking-based estimate is far more accurate than an in-the-moment feel for confidence.

Pre-Round Decision Planning

A structured template for building hole-by-hole decision commitments before the round begins — eliminating the highest-pressure in-round decisions through pre-planning.

Pre-Round Template
The Planning Protocol

15 Minutes Before Every Competitive Round

  • 1
    Identify your 5 highest-risk holes: From your scoring pattern analysis (Guide 24 Scoring Patterns tab), identify the 5 holes where you most frequently score double bogey or worse. These are your pre-planning priorities.
  • 2
    For each risk hole, write one specific pre-commitment: Not "play sensibly" — a specific, unconditional decision. "Hole 7: 3-wood every time. Hole 12: centre of green always — never the left flag. Hole 16: lay up to 90 yards regardless of score." Write it on your scorecard.
  • 3
    Confirm your three EV decisions: Of your pre-commitments, which three involve the highest EV consequence? Run a quick sanity check — does the pre-committed decision align with the EV calculation? It should. If not, update the pre-commitment.
  • 4
    Set the score context rule: Decide in advance how you will adjust your overall strategy if you are significantly above or below target at the turn. "If 3+ over at the turn: conservative for the back nine — protect against double bogeys. If 3+ under: maintain standard EV play — do not become aggressive just because the round is going well."

Format Adjustment

The same EV logic applies differently to stroke play, Stableford, and match play. Understanding the adjustments prevents the error of playing stroke play strategy in a Stableford competition.

Format-Specific Rules
Why Format Changes EV

The Value of a Stroke Is Not Constant Across Formats

In stroke play, every shot counts equally. A double bogey is exactly 2 strokes worse than par — no more, no less. In Stableford, a double bogey scores 0 points — the same as a triple bogey or a 10. The excess damage above double bogey is worthless in Stableford, which means avoiding double bogeys has the same value as avoiding triple bogeys — but making birdies has twice the value they have in stroke play.

Stableford Strategy Adjustments

How EV Decisions Change in Stableford

  • 1
    The hole you cannot score points on — pick up immediately: Once you are at double bogey on a Stableford hole (0 points certain), picking up and moving to the next hole is always correct. Every additional stroke costs you nothing in points but costs you physical and mental energy needed for the next hole.
  • 2
    Birdie attempts have higher EV: In Stableford, a birdie is worth 3 points vs. 2 for a par — a 50% premium over par. This shifts the EV calculation toward more aggressive aim points when birdie opportunities exist. Flag attacking on accessible pins is more justified in Stableford than in stroke play.
  • 3
    Bogey protection is less important: In stroke play, the difference between par and bogey is 1 stroke. In Stableford, it is 1 point — the same magnitude but lower relative penalty since double bogey is the floor. Slightly more risk is justified on approach shots in Stableford because a missed green costs only 1 point, not a potential double-bogey score.
  • 4
    Double bogey prevention remains the primary rule: Despite adjustments 2 and 3, the double bogey prevention imperative from stroke play transfers fully to Stableford. Scoring 0 on a hole kills a Stableford round just as surely as a double bogey kills a stroke play round.
⭐

Match play addendum: Match play EV is covered in depth in Guide 24 Match Play Deep Strategy tab. The core principle: match state entirely determines the correct aggression level. Never apply stroke play EV instincts in a match play context — the unit of currency is holes, not strokes, and the EV framework adjusts completely.
