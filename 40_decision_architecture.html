The Decision Framework

Every strategic decision on a golf course has an expected value — the average score outcome across all possible results, weighted by their probability. Playing the highest-EV option on every shot is how scratch and plus-handicap players score better than their ball-striking ability alone would predict.

♟️ Why EV Matters
The Stroke Cost of Poor Decisions

How Much Strategic Errors Cost

Strokes gained research consistently shows that mid-to-low handicap players lose more strokes to poor decision-making than to poor ball striking. A 10-handicapper who aims at flags rather than optimal aim points, attempts carries they make 40% of the time, and ignores their dominant miss pattern will score 3–5 shots worse per round than a player with identical ball-striking who makes optimal decisions.

The best tee shot is not the one that goes the furthest — it is the one that leaves the highest expected value approach shot. Those are often not the same thing.

— Scott Fawcett, Decade Golf
📊 The Decade Framework
Five Core Principles

What the Decade System Actually Says

  • Aim at the highest-EV target, not the flag. The flag is almost never the optimal aim point — the optimal aim point is the centre of the distribution of good outcomes, accounting for your miss pattern.
  • Your miss is not random — it is patterned. Arccos data shows your actual miss distribution. Use it. Aim points shift left or right depending on your dominant miss, not the architect's intention.
  • Risk is only worth taking when the expected value is positive. Going for a flag tight to a bunker is correct when the EV of the aggressive play exceeds the EV of the safe play. The calculation, not the feeling, determines the answer.
  • Scoring position changes the calculation. Needing to make birdie on the last hole to win changes optimal strategy — not because the physics changes, but because the consequence weighting changes.
  • Most aggressive-looking decisions are wrong; most conservative-looking decisions are also wrong. The optimal play is usually in the middle — neither hero nor safety mode.

Dispersion → Probability

Converting your actual shot dispersion data — from Guide 11 or Arccos — into a probability distribution across landing zones. This is the input to every EV calculation in this guide.

📊 Your Dispersion Cone
From Guide 11 to a Probability Map

Reading Your Dispersion Data as a Probability Distribution

Your Guide 11 dispersion cone shows where your shots land as a scatter plot. To convert this into an EV calculation, you need to know the probability of landing in each zone — not just the average outcome.

  • 1
    Pull your Arccos approach shot data for a specific club — ideally 30+ shots from a consistent distance bracket (e.g. 150–160 yards with 7-iron). Export or note the landing positions relative to the flag.
  • 2
    Sort into zones: On green (within 30 feet), on green (30–60 feet), fringe/collar, greenside rough, greenside bunker, and penalty/disaster. Count the shots in each zone and convert to percentages. These are your zone probabilities.
  • 3
    Identify your dominant miss direction. If more than 60% of your misses go to one side, you have a directional bias — your aim point must shift to account for this. Most players have a dominant miss; using average data without accounting for bias understates the EV cost of aiming at flags on the miss side.

🔄 Updating Your Probabilities
How Often to Recalculate

Keeping Your EV Inputs Current

Your zone probabilities should be recalculated every 3 months from updated Arccos data — your game changes, especially as you work through this programme. A major swing change (new ball flight shape, new trajectory) will shift your zone distribution and make old EV calculations inaccurate.

Also recalculate per-club separately for distance brackets where you carry significantly different clubs — your 7-iron probabilities from 155 yards differ from your 8-iron probabilities from 145 yards, even if the distances are similar.

🗺️ Building a Hole-Specific Consequence Map
The Pre-Round Process

Mapping Consequences for a Specific Flag Position

  • 1
    Draw the green mentally (or on your yardage card). Mark the flag position. Identify the zones around it: left, right, short, long, and on-green distances from flag.
  • 2
    Assign a consequence value to each zone. Use the table above as a base, then adjust for the specific hole: a bunker on the left that you rarely escape cleanly is worth 3.2, not 2.8. Water right that costs a penalty stroke is 3.5+. A wide flat fringe short is 2.4.
  • 3
    Note the asymmetry. On most holes, one miss direction is significantly more costly than the other. The optimal aim point will be offset toward the cheaper miss side — sometimes by 10–20 yards from the flag. This is the fundamental insight of the Decade system: aim at the centre of the acceptable outcome distribution, not the flag.

Aim Point Calculation

The 4-step calculation from dispersion data and consequence map to optimal aim point. Once you have done this process 10–15 times, the logic becomes intuitive and you can execute approximate versions on-course without a calculator.

🧮 The 4-Step Process
Complete Worked Method

From Data to Optimal Target — Step by Step

  • 1
    Set your aim point at flag. Calculate the EV of aiming directly at the flag: multiply each zone probability by its consequence value and sum them. This is your baseline EV.
  • 2
    Shift the aim point 5 yards toward the safe side. Recalculate EV with the shifted aim point — zone probabilities change (less probability of the costly miss, more probability of the safe miss). Compare to baseline.
  • 3
    Continue shifting in 5-yard increments until EV stops improving. The aim point that produces the lowest expected strokes is your optimal target. For most amateur golfers, this is 8–18 yards away from a flag located near trouble.
  • 4
    Apply your dominant miss adjustment. If your dominant miss goes left, shift the final aim point further right (so your miss lands near the calculated centre). The aim point is not where you aim to land the ball — it is where you aim so that your actual shot distribution centres on the optimal zone.

Flag Left, Bunker Left — 155 Yards

Over 72 holes with 10–12 such flag positions per round, this represents approximately 1.5–2.0 strokes per round of strategic gain.

⚡ The On-Course Shortcut
When You Cannot Do the Full Calculation

The Rule of Thumb That Gets You 80% of the Way

For on-course use without a calculator, apply this heuristic: aim at the safe side of the green whenever trouble on the flag side is worse than a two-putt from 40 feet.

In practice: if the flag is left and there is a bunker left, aim at the right-centre of the green unless you have a specific reason to believe your dispersion pattern will keep you clear of the bunker. The specific aim point — right-centre — is the pre-calculated optimal target for the majority of such situations.

Tee Shot EV

Driver vs. iron decisions, dogleg carry decisions, and fairway width vs. distance trade-offs — all expressed as EV calculations with real numbers. The most commonly misplayed area in amateur golf, and the highest-leverage improvement opportunity in many players' games.

🏌️ Driver vs. Iron Framework
The Calculation Most Amateurs Skip

When to Hit Driver and When Not To

The decision to hit driver or a shorter club off the tee should be based on the expected score from each approach position — not on how comfortable you feel with each club. Driver is correct when the closer approach position produces enough EV improvement to outweigh the additional dispersion risk of driver off the tee.

📐 The OOB Rate Calculation
Using Your Arccos Data

Personalising the Driver EV Calculation

  • 1
    From Arccos, find your OOB/penalty rate with driver over the last 50 rounds. This is your personal penalty probability — not the population average.
  • 2
    Calculate the stroke cost of your average OOB: penalty stroke + re-hit from tee (or drop) = approximately 2.0 strokes above average tee shot outcome. Multiply by your OOB rate to get your expected penalty cost per driver shot.
  • 3
    Compare to the approach EV gain from driver vs. iron. If driver gives you a 30-yard shorter approach, that is worth approximately 0.20–0.30 strokes in expected approach outcome. If your penalty cost exceeds this gain, the iron is correct even if you feel confident with driver.
Driver EV Formula
Driver EV = Approach EV gain − (OOB rate × 2.0 stroke penalty)
If Driver EV is positive: hit driver. If negative: hit the shorter club. Example: 0.25 approach gain − (0.08 OOB rate × 2.0) = 0.25 − 0.16 = +0.09. Driver is correct by a small margin.

Par 5 Strategy

Go-for-two vs. three-shot par 5 EV calculations. Optimal layup zone identification. How scoring position changes the calculation. The most birdie-generating decision framework in amateur golf — and the most commonly executed suboptimally.

⚡ Go-For-Two Decision
The EV Crossover Point

When Going For It in Two Becomes EV-Positive

Going for a par 5 green in two is EV-positive when: the expected score from going for it (accounting for your probability of reaching the green, your probability of a penalty, and your resulting putts) is lower than the expected score from the three-shot route.

📐 The Optimal Layup Zone
Where to Lay Up When You Cannot Go For It

The 80-100 Yard Wedge Zone Principle

The highest-EV layup target is the distance that leaves your best wedge shot — typically 80–100 yards for most players. This is not the maximum distance you can lay up to; it is the optimal distance based on your scoring statistics from that range.

Finding your optimal wedge distance: From Arccos, identify the yardage bracket where your SG: ARG is highest — typically the distance that produces the most approaches inside 20 feet. That is your optimal layup distance. Many players assume it is "as close as possible" — it is often not.

💡

The full-swing wedge advantage: A full-swing 100-yard shot produces significantly more consistent distance control than a 60-yard half-wedge. Laying up to a distance that requires a partial shot — because you overshoot your optimal zone — is a common error that costs strokes.

Scenario Library

Ten fully worked scenarios covering every major strategic decision type — with the EV calculation shown in full and the strategic conclusion derived from it. Study these to build the intuition that lets you apply EV thinking on-course without calculation.

📋 10 Worked Scenarios
Every Major Hole Type — Full EV Calculation

Complete Scenario Reference

Scenario 1 — Driveable Par 4

Short par 4, green reachable with driver, OOB behind green

Setup: 285-yard par 4, green reachable but OOB behind. Your driver OOB rate: 8%. Layup leaves 120-yard approach.
Calculation: Driver: Eagle 5% + Birdie 35% + Par 45% + Bogey/OOB 15% = EV 3.80. Layup: Birdie 28% + Par 58% + Bogey 14% = EV 3.86.
Verdict: Hit driver — EV advantage 0.06. The eagle/birdie upside outweighs the OOB risk at 8% rate.

Scenario 2 — Water Short, Flag Back

Approach with water short of green, flag back-centre

Setup: 155 yards, water short, flag at back of green. Carry required: 148 yards. Your carry rate at 155: 82%. Short miss penalty: 3.5 expected strokes.
Calculation: Aiming at flag: 12% short of carry = 0.42 expected penalty strokes absorbed. Club up 1: reduces short miss to 4%, removes most of the risk. EV improvement: ~0.22 strokes.
Verdict: Club up one. Aim at the back of the green. The longer distance removes carry risk; the additional putts from a longer flag distance cost far less than the penalty.

Scenario 3 — Flag Tight to Left Bunker

145 yards, flag 8 yards from left bunker, dominant miss: left

Setup: Your left miss rate: 55% of misses go left. Bunker EV from 8 yards: 2.80. Green right side EV: 2.15 (40-foot putt).
Calculation: Aiming at flag: 18% in bunker × 2.80 = 0.50 weighted cost. Aiming 12 yards right: bunker drops to 4% × 2.80 = 0.11. Reduction: 0.39 strokes minus cost of longer average putt (0.12) = net gain 0.27 strokes.
Verdict: Aim 12 yards right of flag. The dominant left miss makes this an even stronger case than average dispersion would suggest.

Scenario 4 — Dogleg Left, OOB Left

Driver into dogleg left with OOB left, 3-wood keeps you in play

Setup: Your left OOB rate with driver on tight holes: 14%. 3-wood leaves 195 yards vs driver leaving 155 yards. Approach EV difference (195 vs 155): ~0.25 strokes.
Calculation: Driver EV gain: 0.25. Driver OOB cost: 0.14 × 2.0 = 0.28. Net: driver is −0.03 EV (marginally negative).
Verdict: 3-wood — by a small margin. If your OOB rate drops to 8% (good driving day/open course), driver becomes correct by 0.09. Reassess on the day.

Scenario 5 — Reachable Par 5 in Two

235 yards to centre, water left of green, flag right

Setup: Your 235-yard carry success rate: 68%. Water left EV: 4.20. Going for it — weighted EV: Eagle 4% + Birdie 38% + Par 42% + Bogey 10% + Penalty 6% = 4.66. Three-shot route EV: 4.53.
Verdict: Lay up. Water left makes the penalty risk too costly at 6% probability. Aim for layup zone at 90 yards. Revisit if water were absent: go-for-two EV drops to ~4.45 (positive vs 4.53 layup).

Scenario 6 — Front Bunker, Centre Flag

165 yards, large front bunker, flag centre of green

Verdict: Club up one, aim at the back half of the green. The front bunker is a trap — it looks like you should aim for the flag but the bunker requires a full carry. Clubbing up removes carry anxiety, produces a higher launch angle that lands more softly, and the long putt costs far less than the bunker. Gain: ~0.18 strokes per occurrence.

Scenario 7 — Greenside Recovery, Two Options

Tight lie, 20 yards from flag: bump-and-run vs. lob wedge

Verdict: Bump-and-run unless a slope or bunker between ball and flag makes it impossible. The lob wedge has a higher catastrophic-miss rate (thin, skull) that dominates the EV calculation for most amateurs. A player who makes the lob wedge 70% of the time and skulls it 30% has a worse EV than a player who bumps and runs consistently every time. Run the numbers from your own chip statistics before committing to a high-trajectory short game approach as default.

Scenario 8 — Putt vs. Chip from Fringe

30 feet from hole, on fringe, perfect putting line

Verdict: Putt. The fringe putt from a clean lie with a consistent path to the hole will outperform a chip for the vast majority of players — the error distribution of chipping (including the catastrophic thin) is wider than a fringe putt at 30 feet. Exception: if the fringe is thick and the putter will not run true, chip.

Scenario 9 — Last Hole, Need Birdie

Stroke play, need birdie to beat your personal best

Verdict: In pure EV terms, play your normal optimal strategy — the birdie requirement does not change the physics or the probability. However, if you need birdie to win a competition, the consequence weighting changes: a bogey and a par are now worth the same (you lose either way), so slightly more aggressive aim points become correct because the downside of a bogey is zero additional cost. The upside of a birdie is maximum. This is the one scenario where the flag becomes a more defensible aim point.

Scenario 10 — Approach Into Wind

20 mph headwind, 155 yards to a flag with trouble back

Verdict: Club up 2–3 clubs (see Guide 23 for the adjustment table) and aim at the front-centre of the green. Into a strong headwind, the optimal aim point shifts forward significantly — the wind amplifies dispersion, making back-of-green positions more costly (balls fly long occasionally) and front positions safer. Never aim at a back flag in a strong headwind. The penalty for running through the back is compounded by the wind.

Pre-Round Planning Template

A reusable 18-hole strategy planning framework — identify the two or three key EV decisions on every hole before you tee off. Pre-committing to decisions removes the in-the-moment pressure that causes strategic errors.

📋 The Pre-Round Protocol
30-Minute Planning Session

How to Plan 18 Holes Strategically Before You Play

  • 1
    Walk the course or review the scorecard/yardage book 30–60 minutes before play. For each hole, identify: (a) the tee shot decision — driver or iron, and the aim point; (b) the likely approach distance and flag positions that change your strategy; (c) any hole where penalty risk is high enough to warrant a conservative plan.
  • 2
    Pre-commit to your three highest-leverage decisions. On every course, 3–5 holes contain the majority of the strategic value — holes where a wrong decision regularly costs a stroke. Identify these and decide in advance what you will do, regardless of how you feel on the day.
  • 3
    Write it down. A plan you have written is far more likely to be followed under competitive pressure than a plan you have only thought about. Use the caddie card template or a small notebook. The simple act of writing "hole 7: 3-wood left, aim 10 yards right of flag" removes the in-play cognitive load that causes drift.

🏆 Stableford-Specific Strategy
The Most Misplayed Format

Why Your Stableford Strategy Is Probably Too Conservative

In Stableford, a double bogey costs only 1 point (or 0 on a handicap stroke hole). A birdie earns 3 points. This asymmetry means aggressive play — going for flags, attempting carries, going for par 5s in two — has a significantly higher EV in Stableford than in stroke play.

Players who use stroke play strategy in Stableford are systematically leaving points on the table. The calculation: an aggressive play that produces birdie 25% of the time and double bogey 15% of the time nets: (0.25 × 3) − (0.15 × 1) = 0.75 − 0.15 = +0.60 points per attempt vs conservative play netting ~1.0 point consistently. The aggressive play is EV-positive in Stableford as long as birdie probability exceeds roughly 20%.

🤝 Match Play Opponent Adjustment
Adapting Strategy to Your Opponent's Situation

When Opponent Situation Changes Your Optimal Play

  • 1
    Opponent is in trouble (OOB, bad lie, 40 feet to putt): Play conservatively — put the ball in play and make them beat you. Their expected score on the hole is already high; you just need a par or better to win or halve. No aggressive plays.
  • 2
    Opponent is in a perfect position (fairway, 15 feet to putt): Play your normal optimal strategy. Do not panic into hero shots — they are likely to make birdie but making par to halve is a legitimate outcome. Only go aggressive if you are down in the match and need to close ground.
  • 3
    All square, last few holes: Tighten up to stroke play strategy — every shot counts as in stroke play. The match situation converges to stroke play EV at this point.
