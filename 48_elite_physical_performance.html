Speed Training Periodisation

The Rypstick protocol in Guide 25 covers the mechanics of overspeed training. What it does not cover — because it sits at the Elite level of complexity — is how to programme speed work across a full competitive season without conflicting with technical development or peaking at the wrong moment.

⭐

The peak competition rule: During the weeks immediately before and during major events (Brabazon, R&A qualifying, county championship), reduce speed work to one light session per week or stop entirely. CNS fatigue from high-intensity speed training degrades fine motor control — the exact quality needed for peak competition performance. Do not train for speed during the weeks that matter most for scoring.

📈 Speed Transfer Protocol
Converting Overspeed Gains to Ball-Striking Improvement

The Transfer Session — Acquiring New Speed in Practice

Overspeed gains achieved in training sessions do not automatically transfer to ball-striking unless a specific transfer protocol is followed. The sequence that produces the highest transfer rate:

  • 1
    Overspeed sets (10–12 minutes): Rypstick protocol — lighter club swings at maximum effort. 3 sets of 6 swings with 90-second rest between sets. Record peak speed on Mevo.
  • 2
    Immediate transfer shots (5 minutes): Within 60 seconds of the final overspeed set, hit 6 driver shots with maximum commitment. The nervous system is primed at the new speed level — hitting real balls immediately captures the transfer window before the priming effect fades.
  • 3
    Compare and record: Compare Mevo speed from transfer shots to your baseline average. The delta represents your current transfer rate. Track this monthly — a shrinking delta means the overspeed gains are permanently encoding into your ball-striking pattern.

HRV-Guided Training at Elite Volume

The HRV basics in Guide 06 cover what HRV is and why it matters for speed training. At Elite level — with 30+ competitive rounds per year plus structured gym work and speed sessions — HRV becomes a daily operational tool, not just a background awareness.

📊 HRV Monitoring Protocol
Making HRV Data Actionable

Daily Measurement and Weekly Review

  • 1
    Measure every morning, same time, same conditions: Immediately on waking, before getting out of bed, before caffeine. 2-minute HRV reading with your chosen device (Garmin, Apple Watch, or dedicated HRV4Training app). Any deviation from this protocol makes the data unreliable.
  • 2
    Build your personal baseline first: The green/yellow/red classification is relative to YOUR baseline, not a population average. Most apps calculate this automatically after 2–4 weeks of consistent measurement. Do not make training decisions based on absolute HRV numbers — only baseline-relative status.
  • 3
    Weekly pattern review: After 4+ weeks of data, review weekly patterns. Do you consistently show red on Mondays? (Post-competition recovery.) Do you show green on Fridays? (Your natural high-readiness day — schedule technical sessions here.) These patterns allow proactive training calendar planning, not just reactive day-by-day decisions.
  • 4
    Competition week taper response: In the week before a major event, expect 2–3 consecutive yellow or green days if your taper is working. Consistently red days in competition week indicate either over-training in the prior block or insufficient taper. This is the most actionable training feedback HRV provides.
⭐

The HRV paradox at elite volume: Players who train the most often show the lowest average HRV because they are consistently under-recovering. If your baseline HRV has been declining over 4+ weeks, your total training load — golf plus gym plus competition — exceeds your recovery capacity. The solution is never to train through it; it is to reduce volume for 1–2 weeks until baseline returns. This is the hardest discipline in elite amateur training.

Cognitive Performance Science

Decision quality over 18 and 36 holes is not constant — it degrades with glucose depletion, dehydration, and cognitive fatigue in ways that directly mirror physical fatigue. Elite players treat their brain as an organ to be fuelled and maintained during competition, not just their muscles.

🧠 Glucose & Decision Quality
The Fuel Dependency of the Decision-Making Brain

How Glucose Depletion Degrades Course Management

The prefrontal cortex — the brain region responsible for strategic decision-making, risk assessment, and emotional regulation — consumes glucose at a higher rate than any other brain region and is disproportionately impaired by glucose depletion. Research by Gailliot et al. (2007) demonstrates that willpower, impulse control, and decision quality all decline measurably within 2–3 hours of the last meal.

In golf terms: your worst decisions — the aggressive recovery attempt from trees on hole 14, the unnecessary flag-hunting on hole 16 — are most likely to occur in the back nine, not because the pressure is higher, but because your glucose is lower.

💧 Hydration and Fine Motor Control
Dehydration's Effect on Putting and Short Game

The 2% Rule

Research demonstrates that a 2% loss of body weight through dehydration produces measurable impairment in fine motor control, reaction time, and concentration. For a 75kg player, this is just 1.5 litres of fluid deficit — easily reached on a warm day over 18 holes without conscious hydration.

  • 1
    Pre-round hydration check: Urine should be pale yellow before teeing off. Dark yellow indicates dehydration already present before the round has started — you are beginning at a disadvantage.
  • 2
    On-course target: 500ml (one large bottle) per 45–60 minutes in normal UK weather. In hot conditions (above 20°C) or wind, increase to 600–750ml per hour.
  • 3
    Electrolyte addition in heat: Water alone does not replace electrolytes lost in sweat. In hot or humid conditions, add an electrolyte tablet or sports drink to one bottle per 18 holes. Sodium and potassium depletion impairs muscle function before dehydration itself becomes noticeable.
☕ Caffeine Optimisation
Using Caffeine as a Precision Performance Tool

Protocol for Competition Rounds

Caffeine is the most extensively studied legal performance-enhancing compound in sport, with robust evidence for improved concentration, reduced perceived effort, and maintained decision quality under fatigue. Used correctly, it is a genuine competitive tool. Used carelessly, it amplifies anxiety and produces the jittery tension that is the enemy of a fluid swing.

Dose3–6mg per kg bodyweight (200–400mg for most players)Too high a dose amplifies anxiety; too low provides no benefit

Competition Week Taper

Peaking physically and cognitively for a major amateur event requires deliberate reduction of training load in the final week — the taper. Most amateur players do not taper at all, arriving at major events carrying accumulated fatigue from full training blocks. Elite players plan their peak.

🔬 How to Know Your Taper Is Working
Objective Taper Indicators

What to Look For in the 7-Day Window

  • 1
    HRV rising toward green: By day 4–5, your HRV should be at or above baseline if the taper load is correct. Consistently red HRV in taper week = training load was too high in the preceding block, or the taper volume is still too high.
  • 2
    Swing feels loose and fast: The "freshness feeling" — where the swing feels uncommonly easy and fast — is a reliable physical indicator of peak CNS recovery. If you feel this in the final practice session before the event, you have peaked correctly.
  • 3
    Appetite increase: As training load drops, appetite often increases sharply. This is the body entering supercompensation — deliberately eat to fuel this process, particularly carbohydrates, in the 3 days before the event.
  • 4
    Slight restlessness: A mild sense of wanting to play — of having too much energy — is a positive sign in taper week. It indicates adequate recovery. If you feel flat or lethargic in taper week, the taper started too late or the preceding training block was too heavy.

A full Elite amateur season involves 30+ competitive rounds, multiple gym sessions per week, speed training, deliberate practice, and potentially county team commitments. Managing this load over 8–9 months without accumulating injury, burnout, or chronic fatigue requires explicit durability management — not just fitness.

🔄 The Monthly Recovery Week
Planned Deload for Chronic Fatigue Prevention

One Recovery Week Every 4–6 Weeks

Elite endurance athletes build a deload week into every training block — a week where volume drops to 40–60% of normal while intensity is maintained. The same principle applies to elite amateur golf training at high volume.

  • 1
    Schedule it before it is needed: Plan recovery weeks in advance based on your calendar — not in response to feeling broken. A recovery week in week 5 prevents the breakdown that would have occurred in week 7.
  • 2
    Recovery week structure: No gym training. No speed work. One competitive round maximum. Two short practice sessions (45 min each, low intensity — putting and short game only). Sleep priority. Normal nutrition.
  • 3
    Trust the process: The common fear is that a recovery week will cause regression. Research consistently shows the opposite — performance improves in the week following a recovery week due to supercompensation. The regression fear is psychological, not physiological.
