🔢 Diagnostic Protocol
Finding Your Biggest Gap

Four Steps from Arccos Data to Priority

  • 1
    Pull your Arccos season SG averages for all four categories. Use the last 20 competitive rounds minimum.
  • 2
    Compare each category against the scratch baseline (0.0). A negative SG: APP of −0.3 means you are losing 0.3 strokes per round vs. scratch standard in that category.
  • 3
    Convert to a plus target: if your goal is +1, you need to gain +0.35 in APP, +0.25 in OTT, +0.15 in ARG, and +0.10 in Putting vs. your current scratch baseline.
  • 4
    Prioritise the category with the largest gap per session-hour invested — almost always APP for scratch-level players. See the 12-Week Cycle tab for the full practice plan.

SG: APP — Proximity Compression

The approach metric that matters at plus level is not GIR percentage — it is proximity. The difference between scratch and +2 is 5–9 feet of average proximity from the 125–175 yard zone.

🔬 Zone Focus Protocol
Training the Highest-Leverage Distance Band

125–175 Yards — The Elite Priority Zone

  • 1
    Identify your worst zone: Pull Arccos proximity data by 25-yard band. The band where your proximity most exceeds the benchmark is your primary training focus.
  • 2
    Trace to cause: Excess proximity in a specific zone traces to either distance control (right number, wrong shape) or direction (correct distance, wrong line). Mevo carry data identifies which.
  • 3
    Zone-specific drill blocks: 20-minute deliberate sessions focused on one distance band only, with proximity feedback using a target circle or measured flag distance.

SG: OTT — Width vs Distance

Plus-handicap amateurs lose more strokes through lateral dispersion than distance deficiency. Being 10 yards shorter than benchmark costs far less than being 15 yards offline. Elite tee play is a width problem, not a distance problem.

Putting at Plus Level

The remaining putting gains for a scratch player are concentrated in the 6–10 foot range. Make percentage from inside 8 feet is the clearest single separator between scratch and plus-handicap putters.

The 6–10 foot range shows the steepest gradient between handicap levels. Lag putting (20+ft) is already near-optimal for serious amateurs — it is not where the plus-handicap putting gains live.

🏋️ 6–10 Foot Practice Protocol
Weekly 100-Putt Protocol

Closing the Plus-HCP Putting Gap

  • 1
    Gate drill (6 feet): 25 putts with tee gates 1 putter-head-width either side. Target: 78%+ make rate. Trains face angle precision — the dominant variable at 6 feet.
  • 2
    Circle drill (8 feet): 8 balls in a circle at 8 feet. Complete the full circle without missing — restart if you miss. Target: complete within 3 attempts consistently.
  • 3
    Pressure drill (10 feet): 10 putts with a consequence for missing more than 4. Record actual vs. target make%. This trains the pressure-specific execution that distinguishes competition putting from practice putting.

Short Game at Plus Level

The plus-handicap ARG differentiator is proximity from non-standard lies — not scrambling percentage from easy chips. Tight lies, downslopes, and long rough are where the separation occurs.

Coaching Conversation Framework

SG benchmark data is only valuable if you can translate it into productive coaching conversations. This tab is the bridge between the analytics in this guide and the technical work you do with your coach — the specific language and frameworks that make the data actionable in a lesson context.

🎓 The Data-to-Coaching Translation
How to Present SG Data to Your Coach

The Pre-Lesson Brief Format

Most amateur players arrive at a coaching lesson with a vague sense of what is wrong ("my irons feel inconsistent") or a specific mechanical complaint ("my club is getting stuck"). Neither of these is as useful as a data-driven brief. The format that produces the most productive lessons:

  • 1
    Lead with SG category, not technique: "My SG: APP from 125–175 yards is −0.4 per round vs. scratch baseline. I need to be at −0.1 to reach +1 standard. That's my primary priority." This tells the coach the outcome target. They will identify the technical cause — that is their job.
  • 2
    Show the proximity data: Share your Arccos proximity report for the specific distance band. The coach can see exactly where the ball is ending up relative to where you aimed. This converts a technical conversation into a problem-solving conversation.
  • 3
    Separate execution from decision: Ask the coach explicitly: "Is this a ball-striking problem, a distance control problem, or a target selection problem?" These have completely different technical interventions, and coaches sometimes address one when the player needs the other.

📊 Interpreting Ambiguous SG Results
When the Data Doesn't Give a Clear Answer

The Three Ambiguity Scenarios

  • 1
    SG: APP negative but GIR percentage is good: You are hitting greens but not close enough. This is a proximity issue, not a GIR issue. The coaching focus should be on quality of strike and trajectory — not on hitting more greens. Bring your Arccos proximity data, not just GIR stats.
  • 2
    SG numbers vary wildly week to week: Variance of more than ±0.5 SG in any category over 20 rounds suggests either course difficulty variance (you are comparing hard course rounds to easy course rounds) or genuine inconsistency that traces to mental game issues under pressure. Use Golfmetrics (Guide 44) to add lie quality data — this often explains unexplained variance.
  • 3
    Technical work has been done but SG hasn't improved: The most common scenario. The technical pattern has changed but the SG has not — usually because the change is not transferring to the course. The coaching question to ask: "We've made this change on the range — what specifically is preventing it appearing in my round data?" Transfer failure is a distinct problem from technical failure, and has different solutions.
