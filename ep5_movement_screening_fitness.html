Training for Your Specific Limitations

Guide 06 prescribes a fitness programme for golf. This guide adds the diagnostic layer — how to identify the specific mobility, stability, and movement pattern deficiencies that are limiting your golf movement before you train for them. Generic fitness programmes produce generic results. Targeted programmes based on individual movement screens produce specific, measurable improvements in the movement patterns that actually affect your swing.

🔍 Why Screening Comes Before Training
The Problem with Generic Golf Fitness

Training Compensation Patterns Makes Them Permanent

A player with limited hip internal rotation who trains rotational power extensively will develop stronger rotation — of their lower lumbar spine, their knees, or wherever their body can find the range of motion that their hips cannot supply. They will get stronger, but stronger in a compensation pattern that increases injury risk and does not improve swing quality.

Movement screening identifies the specific limitation before it is trained around. Correcting the limitation first, then training power on top of it, produces both better movement quality and better injury resilience than training without the diagnostic step.

ApproachWithout ScreeningWith Screening First
Training outcomeStronger compensation pattern; injury risk increasesImproved fundamental movement; training amplifies correct pattern
Swing improvementLimited — compensation pattern constrains movement quality ceilingSignificant — removing the movement constraint unlocks pattern improvement
Time to resultMonths to significant changeOften weeks — mobility limitations respond quickly when targeted correctly

Interpreting the Screen

Prioritising Your Corrective Work

  • 1
    Address the most fundamental limitation first. The overhead squat failure tells you the most — it implicates the whole movement chain. Thoracic rotation and hip 90/90 failures are more specific. Start with the test that is furthest from pass criterion and work sequentially up to the more specific tests.
  • 2
    Do not train power on a failed pattern. If you fail the hip 90/90 test, do not add hip rotation power exercises to your programme until you pass it. Training power through a restricted range amplifies the restriction and builds a stronger compensation. Correct the restriction first, then add power.
  • 3
    Re-screen monthly. Most mobility restrictions respond significantly within 4–6 weeks of targeted corrective work. Monthly re-screening tracks the response and triggers the transition from corrective work to power training when the movement criterion is met.

Hip & Pelvis — The Power Base

The hip complex is the most commonly limiting structure in amateur golf movement. Limited hip mobility — specifically hip internal rotation and hip flexor flexibility — is the root cause of early extension, sway, and poor GRF Phase 2 execution in a large proportion of players.

The Three Hip Limitations That Affect the Swing

What Limited Hips Do to Your Golf Movement

Hip LimitationGolf Movement ConsequencePrimary Corrective
Limited hip internal rotation (trail hip)Reverse pivot / swaying in backswing — the hip cannot rotate correctly so the whole body sways laterally90/90 hip mobility drills; pigeon pose; seated internal rotation stretches
Limited hip internal rotation (lead hip)Early extension — the lead hip cannot internally rotate through impact so it thrusts toward the ballLead hip internal rotation mobilisation; butterfly stretch; deep squat hip openers
Limited hip flexor flexibility (bilateral)Anterior pelvic tilt at address — affecting spine angle and dynamic balance throughout the swingKneeling hip flexor stretch; couch stretch; psoas release
The Hip Mobility Protocol

Daily 8-Minute Routine for Hip Complex Restoration

  • 1
    90/90 hip switches (2 min): Sit on the floor with both legs in 90/90 position. Rotate forward slowly to the opposite 90/90 position, keeping both knees on the ground. 10 repetitions each direction, 3 seconds per transition. Focus: feel the internal rotation of the trail hip loading the backswing position.
  • 2
    Deep squat hip openers (2 min): Full depth squat with heels flat (use a wedge under heels if necessary). From the bottom position, use your elbows to push your knees out and rotate your pelvis. 30 seconds hold; 3 repetitions. Progress: over 4–6 weeks, reduce the wedge height until the heels are flat on the floor.
  • 3
    Half-kneeling hip flexor stretch (2 min): One knee on floor, other foot forward. Posteriorly tilt the pelvis (tuck the tail bone under) and drive forward gently until you feel the hip flexor stretch in the back leg. 45 seconds each side; 2 repetitions. The posterior pelvic tilt is critical — without it you are stretching the rectus femoris, not the psoas.
  • 4
    Hip 90/90 rotations (2 min): In 90/90 position, try to press the front knee to the floor (internal rotation) and the back knee to the floor (external rotation) against gentle resistance. 10 repetitions each direction, 2 seconds press-and-release. This is the active strengthening component — not just passive stretching.

Thoracic Spine — The Rotation Engine

Thoracic spine mobility is the most direct physical correlate of shoulder turn — the "X-factor" that produces the separation between hip and shoulder rotation. A player with limited thoracic rotation cannot produce an effective shoulder turn regardless of how well they understand the swing.

🔄 Thoracic Mobility Assessment
Normal Range vs. Golf-Optimal Range

What Thoracic Rotation Levels Mean for Your Swing

Thoracic Rotation (each direction)AssessmentGolf Impact
Below 35°Significantly restricted — likely compensation patterns presentVery limited shoulder turn; likely arm-dominated swing; high injury risk in lumbar spine
35–45°Mildly restrictedShoulder turn limited to 70–80°; some compensation likely; priority for corrective work
45–55°Normal rangeSufficient for a functional shoulder turn with good form
55°+Golf-optimal rangeCapable of full shoulder turn with hip-thorax separation (X-factor); Sportsbox AI metrics in target range
Thoracic Mobility Protocol

Daily 6-Minute Thoracic Restoration Routine

  • 1
    Foam roller thoracic extension (2 min): Position a foam roller perpendicular to the spine at the mid-back. Hands behind head, gently extend over the roller. Hold 5 seconds; move 1 vertebra up. Work from T12 to T4 (low to mid thoracic). This reduces the thoracic kyphosis (rounded upper back) that restricts rotation.
  • 2
    Thread-the-needle rotations (2 min): Start in quadruped position. Thread one arm under the body, rotating the thoracic spine. Follow with the eyes — cervical rotation helps drive thoracic rotation. 10 repetitions each side, 2 seconds at the end range. This is the active rotational component.
  • 3
    Seated thoracic rotation (2 min): Seated with feet flat, a club across the shoulders (arms folded). Rotate as far as comfortable, then try to go another 5° using breath — exhale at maximum range. 10 repetitions each direction. This simulates the shoulder turn movement most directly and is a good daily check on thoracic rotation range.

Communicating Physical Limitations to Your Coach

How to Explain the Screening Results in a Lesson Context

  • 1
    Share the screen results before the lesson. Email or show your coach the specific tests you failed before the coaching session. A coach who knows you have 30° of thoracic rotation will not waste lesson time trying to cue a bigger shoulder turn — they will understand the physical ceiling and design the lesson around it.
  • 2
    Separate the movement goal from the physical ceiling. The conversation with your coach should distinguish between "what movement pattern do I need?" (the technical goal) and "what is my current physical range?" (the current ceiling). The corrective work bridges the gap — the coach sets the target, the corrective programme builds toward it.
  • 3
    Cross-reference with Sportsbox AI. After corrective work has been running for 6–8 weeks, run a Sportsbox AI session. The key metrics to track: X-factor (hip-thorax separation angle), lead arm rotation rate, and pelvis-thorax sequence timing. These are the Sportsbox metrics most directly affected by the three physical limitations addressed in this guide.
