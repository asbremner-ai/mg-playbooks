What Elite Is

Elite is not a third product competing for cold traffic — it is a structured continuation for players who complete the 24-month Standard/Pro journey. The target is scratch to +3, county and national amateur competition, and the performance disciplines that separate club champions from representative golfers.

⭐ Tier Definition
Who Elite Is For

The Post-Scratch Player

The Standard/Pro system takes a 5–18 handicapper to scratch over 24 months. Elite begins where that journey ends. The audience is players who have reached scratch or are within 2 shots of it and want to continue improving — not restart. These are not beginners to data-driven training. They understand SG, they have tracking data, they have a coaching relationship. Elite gives them the next layer of depth.

⭐

Handicap range: 0 to +3 — county-eligible, entering scratch trophies and national qualifying events, seeking Brabazon Trophy or R&A Amateur qualification at the upper end.

New Guides 38–43

Six guides that do not exist at any level in the current system. Each addresses a domain that only matters once a player is at or near scratch — content that would confuse or overwhelm a 12-handicapper but is immediately actionable for a 0–2 index.

Guide 38
Plus-Handicap SG Targets
Elite · Measure Pillar · SG Analytics
The specific strokes gained benchmarks that separate scratch from +1, +2, and +3 — with category-by-category gap analysis and the practice priorities that close them.
6 Tabs
Tab 1
The Plus-Handicap Benchmarks
Full SG target table for scratch, +1, +2, and +3 across all five categories (OTT, APP, ARG, Putting, Total). Derived from Broadie's dataset and Mark Sweeney's proximity data. Shows exactly which categories differentiate each step of the plus-handicap journey. Anchor card: the four categories where scratch players still bleed strokes vs. +2.
Tab 2
The SG: APP Deep Dive at Elite Level
At scratch, approach play from 125–175 yards is typically the largest remaining gap. At Elite, the focus shifts to proximity compression — the difference between 20-foot and 14-foot average approaches from every distance band. Complete proximity target table by yardage zone for +1/+2/+3 standard. Includes the concept of "approach efficiency" — not just proximity, but shot quality per attempt.
Tab 3
SG: OTT — Width vs. Distance at Plus Level
Tour data shows plus-handicap amateurs lose more to width than distance off the tee. This tab addresses the lateral dispersion problem specifically — dispersion targets by club for +1/+2/+3 standard, the fairway width strategy for different hole types, and how to use Arccos data to identify which tee shot patterns cost the most strokes in context (not raw accuracy).
Tab 4
Putting at Plus Level — The Remaining Gains
For a scratch player, the putting gains are in the 6–10 foot range — not 30-footers. This tab covers the specific make% targets for each distance bracket at +1/+2/+3 standard, AimPoint Advanced refinements beyond Express (using feet as a unit rather than half-feet), and the stroke average from inside 8 feet that separates county-level players from club champions.
Tab 5
Short Game at Plus Level — Save % vs. Proximity
The plus-handicap short game metric that matters is not scrambling percentage — it is how close you are getting the ball from non-standard lies (tight lies, rough, slopes) at Elite save distances (15–30 feet). Complete save % target table and the practice priority framework for improving from scratch-standard to +2-standard around the green.
Tab 6
The Gap Closer — Translating SG Data to a Plus-Handicap Practice Plan
A structured 12-week practice cycle built specifically for scratch-to-+2 improvement — derived from the player's own SG data. Includes the decision tree: which category to prioritise first (the one with the largest gap per session-hour invested), how to weight competitive rounds vs. deliberate practice when data is available, and how to re-benchmark every 12 weeks.
Guide 39
National Amateur Circuit
Elite · Compete Pillar · UK Amateur Structure
The full picture of UK national amateur golf — how to enter, what to expect, how to prepare, and how to use national event results to accelerate handicap improvement and county selection.
7 Tabs
Tab 1
The National Amateur Landscape
Complete map of UK national amateur events accessible to scratch-and-below players. England Golf National Amateur Championship, Brabazon Trophy, R&A Amateur qualifying, EG Order of Merit events, Kolven Trophy, and county inter-union events. Entry requirements, qualification routes, and handicap thresholds for each. Which events feed into which selections.
Tab 2
The Brabazon Trophy — Specific Preparation
72-hole strokeplay preparation protocol. How the Brabazon field compares to county-level competition (the quality jump is significant — typical winning score 10–14 under). Course recon approach for an unfamiliar venue, the three rounds of practice that elite amateurs use before a major 72-hole event, and how to manage the psychological reality of playing a four-round event for the first time.
Tab 3
R&A Amateur Qualifying
The qualifier structure for The Amateur Championship — regional qualifying, venues, and the score requirements that typically advance. How to approach an R&A qualifying round differently from a club strokeplay event (the field quality, the venue conditions, the pressure environment). Practical entry guide via The R&A's competition portal.
Tab 4
The EG Order of Merit
England Golf's ranking system for amateur golfers. How Order of Merit points are accumulated, which events carry the most points, and how a structured annual programme of open amateur events builds a ranking that opens doors to invitation events and county team selection. The minimum viable season for a player targeting a meaningful OoM ranking.
Tab 5
Matchplay at County and National Level
Matchplay strategy at elite amateur level differs fundamentally from strokeplay. Hole-by-hole aggression calibration (when to attack, when to take par and move on), opponent management (reading their body language without being distracted by it), the psychological dynamics of being 2 down vs. 2 up in a county team match, and the specific situations where aggression is statistically correct in matchplay that would be wrong in stroke.
Tab 6
Building Your National Competition Calendar
A structured annual calendar framework for a player targeting national events. Which months contain which events, when to peak, how to taper before major events, and how to manage the psychological load of a full competitive season. Includes the England Golf competition portal walkthrough for entering national events and tracking results.
Tab 7
What Elite Competition Teaches You
The feedback loop from national-level competition is uniquely valuable — the data you collect on your game under maximum pressure reveals weaknesses that club competition never exposes. How to use national event rounds as diagnostic data, what to look for in your SG data after a national event vs. a county event, and how to integrate that data into the next training block.
Guide 40
Caddie Preparation & Yardage Books
Elite · Score Pillar · On-Course Execution
How elite amateur players prepare for competition venues — building yardage books, mapping wind corridors, identifying bail-out zones, and the caddie communication frameworks used by county and national-level players.
6 Tabs
Tab 1
The Elite Venue Preparation System
The structured approach that county and national-level amateurs use before a competition at an unfamiliar venue. The three-visit sequence (recon, practice, competitive), what to record on each visit, and how to convert raw observations into a actionable game plan. Includes the information hierarchy — what must be known vs. what is useful vs. what is detail noise.
Tab 2
Building a Personal Yardage Book
Step-by-step guide to creating a functional yardage book for any course. What to measure (front, middle, back of each green; specific pin position zones; cross-hazard carries; lay-up yardages), how to record it (annotated sketches, GPS measurements), and how to use it in combination with Arccos during a competitive round. Includes a printable template for the book format used by many county players.
Tab 3
Wind Corridor Mapping
Courses have predictable wind patterns based on their orientation and local geography. How to identify these patterns during a recon visit — which holes funnel wind, which are sheltered, where the wind swirls. Combines with Guide 23 wind adjustment principles but at a venue-specific level. Includes how to adjust your game plan when the wind is atypical vs. the prevailing pattern.
Tab 4
Green Mapping at Elite Level
Beyond slope percentages — the specific pin position analysis that elite players do on greens they don't know. Identifying the three zones (attack, conservative, avoid) for each green based on slopes, run-off areas, and approach angles. How to map these in a practice round without it consuming too much of the round. The list of questions to ask the greenkeeper that gives you the most useful information fastest.
Tab 5
Caddie Communication for Amateur Competition
Most elite amateurs compete without a full-time caddie — but when carrying a caddie in county or national events, the communication framework is critical. What the caddie is responsible for, what the player is responsible for, the specific language that avoids confusion under pressure ("that's the number to the front, carry is 5 more"), and how to structure the pre-shot conversation so decisions are made behind the ball, not over it.
Tab 6
Self-Caddying at National Level
The cognitive load management system for when you're carrying your own bag in a 36-hole or 72-hole event. Decision-making frameworks that prevent information overload, the simplified on-course reference system (condensed from Guide 31 to a single card for elite events), and how to maintain the yardage book / decision quality through the back nine of round three when fatigue degrades judgement.
Guide 41
Motor Learning & Practice Design
Elite · Strike Pillar · Practice Science
The graduate-level practice science that separates elite improvement from amateur plateau — how the nervous system actually encodes motor skills, and how to structure practice to maximise long-term retention and competition transfer.
7 Tabs
Tab 1
How Skills Are Actually Encoded
The neuroscience of motor learning — why blocked practice produces fast short-term gains but poor retention, why variability and challenge accelerate long-term encoding, and the specific mechanisms (myelin sheath thickening, synaptic strengthening) that determine whether a skill becomes automatic. Actionable framework: the difference between "training" and "practising" and why most golfers spend their range time in the wrong mode.
Tab 2
Blocked vs. Random vs. Interleaved Practice
The three practice structures and when each is appropriate. Blocked (same shot repeatedly) for initial motor pattern acquisition only. Random (changing target/club every shot) for competition transfer. Interleaved (structured variety within a session) as the primary approach for elite-level maintenance and refinement. Research-derived time allocations for each type at different stages of skill development.
Tab 3
Deliberate Practice Design for Elite Players
Ericsson's deliberate practice framework applied specifically to golf — the four elements (focused effort, immediate feedback, working at the edge of ability, coaching guidance) and how to build each into a practice session. The common failures: comfortable practice masquerading as deliberate practice, over-reliance on blocked repetition for skills already acquired, and insufficient feedback loops.
Tab 4
Constraint-Led and Differential Learning
Two elite practice methods: constraint-led (using physical constraints to create motor exploration — hitting off slopes, restricted backswings, impact bag work) and differential learning (deliberately introducing errors to force the nervous system to self-organise). Why these methods produce better long-term retention than technically-directed practice. Specific drill protocols for each approach applicable to putting, wedges, and full swing.
Tab 5
Mental Representation and Self-Coaching
The elite player's ability to detect and self-correct errors is largely determined by the quality of their mental representation — the internal model they hold of what a correct swing or stroke feels like. How to build a more accurate mental representation using video, Sportsbox AI, HackMotion data, and structured self-observation. The difference between verbal and kinaesthetic mental representations and which is more useful for competition.
Tab 6
Periodising Practice at Elite Level
A 12-week practice periodisation model for a competitive season — building from acquisition (blocked, new patterns) through consolidation (mixed, higher variability) to competition readiness (random, high pressure simulation). How to taper practice in the final week before a major event and why. Integration with HRV data to schedule high-intensity deliberate practice on high-readiness days only.
Tab 7
The Transfer Problem — Bridging Practice and Competition
Why players who hit the ball brilliantly in practice score far worse in competition — and the specific interventions that close the gap. Pressure simulation at Elite level (financial consequences, audience, consequence scoring), the pre-competition practice protocol that maximises transfer (not warm-up — a specific activation sequence), and how to use round data to identify which practice elements are and aren't transferring.
Guide 42
Golfmetrics Deep-Dive
Elite · Measure Pillar · Advanced Analytics
The most granular SG tracking method available to amateur golfers — manual shot-by-shot data entry that produces research-grade proximity and SG analytics. For players with 12+ months of Arccos data who want the next level of analytical precision.
6 Tabs
Tab 1
Why Golfmetrics, and When
The case for manual tracking at Elite level — what Arccos misses (lie quality, specific shot type, partial vs. full swing classification, actual proximity-to-hole rather than estimated) and what Golfmetrics captures. Not a replacement for Arccos — a quarterly supplement that reveals patterns invisible to automatic tracking. The decision point: if your Arccos data has plateaued in revealing new weaknesses, Golfmetrics is the next step.
Tab 2
The Golfmetrics Data Collection System
Complete setup guide — account creation, data entry format, how to record shots without slowing pace of play. The three-number system: starting position, lie quality (1–5 scale), and shot result. How to record partial shots (specifically wedge yardages), bunker shots, and chipped shots with enough precision to produce meaningful analytics. Average entry time per round once the system is learned: 12–15 minutes post-round.
Tab 3
Reading the Golfmetrics Dashboard
The specific reports that are most actionable at Elite level — proximity by distance band, SG per shot by lie type, approach SG by direction (left/right/straight of target), and the proximity scatter plot that shows whether your misses are random or systematic. How to interpret the benchmark comparisons (Golfmetrics benchmarks against its entire user database, which skews toward serious amateurs and low handicappers).
Tab 4
Lie Quality as an Analytical Variable
Golfmetrics is unique in tracking lie quality as a variable — most platforms don't. This tab covers how to use lie quality data to separate skill from luck (a player who loses 0.4 SG per round from rough but consistently plays well from tight lies is a different player than one who loses from both), and how to identify whether your weak SG numbers come from poor ballstriking or poor decision-making (both look the same in Arccos).
Tab 5
The Quarterly Golfmetrics Audit
A structured 4-round quarterly data collection protocol — same courses where possible for comparability, full manual entry, and a post-audit analysis template. The 12-question quarterly audit that extracts maximum insight: which patterns are consistent across all 4 rounds (real weaknesses), which are noise (sample variance), and what changed vs. the prior quarter. How to export data and cross-reference with Arccos season totals.
Tab 6
Multi-Season Trend Analysis
For players with 2+ years of data — how to identify long-term improvement patterns, seasonal performance cycles, and which training blocks produced measurable SG improvements vs. which didn't. The specific metrics that plateau first (often ARG and Putting plateau before APP at Elite level), why plateaus happen, and how to use historical data to identify the practice investment that produced the best SG return. A data visualisation template for tracking 8+ quarters of SG history.
Guide 43
Sportsbox AI Integration
Elite · Strike Pillar · 3D Motion Analysis
Using Sportsbox AI's 3D motion analysis to move beyond 2D video — understanding pelvis and thorax rotation data, kinematic sequencing at Elite level, and how to integrate Sportsbox feedback with your coaching relationship.
6 Tabs
Tab 1
What Sportsbox AI Measures That 2D Cannot
The specific 3D variables Sportsbox captures: pelvis sway and thrust, thorax side-bend, lead arm rotation rate, wrist bow/cup in 3D (vs. HackMotion's 2D plane measurement). Why these matter at Elite level — the kinematic sequence differences between scratch and +2 players are subtle and largely invisible on 2D video. Which Sportsbox metrics correlate most strongly with ball-striking consistency at sub-scratch handicaps.
Tab 2
Setting Up and Capturing Data
Complete setup protocol: camera angle requirements (Sportsbox is more sensitive to camera position than 2D apps), lighting conditions, distance from camera, and the club speed range where Sportsbox produces most accurate data. How to capture a proper baseline session — the minimum number of swings per club for statistical reliability, and how to set up a comparison session that makes improvement measurable.
Tab 3
Reading the Sportsbox Dashboard
The key metrics to prioritise vs. which are secondary noise. Pelvis-thorax separation angle at the top of backswing (the "X-factor") and its correlation to power. Lead arm rotation rate through impact and its correlation to consistency. The sequencing graph — how to read the timing of peak velocities in each body segment and identify out-of-sequence patterns. Benchmarks for each metric at scratch vs. +1/+2 standard.
Tab 4
Integrating with Your Coach
Sportsbox is most valuable as a coach communication tool — it gives your coach data they cannot see from 2D video. How to share Sportsbox sessions with your coach before a lesson (the specific export format that coaches find most useful), which metrics to highlight, and how to structure the coaching conversation around Sportsbox data without it overwhelming a lesson. The coaches who are certified Sportsbox users and how to find one if your current coach is not.
Tab 5
Sportsbox + HackMotion: Combined Protocol
HackMotion measures lead wrist position (face control); Sportsbox measures body motion (sequencing and power). Used together, they provide a complete picture of why specific shot patterns occur. The combined protocol: Sportsbox session first (identify body pattern), HackMotion session second (identify club face response to that pattern), coaching session third (address root cause). This is the Elite measurement stack.
Tab 6
Monthly Sportsbox Benchmark Protocol
A structured monthly Sportsbox session that tracks 3D movement benchmarks alongside the existing Mevo and HackMotion monthly protocol. Same conditions, same camera position, same club selection — 8 driver swings, 8 7-iron swings. The four metrics to track monthly: peak pelvis velocity, peak thorax velocity, X-factor at top, and lead arm rotation rate at impact. How to cross-reference with Mevo smash factor data to identify when a movement change has transferred to ball data.
