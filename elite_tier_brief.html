What Elite Is

Elite is not a third product competing for cold traffic — it is a structured continuation for players who complete the 24-month Standard/Pro journey. The target is scratch to +3, county and national amateur competition, and the performance disciplines that separate club champions from representative golfers.

⭐ Tier Definition
Who Elite Is For

The Post-Scratch Player

The Standard/Pro system takes a 5–18 handicapper to scratch over 24 months. Elite begins where that journey ends. The audience is players who have reached scratch or are within 2 shots of it and want to continue improving — not restart. These are not beginners to data-driven training. They understand SG, they have tracking data, they have a coaching relationship. Elite gives them the next layer of depth.

⭐

Handicap range: 0 to +3 — county-eligible, entering scratch trophies and national qualifying events, seeking Brabazon Trophy or R&A Amateur qualification at the upper end.

Guide 41
Plus-Handicap SG Targets v2 — coaching framework added
Elite · Measure Pillar · SG Analytics · 41_plus_hcp_sg_targets.html
The specific strokes gained benchmarks that separate scratch from +1, +2, and +3 — with category-by-category gap analysis, the practice priorities that close them, and a coaching conversation framework for translating the data into a structured lesson.
7 Tabs (v2 adds Tab 7)
Tab 1
The Plus-Handicap Benchmarks
Full SG target table for scratch, +1, +2, and +3 across all five categories (OTT, APP, ARG, Putting, Total). Derived from Broadie's dataset and Mark Sweeney's proximity data. Shows exactly which categories differentiate each step of the plus-handicap journey. Anchor card: the four categories where scratch players still bleed strokes vs. +2.
Tab 2
The SG: APP Deep Dive at Elite Level
Proximity compression targets by yardage zone for +1/+2/+3 standard. At scratch, the 125–175 yard zone is typically the largest gap. At Elite, the focus shifts to the difference between 20-foot and 14-foot average approaches — the concept of "approach efficiency": not just proximity, but shot quality per attempt.
Tab 3
SG: OTT — Width vs. Distance at Plus Level
Tour data shows plus-handicap amateurs lose more to width than distance off the tee. Dispersion targets by club for +1/+2/+3 standard. How to use Arccos data to identify which tee shot patterns cost the most strokes in context (not raw accuracy).
Tab 4
Putting at Plus Level — The Remaining Gains
The putting gains at scratch level are in the 6–10 foot range. Make% targets for each distance bracket at +1/+2/+3 standard. AimPoint Advanced refinements (feet as a unit rather than half-feet). The stroke average from inside 8 feet that separates county-level players from club champions.
Tab 5
Short Game at Plus Level — Save % vs. Proximity
The plus-handicap short game metric that matters is not scrambling percentage but proximity from non-standard lies (tight lies, rough, slopes) at Elite save distances (15–30 feet). Complete save % target table and practice priority framework for improving from scratch-standard to +2-standard around the green.
Tab 6
The Gap Closer — SG Data to a Plus-Handicap Practice Plan
A structured 12-week practice cycle built specifically for scratch-to-+2 improvement — derived from the player's own SG data. Decision tree: which category to prioritise first, how to weight competitive rounds vs. deliberate practice, and how to re-benchmark every 12 weeks.
Tab 7 · v2
Coaching Conversation Framework
How to take the benchmark data from Tabs 1–5 into a coaching lesson and have a productive, directed conversation. The specific framing questions to give your coach before they see your SG report, how to identify which data point is the root cause vs. a symptom, and the session format that moves from data review to on-range diagnosis in under 20 minutes. Transforms the benchmark table from a lookup reference into a coaching tool.

Guide 44
Motor Learning & Practice Design
Elite · Strike Pillar · Practice Science · 44_motor_learning.html
The graduate-level practice science that separates elite improvement from amateur plateau — how the nervous system encodes motor skills, and how to structure practice to maximise long-term retention and competition transfer.
7 Tabs
Tab 1
How Skills Are Actually Encoded
The neuroscience of motor learning — why blocked practice produces fast short-term gains but poor retention, why variability and challenge accelerate long-term encoding, and the specific mechanisms (myelin sheath thickening, synaptic strengthening) that determine whether a skill becomes automatic. The difference between "training" and "practising" and why most golfers spend their range time in the wrong mode.
Tab 2
Blocked vs. Random vs. Interleaved Practice
The three practice structures and when each is appropriate. Blocked (same shot repeatedly) for initial motor pattern acquisition only. Random (changing target/club every shot) for competition transfer. Interleaved (structured variety within a session) as the primary approach for elite-level maintenance and refinement. Research-derived time allocations for each type at different stages of skill development.
Tab 3
Deliberate Practice Design for Elite Players
Ericsson's deliberate practice framework applied specifically to golf — the four elements (focused effort, immediate feedback, working at the edge of ability, coaching guidance) and how to build each into a practice session. The Elite player's practice template that differs from the Pro-level template already in Guide 38.
Tab 4
Contextual Interference and Desirable Difficulties
Why making practice harder produces better long-term results — the contextual interference effect, spacing and interleaving as desirable difficulties, and the "generation effect" (attempting a shot before seeing the answer encodes it better than observing first). Applied: how to redesign a typical range session to include desirable difficulties without losing the practice volume.
Tab 5
Variability Training and the Constraint-Led Approach
Constraint-led practice: using equipment or environmental constraints (a towel under the arm, a gate to swing through, a slope) to shape movement without conscious technical thought. Why this produces more stable motor patterns than verbal instruction. The constraint library for the most common Elite-level movement goals: maximising lead wrist bow, improving pelvis-thorax separation, reducing early extension.
Tab 6
Competition Simulation and Pressure Protocols
How to replicate competitive pressure conditions in practice — the "one chance" drill protocol, consequence-based practice (something at stake for each shot), and the specific mental rehearsal sequence that bridges practice to competition. Research on arousal regulation during motor skill execution and why "calm" is not always optimal.
Tab 7
The Elite Practice Session Template
A complete session structure — warm-up protocol, technical block (blocked), competitive block (random/interleaved), scoring game, cool-down review. Time allocations per block. How the template changes during competition week vs. off-season. The 1-in-3 feedback rule: external feedback (coach, video, launch monitor) no more than one shot in three to preserve self-regulation development.

Wind Game Construction
Beyond Guide 23's general adjustments — building a specific wind game for links and heathland conditions. The three shots every elite links player must own: the low punch iron (full control of trajectory), the knockdown wedge (partial shot held low against wind), and the wind-cheating driver (de-lofted, accelerated through impact). Scoring Under Par — The Birdie Mindset
The specific cognitive and strategic difference between trying to make par and trying to make birdie. At Elite level, your mindset on reachable par 5s and short par 4s needs to be different from scratch-level conservative thinking. The alignment between your game identity and your practice priorities — the highest-leverage insight this system can provide.
