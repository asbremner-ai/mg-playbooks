The Elite Venue Preparation System

County and national-level amateurs prepare for competition venues with a rigour that most club golfers reserve for summer holidays. The competitive advantage is real and measurable — players with written venue preparation consistently make better decisions under pressure than those relying on memory.

🗂️ Information Hierarchy
What You Must Know vs. What Is Useful vs. What Is Noise

Prioritising Venue Information

  • 1
    Must know (non-negotiable): Carry distance to clear each greenside bunker from your typical approach distance band. Which greens slope severely enough to eliminate certain pin positions. The 2–3 holes where water or OB punishes a specific miss — your primary avoidance zones.
  • 2
    Useful (record if time allows): Prevailing wind direction and which holes are crosswind vs. into/downwind. Rough depth and how it affects ball flight. Green speed relative to your home course.
  • 3
    Noise (do not collect): Historical scoring averages. Other players' strategies. Anything you cannot personally verify with your own yardages and ball flight.

Building Your Yardage Book

A personal yardage book is a permanent, compounding asset — it improves with every visit and becomes your most valuable competitive tool on any course you play regularly. Building it correctly the first time saves hours of rework.

📝 Physical Format
The Book Format Used by Most County Players

Structure and Layout

The most functional amateur yardage books are A6 (half-A4) spiral-bound notebooks — small enough to fit in a back pocket, large enough to draw a meaningful green sketch. One page per hole is the standard format:

  • 1
    Top section: Hole number, par, handicap stroke index, scratch target score. Tee yardage from competition tees.
  • 2
    Middle section: Rough hand-drawn overhead of the hole showing tee shot landing zone, fairway shape, hazard positions. Not artistic — functional. The lines should represent what you actually need to know, not a cartographic representation.
  • 3
    Bottom section: Green diagram with front/middle/back yardages, slope arrows per zone (one arrow per zone showing break direction), and pin position zones labelled A/B/C/D (front-left, front-right, back-left, back-right).
⭐

Arccos integration: Use your Arccos Air data from any previous rounds at the venue to cross-reference your yardage book entries. Arccos shows exactly where you missed greens historically — this tells you which hazard carries you have miscalculated and where your bail-out zones need to be.

Wind Corridor Mapping

Courses have predictable wind patterns based on their orientation, local geography, and tree coverage. A player who understands a course's wind architecture makes better decisions in any conditions — not just the prevailing wind.

🌬️ Wind Architecture Analysis
How to Map a Course's Wind Patterns

Wind Corridor Identification Protocol

  • 1
    Orientation assessment: Before the recon visit, open the course on Google Maps satellite view. Note the overall orientation of the course (which compass direction the holes run predominantly). The prevailing wind in the UK is south-westerly — identify which holes run into or downwind of a south-westerly.
  • 2
    Shelter mapping: On the course, note which holes are sheltered by woodland, embankments, or club buildings. Sheltered holes play significantly shorter and with less wind variance — they are where you should take your birdie chances.
  • 3
    Funnel holes: Some holes run through corridors that concentrate wind speed — narrow gaps between trees, valley floors, seaside channels. Wind speed in a funnel can be 30–50% higher than on adjacent open holes. These require a minimum one-club upward adjustment in any wind.
  • 4
    Atypical wind planning: Record your standard wind notes assuming the prevailing south-westerly. Then ask: what changes if the wind is from the east? (The most common atypical wind in UK competition.) Holes that play completely differently in an easterly are your highest-risk decisions — plan them separately in your pre-round routine.

Green Mapping at Elite Level

The green mapping approach that separates elite amateur preparation from club-level preparation is pin position planning — identifying the three zones on each green (attack, conservative, avoid) before you hit an approach shot, not while standing over it.

🎯 Pin Zone Classification
The Three-Zone System

Classifying Every Green Position

ZoneDefinitionApproach Strategy
AttackPin accessible with direct approach; miss short of pin leaves uphill putt; no severe penalty for 5-yard missAim at the pin. Accept the birdie attempt.
ConservativePin accessible but a specific miss is severely penalised (run-off, bunker, severe slope)Aim at the fat of the green — take a known two-putt rather than risk a chip from a difficult position.
AvoidPin cut in a position where a direct approach produces a high-penalty miss (severe drop-off, water, very difficult recovery)Play to the widest safe part of the green regardless of pin position. A 25-foot putt is a better outcome than a chip from trouble.
🗣️ The Greenkeeper Conversation
Extracting Maximum Information Efficiently

Five Questions That Give You the Most Useful Data

  • 1
    "What are the green speeds running this week?" — establishes your AimPoint calibration for the round.
  • 2
    "Are there any greens that are particularly soft or firm?" — identifies putts that may check up unexpectedly and chips that will roll more or less than expected.
  • 3
    "Are there any temporary greens or unusual pin positions for the event?" — prevents a wasted strategy on a hole that is playing differently from its normal layout.
  • 4
    "Which holes are playing longest/shortest this week given the weather?" — identifies where your distance calculations need adjustment.
  • 5
    "Has the rough been cut recently?" — significant for lie quality assessment around the greens.

Caddie Communication

Most elite amateurs compete without a caddie for the majority of events — but for county team matches and major national events where a caddie is available, the communication framework is critical. A confused caddie costs more strokes than no caddie at all.

🤝 The Communication Framework
Role Division and Language Protocol

What the Caddie Owns vs. What the Player Owns

ResponsibilityWho Owns It
Yardage to front/middle/back of greenCaddie
Wind speed and directionCaddie
Club selection decisionPlayer (caddie inputs; player decides)
Shot shape and target selectionPlayer
Pace management and rulesShared
Mental state management between shotsPlayer

The single most important caddie communication rule: all decisions are made behind the ball, not over it. The caddie gives information at the bag; the player processes and commits to a decision before stepping into the shot. Once the player addresses the ball, the caddie is silent.

📣 Standard Language
Preventing Misunderstandings Under Pressure

Caddie Number Convention

The most common caddie confusion is whether a number refers to carry, total, front-of-green, or pin. Establish this convention before the round and never deviate from it during play:

💡

Standard format: "162 to the front, 175 to the pin, 188 to the back. Wind is quarter left, playing about 4 yards extra. I'd say 178." The caddie always gives all three green yardages first, wind second, their recommendation last. The player confirms with the club they are taking: "6-iron." No other conversation until the caddie says "good number" or "might be one more."

Self-Caddying at National Level

For most national-level amateur events, you are carrying your own bag. The cognitive load of managing yardages, strategy, and mental game simultaneously — across 36 or 72 holes — is a trainable skill that most amateur players have never deliberately practised.

🧠 Cognitive Load Management
Decision Framework for Bag-Carrying Players

The Two-Stage Decision System

  • 1
    Walking to the ball (Stage 1 — information): As you walk from your previous shot, gather all information: wind, lie assessment from 20 yards out, rough estimate of yardage. Do not make a club decision yet. Arrive at the ball with data, not a conclusion.
  • 2
    Behind the ball (Stage 2 — decision): Stand 5 yards behind the ball. Check GPS/yardage book. Make the club decision. Visualise the shot. Commit. Step in. Swing.

The most common self-caddy error is making the decision too early — during the walk — then arriving at the ball and second-guessing. Stage 1 is data only. Stage 2 is decision and commitment. Keep them separate.
