The Management Framework

Course management is the application of probability and expected value to every shot decision. At the 10 HCP level, poor decision-making costs an estimated 3–5 strokes per round — more than most technical deficiencies. You cannot out-swing bad decisions.

📊 Expected Value Thinking
The Core Principle

Aim Where You Can Afford to Miss

Every shot has a dispersion cone — a realistic spread of outcomes around your intended target. Course management is not about where you want the ball to go. It is about where your worst acceptable outcome lands, and whether that outcome is recoverable.

The Decision Framework
Best case outcome: where does a perfect shot finish?
Worst case outcome: where does a miss finish?
Expected value: weighted average across both

→ If worst case = double bogey or worse, reconsider the target.
Tour caddies think about worst-case outcomes first. Amateurs think about best-case outcomes first. This single mental shift is worth 2–3 strokes per round.

Tee Shot Strategy

The tee shot sets up the entire hole. A poor tee shot forces a recovery decision before you have even attempted to score. Every tee strategy decision must account for hazard positions, landing zone width, miss direction, and approach angle preference.

🏌️ Tee Box Decision System
The Five Tee Shot Questions

Answer These Before Every Drive

  • 1
    What is the dominant miss? Your natural shot shape determines which side of the fairway is safe. If you miss left, stand on the left side of the tee box and aim down the right — your miss brings you back to centre. Never aim toward your miss.
  • 2
    Where is OOB or the penalty hazard? OOB costs stroke + distance — the most expensive outcome in golf. Any hole with OOB on one side demands your tee shot plays away from it, even at the cost of a worse angle in.
  • 3
    What landing zone width is available? Measure the effective fairway width against your dispersion cone. If the fairway at your carry distance is narrower than your realistic spread, use less club to land in a wider zone.
  • 4
    What is the ideal approach angle? Pin positions and green shapes dictate the best angle into the green. On doglegs, the correct side of the fairway can give 15–20 yards of distance advantage and a better angle. Think backward from the flag to the tee.
  • 5
    What club gives the best risk/reward? Driver is not always the correct play. A 3-wood to the ideal position in the fairway is worth more expected value than a driver into the rough or a hazard.
Tee Box Positioning

Use the Full Tee Box Width

Most amateurs tee up in the centre of the tee box by default. Tour players use the full width strategically — a 44-yard wide tee box effectively widens the fairway by up to 22 yards when used correctly.

Club Selection off the Tee

When Driver Is the Wrong Club

  • 1
    Fairway width under 30 yards at carry: A 3-wood or hybrid with its tighter dispersion is the mathematically correct play. 20 extra yards of driver distance is worthless from the rough at 200+ yards.
  • 2
    OOB present on either side: Stroke + distance is a 2-shot penalty. Any tee shot with OOB present demands conservative club selection unless the fairway is wide enough to comfortably contain your full dispersion cone.
  • 3
    Short par 4s under 320 yards: Driver can carry through the fairway or leave an awkward partial wedge. A hybrid or long iron to a full wedge distance is frequently the better scoring play.
  • 4
    After a bogey or worse: Research shows error rates increase 18% on the hole immediately following a double bogey. Take one club less and aim at the widest part of the fairway. Reset, don't press.

Club Selection — The Under-Clubbing Epidemic

The Most Expensive Habitual Error

Statistical data across 100,000+ tracked rounds shows that 10 HCP players leave approach shots short of the flag 65% of the time. The primary cause is using maximum carry distances rather than average carry distances.

  • 1
    Use average carry, not best carry: Your best 7-iron might go 165 yards. Your average carry is likely 152–158 yards. Always club to your average — the course plays averages, not bests.
  • 2
    Add one club as default: Until you have verified your real carry distances on a launch monitor, add one club to whatever feels right. The vast majority of approach errors are short, not long.
  • 3
    Account for conditions: Into wind (+10–30%), cold temperatures (−5–10%), uphill (+1 yd/ft elevation), wet rough (−15–20% carry). Every factor reduces carry; factors rarely increase it beyond altitude.
  • 4
    Know your land angle: A high-lofted approach stops within 2–5 feet of landing. A low, running approach stops 20–40 feet past landing. Your target zone must account for where the ball will finish, not just where it lands.

Miss Direction — Approach Shots

Pre-selecting the Safe Side of Every Green

  • 1
    Short is almost always safer than long: The chip from just short of a green is simpler than from over the back. From 150+ yards, always favour landing at the front of your target zone, not the back.
  • 2
    Read the run-off: Identify which side of the green falls away most steeply. Missing to a severe run-off creates a near-impossible chip. Identify this before selecting your target.
  • 3
    Bunker vs. rough: A miss into greenside rough is usually preferable to a bunker miss. Factor this into your left/right target bias. Aim the centre of your dispersion cone away from bunkers.
  • 4
    Slope of putt: If you must miss the green, miss to the side that leaves a straight or uphill putt after the recovery chip. Downhill breaking putts after a chip are the most 3-putt-prone situation in golf.

Layup vs. Attack

The layup vs. attack decision is the highest-leverage strategic choice in golf. It is never answered by ego, always answered by expected value. The correct answer depends on your carry distance reliability, the nature of the hazard, and the scoring opportunity created by each option.

⚖️ Expected Value Decisions
The Decision Model

Four Questions That Determine the Answer

  • 1
    What is your carry reliability? Can you carry the required distance 90%+ of the time? Under 90% reliability, the penalty strokes from failures almost always outweigh the scoring gain from success. Use 90% as your minimum threshold.
  • 2
    What does the penalty cost? Water with a drop = 1 stroke. OOB = stroke + distance (2 shots effectively). Greenside bunker = fraction of a stroke. A carry attempt over water requires significantly more success probability than a carry attempt over a bunker.
  • 3
    What does the layup leave? A layup to a good position can leave a full wedge — your highest-percentage scoring shot. A layup to a poor position (awkward yardage, downhill lie, deep rough) can be worse than an aggressive play.
  • 4
    What is your short game confidence today? The scoring benefit of an aggressive play is only realised if you can capitalise on it. If your short game is off today, lay up more often — the birdie opportunity from 80 yards requires functioning short game to convert.

The 90% Carry Rule

The Most Important Number in Golf Strategy

90% Carry Threshold
If you cannot carry the required distance 9 times out of 10,
the expected value of the aggressive play is almost always negative.

Example: 85% success rate over water (1-stroke penalty)
Expected penalty cost = 0.15 × 1 stroke = 0.15 strokes per attempt
Over 18 holes with 3 such opportunities = 0.45 strokes lost

At 75% success rate = 0.75 strokes lost over 3 attempts
At 65% success rate = 1.4 strokes lost — almost 1.5 shots surrendered
Most amateurs dramatically overestimate their carry reliability, especially under pressure, from rough, into wind, or on the second nine. The 90% rule accounts for this by demanding a high confidence threshold before committing to the aggressive line.
Recovery Shot Management

After a Poor Shot — The Decision Tree

  • 1
    Assess the lie first, not the distance: From deep rough, trees, or a steep slope, the lie — not the yardage — determines the available options. A ball in heavy rough 150 yards from the flag has fewer options than a ball in the fairway at 200 yards.
  • 2
    Get back in play above all else: The primary objective of any recovery shot is returning the ball to a position from which a normal shot is possible. A sideways punch out to the fairway is more valuable than an ambitious recovery that stays in trouble.
  • 3
    Never compound a poor shot: The second most damaging management error (after the original poor decision) is attempting a hero recovery. An aggressive recovery from trees that fails turns a bogey into a double or triple. Bogeys are manageable; doubles derail rounds.
  • 4
    Identify the next best scoring position: After getting back in play, your recovery is complete. Now play the next shot from the new position as if the poor shot never happened. Reset your expectations to the new reality of the hole.

The Awkward Yardage Problem

Managing In-Between Distances

Awkward yardages — distances that fall between your standard club distances — are among the most common sources of poor approach shots. The correct response is always a known distance, not a guessed mid-swing adjustment.

  • 1
    Club down and move forward: If you're between a 9-iron (140) and PW (130) at 135 yards, take the 9-iron and grip down. A smooth 9-iron from 135 is more reliable than a forced PW.
  • 2
    Use your wedge matrix: Your 4-club wedge matrix gives you 16 precise distances. Any yardage under 115 should have a known answer from your wedge matrix. If it doesn't, build it during your next range session.
  • 3
    Never force a swing adjustment on-course: Attempting to take 8 yards off a full swing shot mid-round is a technique task, not a management decision. Use a known distance shot from a known setup every time.
Par 3 Strategy

The Simplest Holes Are the Most Mismanaged

  • 1
    Take enough club — always: Par 3s are the most under-clubbed holes on the course. Over 80% of amateur par 3 misses are short of the green. A long miss from a par 3 is almost always a simpler chip than a short-sided miss.
  • 2
    Aim for the fat of the green: The flag is only the correct target on open pin positions. Tucked par 3 pins demand a centre green target — the consequence of missing short-side on a par 3 is frequently a bogey.
  • 3
    Account for adrenaline: Par 3s — especially long ones — produce more tension than any other hole. Tension tightens the grip and shortens the swing, reducing distance by 5–15 yards. Take one extra club on par 3s over 150 yards.

Pin Sheet Strategy — Competition Edge

Using Hole Location Sheets Before You Reach the Hole

Tour players and caddies use the pin sheet to make approach decisions before they reach the hole. At club competition level, using pin sheets correctly is a genuine scoring edge over players who ignore them.

  • 1
    How to read a pin sheet: Each hole shows the green outline with the pin location as a distance from the front edge and the left edge (from the playing direction). "8 from front, 5 from left" = hole is 8 yards back from the front and 5 yards from the left edge.
  • 2
    Pre-round analysis (15 minutes): Mark the 4 easiest and 4 hardest pin positions. For the hardest: identify the safe miss direction (far side of green from the trouble). For par 3s: note whether the pin is back or front — back pins demand more club, front pins demand precision landing.
  • 3
    On-course use: Before selecting your target on any approach, check the pin sheet distance from the danger edge. A pin 5 yards from the left edge with a bunker left = your aim point shifts well right of the flag regardless of distance. Never aim at a flag that the pin sheet reveals is within 5 yards of a penalty edge.
Tiered Green Management — Elite Strategy

The Tier Is the Target, Not the Flag

Most elite course architects use tiered greens as the primary difficulty mechanism. A ball on the wrong tier creates a putt worth 0.3–0.5 strokes more than an equivalent distance putt on the correct tier — due to the severe downhiller or ridge-crossing putt that results.

  • 1
    Tier first, flag second: Your approach target is the tier containing the flag, not the flag itself. A flag on the upper tier demands your approach carries to the upper tier. Approaching short to the lower tier gives a putt that either needs to crest a steep ridge or from which a downhiller is guaranteed.
  • 2
    Cross-tier putt management: A 15-foot putt requiring the ball to crest a false ridge and fall to a lower tier has no reliable pace calculation. The ball must be struck firmly enough to get over the ridge, then drops dramatically. The correct play is to aim at the centre of the crest as your intermediate target — not the hole. Most amateurs leave these putts consistently on the ridge short.
  • 3
    Wrong tier = two-putt becomes three-putt: At tour level, a cross-tier putt from the wrong tier has a 3-putt rate of 22% — more than double the rate from the correct tier at the same distance. At 10 HCP level, this 3-putt rate from the wrong tier is over 40%. Being on the right tier is worth more SG than proximity.

Playing the Conditions

Conditions change the game entirely. Wind, rain, cold, and firm or soft turf each demand specific adjustments to club selection, target selection, and shot shape. The player who adapts fastest gains the most.

Rain & Wet Conditions

Adjustments That Protect Your Score

  • 1
    Distance loss — 5–15%: Wet grip and wet ball reduce smash factor. Add at least one club on every approach. Wet rough reduces distance 15–25%. Take two clubs in heavy rough conditions.
  • 2
    Greens hold more — attack more flags: Soft greens stop the ball faster. You can attack tucked pins you would normally avoid. Short iron approach shots can be aimed at flags you'd aim away from in summer conditions.
  • 3
    Putting pace is slower — hit it harder: Wet greens are significantly slower. Adjust pace expectations — the ball will not break as much. Putts that normally die at the hole will finish 2–3 feet short on wet greens if struck with normal pace.
  • 4
    Grip pressure — the silent killer: Wet grips tighten unconsciously. Consciously reduce grip pressure on every shot in rain. Carry multiple gloves and rotate them every 3–4 holes.

Muscle stiffness: Cold also reduces muscle activation, shortening the backswing and reducing club speed by 3–8 mph. Add this to the ball flight effect. In cold conditions, always take more club than feels necessary — the tendency to under-club is amplified dramatically.

Firm & Fast Conditions

Links and Summer Management

  • 1
    Land short, use the ground: On firm ground, approach shots that land on the green will run 15–40+ feet further than normal. Land on the front third of the green and use the run. Alternatively, land short of the green and bump the ball in.
  • 2
    Driver runs further — adjust targets: Tee shots on firm fairways run 20–40 yards beyond carry. Account for this in hazard carry calculations — you may overshoot intended landing zones.
  • 3
    Avoid short-siding at all cost: On firm greens, chips from the short side run quickly past the hole. The penalty for a short-side miss is amplified significantly in firm conditions — aim further from tucked pins than normal.
  • 4
    Putting — faster pace, less break: Firm greens are faster. Strike putts more softly. Counterintuitively, faster greens show more break on side-slope putts — read break more aggressively and strike more softly.

Hole Type Strategies

Different hole designs reward different strategies. Recognising the type of hole you're playing and applying the correct strategic template is a skill developed through deliberate thought — not trial and error.

🗺️ Hole-by-Hole Templates
Tight Par 4s (Under 380 yards)

Precision Over Power

  • 1
    Identify the ideal approach angle: Where is the easiest approach into this green? Work backward from the flag to find the ideal fairway position — not the maximum drive position.
  • 2
    Select club for landing zone, not distance: A tight par 4 is not about hitting driver. It is about placing the ball in a specific 20-yard landing zone that gives the best approach angle. 3-wood, hybrid, or long iron may be the correct play.
  • 3
    Bogey is acceptable, double is not: Tight par 4s are designed to be difficult. A bogey from a conservative play is a fine result. A double from an aggressive tee shot into a penalty area is not.
Long Par 4s (Over 400 yards)

Length Management

  • 1
    Maximise distance off the tee within control: Long par 4s reward length — but only when in play. Take the most distance you can reliably deliver to the fairway, not the most distance available in your bag.
  • 2
    Accept the long approach: From 190–210 yards with a fairway wood or hybrid, the target is the centre of the green. GIR from this distance at 10 HCP is around 15%. A centre green approach leaves a manageable par putt. Any GIR at this distance is a bonus.
  • 3
    Chip and one putt is the goal: If you miss the green from 190+ yards, a good chip and one putt is the scoring plan. Accept the bogey as a good result on genuinely long holes when conditions are difficult.

Par 5 Strategy Templates

Three-Shot Planning

  • 1
    Reachable par 5 (under 520 yards): Only attempt in two if you can carry the second shot hazard 90%+ of the time. If yes — attack. If not — lay up to your best wedge distance (not just "short of the hazard").
  • 2
    Unreachable par 5 (over 520 yards): Three shots is the plan. Shot 1: maximum controlled distance into the fairway. Shot 2: advance to your ideal wedge yardage. Shot 3: wedge attack. The second shot yardage is the key strategic decision.
  • 3
    The second shot on a three-shot par 5: The most under-strategised shot in golf. Aim for your best wedge distance (typically 80–110 yards). A half-yard perfect lie at 95 yards is worth more than 20 extra yards into rough or an awkward partial wedge yardage.
⭐

Tour data on par 5 scoring: The scoring advantage on par 5s comes almost entirely from GIR percentage — not from being short or long. A player who hits 70% of par 5 greens in regulation scores 0.4 strokes per hole better than one who hits 40%, regardless of driving distance. Get on the green.
