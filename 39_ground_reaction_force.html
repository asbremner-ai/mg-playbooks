Guide 39 · Speed Science

Ground Reaction
Force

The largest untapped speed source in most amateur swings. GRF accounts for 30–35% of all clubhead speed — more than any single technical element above the ground.

The Physics of Ground Reaction Force

Newton's third law applied to the golf swing: every force you apply to the ground produces an equal and opposite force from the ground into your body. Understanding which direction to push and when transforms how you generate speed.

⬡ Guide 39 · Pro Edition
Newton's Third Law in the Swing

Why the Ground Is Your Primary Speed Source

Clubhead speed originates with the body, not the arms or hands. And the body cannot generate speed without something to push against. That something is the ground. The force the ground exerts back into your feet — the ground reaction force — is the raw input that, when correctly channelled through the kinematic chain, drives clubhead speed.

GRF Speed Contribution (Tour Data)
Average tour driver club speed: 113 mph Speed source breakdown (approximate): Ground Reaction Force: 30–35% of total speed Hip-to-shoulder separation: 25–30% Wrist lag and release: 20–25% Arm swing: 15–20% A 10% improvement in GRF mechanics produces: ~3–4 mph additional club speed ~8–12 yards additional carry
Most amateur instruction focuses on arm swing and wrist release — the bottom 40% of the speed chain. GRF training addresses the top 30–35%. This is why players who have spent years on arm and hand mechanics can see immediate speed gains when GRF is introduced — they have been ignoring the largest source.

The Three-Phase Model

Load → Drive → Brake

GRF in the golf swing operates in three distinct phases. Each has a specific foot pressure pattern, timing requirement, and consequence when missing. The tabs above cover each phase in detail.

Phase 1

Vertical Loading

Trail foot pushes DOWN at the top of backswing. Creates stored energy. Often missing — manifests as a weak, arm-dominated backswing.

Phase 2

Lateral Drive

Hip SHIFT toward target before rotation. The most commonly missing phase. Absence produces hip spin-out and over-the-top path.

Phase 3

Lead Side Braking

Lead foot plants hard. Braking force drives upper body acceleration. Passive lead heel is the most common Phase 3 fault.

Sequence

Phase 2 Before Phase 3

Critical: lateral drive (Phase 2) must happen BEFORE rotation (Phase 3). Most players go straight from load to rotation — skipping the most important phase.

Phase 1 — Vertical Loading

At the top of the backswing, the trail foot pushes vertically downward into the ground. The ground pushes back — loading the system with potential energy that will be released in the downswing.

Backswing to Top
The Mechanism

What Happens During Vertical Loading

As the club reaches the top of the backswing, elite players apply downward force through the trail foot that equals 1.2–1.5 times their body weight. This is not a static balance — it is an active push into the ground. The ground's equal and opposite force loads the trail glute, hip flexor, and external rotator system with elastic potential energy that is available for the downswing.

  • 1
    The feel cue: At the top of the backswing, feel as if you are "screwing" the trail foot into the ground. Not lifting it, not rolling it — pushing it downward and slightly outward. This creates the braced, loaded position that makes Phase 2 available.
  • 2
    The position to reach: Trail knee should be slightly flexed (not locked) at the top. Trail hip should be behind the ball, not in front. This position loads the trail glute — the primary spring in the GRF system.
  • 3
    The posture requirement: Spine angle must be maintained from address through the top. A significant spine angle change (lifting, swaying back) prevents effective vertical loading because the body is no longer in the position to push the foot into the ground correctly.
⚠️

The swaying fault: Many players who try to increase trail foot pressure inadvertently sway their hips away from the target in the backswing. This is counterproductive — it shifts weight laterally (sideways) rather than loading vertically (down). The trail hip should rotate back, not slide back.

The Pressure Stomp Drill

5 min · Daily · Phase 1 awareness

Without a club: take your backswing position. At the top, actively push the trail foot into the ground as hard as you can — literally stomp it down. Feel where the resistance is (trail glute, hip). Do this 10 times. Then make slow practice swings trying to replicate that loaded feeling at the top. The stomp creates proprioceptive awareness of what loaded feels like.

🪑

The Chair Drill

5 min · Daily · Hip loading position

Place a chair or stool at trail hip height behind you. Take your backswing and feel the trail hip sit back against the chair at the top — not slide back (swaying) but rotate back into it. The chair gives instant feedback on whether you are loading (rotating) or swaying (sliding). Do not let the hip push the chair backwards — rotate into it.

Phase 2 — Lateral Drive

The most commonly missing phase in amateur golf. Phase 2 is the lateral hip shift toward the target at the very start of the downswing — before rotation begins. Without it, the club comes over the top.

The Critical Phase
Why This Phase Is Missing

The Over-the-Top Epidemic and Its Root Cause

The over-the-top swing path — the most common fault in amateur golf — is not primarily a shoulder, arm, or hand problem. It is a Phase 2 problem. When a player starts the downswing by rotating the hips immediately (without first shifting laterally), the trail shoulder has nowhere to go but forward and over the ball. The club path is set above the desired plane before the arms have moved.

The Sequencing Failure
CORRECT SEQUENCE (Phase 2 present): 1. Trail foot pushes into ground (maintaining Phase 1 load) 2. Hips SHIFT laterally toward target (2–4 inches) — Phase 2 3. Hips BEGIN to rotate — Phase 3 braking loads 4. Shoulders follow — in-to-out or neutral club path INCORRECT SEQUENCE (Phase 2 absent): 1. Trail foot releases (early Phase 1 exit) 2. Hips rotate IMMEDIATELY (no lateral shift) 3. Trail shoulder thrown forward and over 4. Club path: outside-in → slice, pull, fade
The lateral shift in Phase 2 is typically 2–4 inches — small enough that many players have never felt it, but large enough to fundamentally change the club path. A player who has never moved their hips laterally will find this movement feels extreme — it is not. Video confirmation is essential.
The Feel Cue

How Phase 2 Should Feel

The Phase 2 lateral drive feels like the lead knee bumping toward the target just before the hips open. Players who have never felt this often describe it as feeling like "sliding" — which is technically true but has a negative connotation. The slide is not a fault; the timing of the slide is what matters. If it comes before rotation, it is correct Phase 2. If it continues through impact (never rotating), it becomes an actual slide fault.

  • 1
    The bump thought: At the transition from backswing to downswing, think "bump lead knee toward target." Not "turn hips" — bump knee. This thought naturally produces the lateral drive without the over-rotation that "turn" produces.
  • 2
    The pause drill feel: Make a backswing and pause at the top for 2 seconds. Then start the downswing with the explicit intention of moving the lead knee toward the target first. This slow-motion pause allows the body to feel Phase 2 as a distinct move, not something that has to happen inside the flow of a full-speed swing.
  • 3
    The video check: On camera (face-on view), your lead hip should move toward the target 2–4 inches before the hips begin to open. If the hips open immediately with no lateral movement, Phase 2 is missing. This is one case where video is essential — feel alone is unreliable because the absent movement does not register as missing.

👣

The Step Drill

10 min · Key Phase 2 drill · With and without ball

Start with feet together. Take a backswing. As you start the downswing, step the lead foot 12–18 inches toward the target before the hips open. Hit the ball from the stepped position. The exaggerated step forces the lateral drive of Phase 2 into a physical, tangible movement. Most players immediately feel the path change. Gradually reduce the step size over multiple sessions until the bump feeling remains with a normal stance width.

Phase 3 — Lead Side Braking

At impact, the lead foot plants aggressively into the ground. This braking action drives the upper body's rotational acceleration — like the handle end of a whip snapping forward as the heavy end stops.

Impact Zone
The Whip Analogy

How Braking Produces Speed

A whip accelerates its tip to supersonic speeds — not by moving the entire whip fast, but by sequentially stopping each segment. The handle stops; its momentum transfers to the shaft. The shaft stops; momentum transfers to the tip. The tip accelerates to maximum speed at the moment the previous segment has fully stopped.

The golf swing works identically. The lead foot plants (stops). Lower body momentum transfers to the torso. The torso decelerates. Momentum transfers to the lead arm, then the club. The clubhead accelerates as the upper body has already begun to decelerate. The critical input is the lead foot plant — without it, the whip sequence cannot begin.

Phase 3 Speed Chain
Lead heel plants (brakes) ← THIS is the Phase 3 trigger ↓ momentum transfer Hips decelerate → torso accelerates ↓ momentum transfer Torso decelerates → lead arm accelerates ↓ momentum transfer Lead arm decelerates → clubhead reaches maximum speed at impact
The Active Heel Plant

Passive vs. Active Lead Heel — A Critical Distinction

Most amateur players allow the lead heel to settle naturally — the heel lands because the foot has to land somewhere. This is a passive heel plant. Tour players show an actively aggressive heel plant — they drive the lead heel into the ground deliberately as a speed-generating action. The difference in timing is subtle (fractions of a second) but produces measurably different clubhead speeds.

  • 1
    The feel cue: Think of pressing the lead heel through the ground — not just letting it touch down, but actively stamping it. This active thought changes the timing of the plant and makes it earlier in the downswing, which amplifies the braking effect at impact.
  • 2
    The stacked lead side: At impact, the lead arm, lead hip, and lead heel should feel stacked vertically — a firm wall that the club is rotating around. If the lead side collapses (lead knee bending through impact, lead hip sliding), the braking effect is lost.
  • 3
    Lead hip internal rotation: As the lead heel plants, the lead hip internally rotates (the lead glute fires, pulling the hip back and around). This internal rotation is what keeps the lead side "stacked" through impact rather than collapsing forward.
⚠️

The blocking fault: Players who over-apply Phase 3 braking without proper Phase 2 lateral shift first will block shots right. The lead side firms up too early and stops the face from squaring. Phase 2 (lateral drive) must occur before Phase 3 (braking). The sequence is non-negotiable.

💪

The Wall Drill

5 min · Phase 3 training · Lead hip bracing

Stand with your lead hip against a wall. Make a normal swing without moving the hip away from the wall through impact. The wall prevents lead hip slide and forces the lead side to brace and rotate correctly around a fixed point. Many players immediately gain 3–5 mph with this drill as the braking mechanism engages properly for the first time.

🦶

The Stomp-Through Drill

5 min · Impact zone feel · Lead foot activation

In slow motion, practise the downswing focusing on the feel of the lead heel hitting the ground. Exaggerate the stomp — literally stamp the lead heel down as the first movement of the downswing (this is slightly exaggerated but builds the proprioceptive feel). Then gradually speed up while retaining the stamping feel. Players with a passive lead heel feel an immediate change in their impact position.

Self-Assessment Protocol

Diagnosing which GRF phase is missing or weak — using Mevo data, video observation, and feel-based tests that don't require force plates.

Diagnostic Framework
The GRF Assessment Battery

Four Tests — 20 Minutes With Your Mevo

  • 1
    Baseline club speed test (5 swings): Hit 5 driver shots at comfortable effort. Record average club speed. This is your GRF baseline. All improvement from the following tests is GRF-attributable speed gain.
  • 2
    Step drill speed test (5 swings): Hit 5 driver shots using the Step Drill (Phase 2 tab). Record average speed. If step drill produces 2+ mph more than baseline, Phase 2 is missing and is your highest-leverage GRF target.
  • 3
    Wall drill speed test (5 swings): Hit 5 driver shots with lead hip against a wall. Record average speed. If wall drill produces 2+ mph more than baseline, Phase 3 is deficient and is your priority.
  • 4
    Pause-at-top drill speed test (5 swings): Hit 5 driver shots with a deliberate 1-second pause at the top of the backswing (creates bracing time for Phase 1). If pause drill produces 2+ mph more than baseline, Phase 1 loading is insufficient.
💡

Interpretation: The drill that produces the largest speed gain reveals your priority GRF phase. Work that phase first for 4–6 sessions before moving to the next. This prevents GRF training from becoming another unfocused technical rabbit hole.

GRF Drill Library

Eight drills targeting each phase — from barefoot feel-work to full swing integration. Each drill has a specific target phase and a measurable outcome.

Drill Library
👣

Step Drill — Phase 2

10 min · 3× per week · Key drill

Feet together, make backswing, step lead foot 12–18 inches toward target as the first downswing move. Hit the ball from the stepped position. Gradually reduce step size while retaining the bump feeling. The definitive Phase 2 training drill.

🧱

Wall Drill — Phase 3

5 min · 2× per week · Phase 3 bracing

Lead hip against a wall, make normal swing without pushing off the wall through impact. Forces lead hip internal rotation and bracing. Measure speed with Mevo vs. free-swing baseline.

🦵

Squat Jump Drill — Phase 1 Loading Feel

3 min · Warm-up · Proprioceptive activation

Without a club: perform 5 squat jumps before hitting. The squat activates the trail glute and hip loading mechanism. Many players find their Phase 1 loading immediately improves after squat jumps because the neuromuscular pathway for hip loading has been primed.

⏸️

Pause-and-Feel Drill — Sequencing

5 min · Daily · Sequence awareness

Make a full backswing and pause for 2 seconds at the top. During the pause, feel: (1) trail foot is loaded, (2) hips are behind the ball. Then begin the downswing with Phase 2 bump first. The pause isolates the transition and allows conscious application of the correct sequence before real-time speed is added.

👟

Barefoot Drill — Ground Pressure Feedback

5 min · Weekly · Proprioceptive training

Hit shots barefoot on grass (or in thin socks on a mat). Removing the shoe increases sensory feedback from the foot and ground interaction. Many players feel their foot pressure pattern clearly for the first time barefoot — which makes it much easier to modify. 10 barefoot swings with awareness of foot pressure sequence, then shoes on to replicate.

🏋️

Lead Leg Press Drill — Phase 3 Strength

Off-course · 2× per week · Gym-based

Single-leg leg press on the lead leg only. Foot position: mimics the impact foot position (heel back, toes slightly open). 3 sets of 8–10 repetitions. This directly strengthens the lead leg's ability to brake at impact — the physical foundation of Phase 3. See Fitness Integration tab for full gym programme.

Rypstick Synergy

GRF training and Rypstick overspeed training address different links in the speed chain. Combining them intelligently produces speed gains that exceed what either produces alone.

Compound Speed Training
The Two-Link Speed Chain

GRF + Overspeed = Compound Gain

Speed Chain Architecture
GRF (ground → body): Increases raw force input to the kinematic chain Does not directly change neurological speed ceiling Produces power that overspeed training can then express Rypstick Overspeed (neurological ceiling): Raises the maximum speed the nervous system allows Does not increase the force available at the base Requires sufficient GRF input to express the new ceiling Combined: GRF raises force input → more power available Rypstick raises neurological ceiling → more power expressed Compound gain: 20–35% greater than either alone
⭐

The analogy: GRF is like installing a larger engine. Rypstick is like tuning the engine management system to allow it to rev higher. A larger engine that is still limited by the old tune cannot reach its potential. The new tune with the old engine has limited new power to work with. Both together produce maximum output.

Fitness Integration

The physical qualities that support GRF — and how to develop them in the gym to underpin on-course GRF improvement. Works alongside Guide 06 Golf Fitness.

Gym Programme — GRF Focus
The GRF Physical Requirements

Four Qualities That Limit GRF Production

Phase 1

Trail Glute Strength

The trail glute is the primary spring for Phase 1 loading. Weak trail glute = limited loading capacity = reduced Phase 1 energy storage. Priority exercise: single-leg RDL, trail leg.

Phase 2

Hip Mobility (Internal/External)

The lateral drive requires sufficient hip internal rotation range. Restricted hips cannot complete Phase 2 without compensations. Priority: 90/90 hip mobility daily.

Phase 3

Lead Leg Braking Strength

The lead leg must absorb 1.5–2× body weight at impact. Insufficient strength = passive, late heel plant. Priority: single-leg press and split squat, lead leg.

All Phases

Hip-Spine Stability

GRF forces travel through the hip-spine complex. Insufficient stability produces energy leak and compensations. Priority: pallof press, dead bug, anti-rotation core.
