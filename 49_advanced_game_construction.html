Shot Library Construction

Elite amateur players don't try to hit every shot — they own a deliberately chosen inventory of shots and build their game around what they reliably own, not what they occasionally pull off. The shot library is the architectural foundation of a plus-handicap game.

📚 The Shot Inventory Principle
What to Own vs. What to Avoid

Building a Functional Shot Library

A tour professional carries approximately 50–60 shots in their active library — specific trajectories, shapes, and distances they can produce on demand with high reliability. A plus-handicap amateur needs approximately 20–25. The key discipline is knowing precisely which shots are in your library and refusing to attempt shots that are not.

🔨 Building Shots Deliberately
How to Add a Shot to Your Library

The Acquisition Protocol — One Shot at a Time

  • 1
    Understand the D-Plane requirement: Every shot shape has a specific club path and face angle requirement. Before practising a new shape, understand the numbers: a consistent 5-yard draw requires approximately 2° closed face to path. Use Mevo data to verify you are producing the correct D-Plane, not just hoping the shape emerges.
  • 2
    Blocked practice first (acquisition phase): Hit 50 consecutive shots of the new shape in a single session. Blocked practice is correct for initial acquisition — you are building the motor pattern, not testing it. Do not mix with other shots during this phase.
  • 3
    Random practice (consolidation phase): After 3–4 blocked sessions, introduce the shot into random practice. Call the shape before each swing — draw, fade, stock — and execute. The shot enters your library when you can call it randomly and produce it with 75%+ reliability.
  • 4
    Pressure test: The shot is not in your library until you can produce it under pressure — on the course in a competition, not just the range. If a shot that works on the range consistently fails in competition, it is not yet library-ready. Continue pressure simulation practice until it is.

Miss Management System

At plus-handicap level, the game is not built around hitting good shots — it is built around making misses manageable. A player who plans every approach shot with a preferred miss direction in mind will score better than a player who simply aims at the best outcome and hopes for it.

🎯 Preferred Miss Architecture
Pre-Round Miss Mapping

The Three Questions for Every Approach Shot

Before selecting a target for any approach shot, a plus-handicap player answers three questions:

  • 1
    If I miss this target by 10 yards in each direction — where do I end up? Map the consequence of a left miss, a right miss, a short miss, and a long miss for every approach shot. The majority of shots in a round will not be perfectly struck — the game plan must account for this reality.
  • 2
    Which miss is most recoverable? On most holes, one direction of miss is significantly more recoverable than others. This is the preferred miss direction — the target should be set so that your natural miss shape lands in the preferred miss zone.
  • 3
    Does my shot shape align with my preferred miss? If your natural miss with a mid-iron is a slight pull-fade and the preferred miss is left of the green (not right where the water is), your natural shape is your friend on this hole. If the preferred miss is right and your natural shape goes left, you need to either change the shape or change the target.
🗺️ Course Miss Map — Pre-Round Preparation
Applying Miss Management to Venue Preparation

Building a Course-Specific Miss Plan

In your yardage book (Guide 43), add one additional notation per hole: an arrow indicating the preferred miss direction for the approach shot. This notation takes 2 seconds to record and produces measurable scoring improvement by converting a reactive decision into a proactive one.

🔬 Miss Pattern Diagnosis
Using Mevo and Arccos Data to Identify Your Miss Pattern

Systematic Miss Analysis

  • 1
    Pull your Arccos approach shot map for the last 20 rounds. Filter by distance band (125–150, 150–175 etc.). The visual scatter of approach shots around greens shows your actual miss pattern — not your perceived miss pattern.
  • 2
    Compare your actual miss pattern to your course management decisions. If 70% of your 150-yard misses go left but you routinely aim at right-side pins, you are playing the wrong game plan. Your strategy should be built around your actual miss pattern, not your ideal shot.
  • 3
    Update your course management system: set targets to the right side of greens on holes where left is the safe miss (because your natural miss goes left to the safe zone). This is counterintuitive but correct — aim away from where you want the ball to go, toward where it will end up if you miss.

Wind Game Construction

UK elite amateur golf is played predominantly on heathland and links courses where wind is not a variable to adjust for — it is a core condition to build a game around. The three shots that every elite UK player must own, and the decision framework that governs when to use each.

"In the wind, your 6-iron punch is not a lesser shot than your normal 8-iron — it is a better shot, because you chose it for the conditions. The player who commits to the punch wins the hole. The player who tries to hit their normal 8-iron into 25 mph loses it."

🔨 Building the Wind Game in Practice
When and How to Practise Wind Shots

Deliberate Wind Practice Protocol

  • 1
    Practise wind shots only in wind: Range sessions on calm days do not train wind shots — they train a shape that happens to be low. True wind shot practice requires actual wind resistance. Schedule specific wind-day range sessions when conditions are above 15 mph.
  • 2
    The punch iron session (45 min): 20 balls with each of your 6-iron, 7-iron, and 8-iron in punch mode. Focus on consistent contact and predictable trajectory, not maximum distance. Record Mevo carry — the punch carry is your working distance for course management decisions.
  • 3
    The bump-and-run library: On a course with firm ground (autumn or a dry summer spell), dedicate one practice round to playing every approach shot within 80 yards as a bump-and-run. This forces you to learn your club's release from various distances — knowledge that is unavailable on a soft, summer parkland course.

Scoring Under Par — The Mindset Shift

The cognitive difference between a scratch golfer and a plus-handicap golfer is not primarily technical — it is in how they approach holes where birdie is possible. The scratch player avoids bogey; the plus player hunts birdie. This is not aggression for its own sake — it is EV-positive strategy applied consistently.

📊 The EV Architecture of Birdie Hunting
Scott Fawcett's Decade Framework at Elite Level

When to Attack vs. When to Manage

The Decade framework identifies that most amateur golfers play too conservatively on holes where they have a scoring advantage (reachable par 5s, short par 4s, favourable par 3 conditions) and too aggressively on holes where they have a deficit (tight driving holes, water par 3s, firm fast greens). The net result is that their aggressive instincts cost strokes and their conservative instincts do not save them.

Course-Specific Game Plans

A course-specific game plan is not a target for every shot — it is a set of pre-made decisions that eliminate in-round deliberation on the holes that most commonly trigger poor choices. Five minutes of planning the night before saves 50 minutes of cognitive load during the round.

📋 Game Plan Architecture
What a Plus-HCP Game Plan Contains

Five-Component Plan Structure

  • 1
    Birdie holes identified: The 4–6 holes where you have the highest probability of making birdie based on your shot library and the hole design. These are your attack holes — you take more risk here than on other holes because the expected value supports it.
  • 2
    Bogey-avoidance holes identified: The 3–4 holes where double bogey or worse is most likely if you make an error. On these holes, your strategy is conservative throughout — tee shot, approach, and short game all target the highest-percentage outcome, not the best outcome.
  • 3
    Miss directions pre-decided: One notation per hole in your yardage book — the preferred miss direction for the approach shot. Removes the in-round deliberation from the most consequential decision type.
  • 4
    Wind adjustments pre-planned: If conditions are forecast (check the morning of), identify which 3–4 holes change strategy most dramatically in the expected wind direction and speed.
  • 5
    Process goal for the round: One sentence describing how you want to play — not a score. "Commit to every pre-shot routine and accept the outcome" is a valid process goal. "Shoot under par" is not a process goal.
⏱️ The 5-Minute Pre-Round Planning Session
When and How to Build the Game Plan

The Night Before — Not the Morning Of

Game plan construction happens the evening before the round, not in the car on the way to the course or on the first tee. The planning session should take 5 minutes maximum — you are confirming pre-existing decisions from your yardage book, not creating new analysis. New analysis under time pressure produces poor decisions.

⭐

Once the plan is made — follow it: The game plan is only valuable if you adhere to it under pressure. The moment you deviate from it because "the pin looks gettable" or "I feel good today," you have abandoned the analytical work for in-the-moment emotional reasoning. In-the-moment emotional reasoning produces worse outcomes than pre-planned analytical decisions in every sport studied. Make the plan; follow the plan.
