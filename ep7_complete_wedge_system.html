The Elite Wedge System Framework

Five Building Blocks

  • 1
    Wedge matrix: Your full carry distances for each wedge at full, three-quarter, half, and quarter swing. The foundation of everything else — must be built at the range with Mevo, not estimated.
  • 2
    Partial shot system: A structured approach to partial distances that replaces guesswork with consistent technique and known carry. The most common cause of wedge unreliability is inconsistent partial swings.
  • 3
    Trajectory and spin control: Two trajectories per wedge (standard and knockdown) and spin manipulation for firm vs. soft greens. The minimum for county-level wedge play.
  • 4
    Lie variation protocol: A systematic approach to non-ideal lies — tight, rough, downslope, upslope — that produces predictable outcomes rather than guesses.
  • 5
    Pressure consistency: The practice structure that ensures wedge quality holds under competitive pressure — the most commonly ignored component of wedge development.

The Complete Wedge Matrix

A four-club, four-swing-length matrix that covers every distance from 40 to 130+ yards with a full swing or known partial. Built using Mevo — not estimates, not range perception. This matrix is the only reliable foundation for the rest of the wedge system.

📐 Building the Matrix
The Mevo Session Protocol for Wedge Matrix Construction

How to Build a Reliable Carry Distance Matrix

  • 1
    Session setup: Range balls only. Mevo in carry distance mode. 15 shots per club per swing length (60 shots per club; 240 total for a 4-wedge system). Use the same ball type for the entire session — ball type affects wedge spin significantly.
  • 2
    The four swing lengths: Full (100%), three-quarter (hands to shoulder on backswing), half (hands to hip on backswing), quarter (wrist hinge only). Each length should feel distinctly different and produce a consistent, reliable carry at practice ball specification.
  • 3
    Record the middle 10 of 15: Discard the top 2 and bottom 3 shots per set. The middle 10 median carry is your reliable matrix distance — not the average of all 15, which is inflated by your best shots.
  • 4
    Apply the range-ball correction factor: Range balls carry 10–15% shorter than premium balls on most launch monitors. Multiply your Mevo carries by 1.12 (conservative) for on-course estimates. Verify the correction factor with 10 premium ball shots once per month.

The matrix should have no more than 8-yard gaps between any two adjacent cells. If gaps exceed 10 yards at any point, an additional swing length or club needs to be developed to fill the gap — this is where wedge distances are most commonly lost during competitive rounds.

The Partial Shot System

Partial shots — anything between half and three-quarter swing — are the source of the most wedge inconsistency for most amateurs. The problem is not ability; it is the absence of a systematic approach. A structured partial shot system converts inconsistent partials into reliable distance-controlled shots.

🎯 The Clock System
The Most Reliable Partial Shot Framework

Using Lead Arm Position as the Swing Length Reference

The clock system uses lead arm position at the top of the backswing as the swing length reference. Most players can reliably reproduce a specific arm position — unlike specific "percentage" swings, which are too abstract to be reproducible under pressure.

Pressure-Proofing Your Partials

The Most Common Failure Mode Under Pressure

Under competitive pressure, most players revert to a more intuitive approach to partial shots — guessing the distance and swinging with some vague intention of hitting it a certain yardage. The result is the most inconsistent shot type in their game appearing in the most consequential moments.

  • 1
    Pre-commit to clock position, not distance. Before a partial shot, decide "this is a 9 o'clock with the gap wedge" — not "this is an 85-yard shot." The clock position is the motor programme cue; the distance is the consequence. Think in swing lengths, not in yardages.
  • 2
    Build through-swing symmetry. A half-swing backswing should be matched by a half-swing follow-through. The most common partial shot error is an abbreviated follow-through that produces a deceleration through impact — causing fat, thin, and direction errors. Full commitment to the follow-through position is as important as the backswing length.
  • 3
    Temperature adjustment for partials. Partial shots are more sensitive to temperature changes than full shots — the reduced swing speed means the ball compression effect of cold temperature is amplified. In cold conditions, add half a club to partial distances more aggressively than to full carries.

Trajectory Control

Two trajectory variants per wedge — standard and knockdown — double the conditions under which you can execute the shot effectively. The knockdown wedge is not an advanced shot; it is a fundamental for any player competing on firm or windy UK courses.

🏹 Standard and Knockdown
The Two-Trajectory Wedge System

When and How to Use Each

TrajectorySetupFeelUse When
StandardBall in normal position; hands neutral; standard follow-throughNormal swing feel; ball launches at normal heightNormal conditions; receptive greens; no significant wind

⭐

Carry adjustment for knockdown: The knockdown version of any wedge shot carries 8–12% shorter than the standard version with the same clock position. When switching to knockdown, take one more club or one longer clock position to achieve the same carry. Verify in a Mevo session — this varies by player.

Spin Control

Spin is the most commonly misunderstood variable in wedge play. Most amateurs chase maximum spin without understanding when high spin is beneficial and when it is counterproductive. Elite wedge play requires deliberate spin management — not maximum spin production.

Groove Maintenance and Spin Consistency

The Equipment Variable in Your Spin Profile

Grooves wear down faster than most players realise — significantly worn grooves can reduce spin by 15–20% on approach shots from rough and semi-rough lies. Players who use premium balls and swing at high speed accelerate groove wear fastest.

  • 1
    Clean grooves before every round. A groove cleaning brush used before the round removes compacted debris that reduces contact surface area and spin generation. Takes 2 minutes; the spin benefit on pitch shots from rough is measurable.
  • 2
    Replace wedges after 75–100 rounds. Premium steel grooves lose measurable spin-generating capacity after approximately 75–100 rounds of active use. Track your wedge age — if your highest-use wedges (typically 52° and 58°) are more than 2 seasons old and you are playing 50+ rounds per year, they need replacing.
  • 3
    Test spin with a Mevo session. Periodically run a 15-shot spin check with your most-used wedge at full distance — specifically from light rough. Compare to your established baseline. A reduction of >1,000 RPM signals groove wear that may be affecting your course performance.

The Wind Wedge Game

Wind has a disproportionate effect on wedge shots compared to longer clubs — the slower ball speed and higher ball flight make wedges the most wind-sensitive club type in the bag. A systematic wind wedge framework prevents the most common scoring disasters in windy conditions.

⭐

Links wind principle: On links and coastal courses, the wind is rarely steady — it gusts and shifts throughout a round. Pre-plan your wind adjustments at the start of each hole rather than mid-approach when judgement is under time pressure. Check the flag direction at the green from the fairway; note any change from the tee shot wind direction.

The One-Number Rule

The Simplest Way to Improve Wedge Decision-Making on the Course

Before every wedge approach shot in competition, determine one number: the carry distance required to land the ball on the front edge of the green — ignoring the flag position entirely. Carry the ball to the front edge as a minimum. Then decide how much more you want to add based on the pin position and your confidence in the specific distance.

This rule prevents the most common wedge error in competition: coming up short of the green. Short misses on wedge shots produce the worst outcomes (chip from rough, bunker, collection area) relative to the same distance long (on the green, runoff area). The one-number rule biases your wedge approach toward missing in the better direction.
