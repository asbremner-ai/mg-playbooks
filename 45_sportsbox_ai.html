What Sportsbox AI Measures

Sportsbox AI converts smartphone video into 3D motion analysis — measuring variables that 2D video cannot capture. At plus-handicap level, the movement differences between players are subtle and largely invisible to the naked eye. Sportsbox makes them quantifiable.

🔄 Baseline Capture Protocol
First Session

Building Your Sportsbox Baseline

  • 1
    Warm up thoroughly first — 20+ balls. Sportsbox data from cold swings does not represent competitive movement patterns.
  • 2
    Driver: 8 swings at full effort. Use the average view (not individual swings) for baseline numbers.
  • 3
    7-iron: 8 swings. Iron data is more consistent and reveals technical patterns more clearly than driver for most players.
  • 4
    Record the session date and conditions. Sportsbox data is most useful when compared to prior sessions in similar conditions.

Coach Integration

Sportsbox is most valuable as a coach communication tool. Share data before a lesson — not during — so the coach arrives with a hypothesis already formed and the lesson spends its time testing and fixing, not diagnosing.

🎓 Pre-Lesson Protocol
Maximising Lesson Value with Sportsbox Data

Three Steps

  • 1
    3–5 days before the lesson: Capture a Sportsbox session. Export the PDF summary or shareable link.
  • 2
    Send with context: One paragraph: the SG problem you are trying to solve, plus the four priority metrics and sequencing graph from the session.
  • 3
    In the lesson: Arrive with a specific question: "The data shows my thorax is peaking before my pelvis — is that the root cause of my ball-striking inconsistency from 150 yards?" The lesson then tests and addresses that hypothesis.

Sportsbox + HackMotion Protocol

HackMotion and Sportsbox together form the Elite measurement stack. Combined sessions reveal cause-and-effect chains that neither tool identifies alone.

🔗 The Combined Session Workflow
Monthly Three-Tool Session

Sequenced Data Collection

  • 1
    Sportsbox first (15 min): 8 driver, 8 7-iron. Note four priority metrics and sequencing graph.
  • 2
    HackMotion second (15 min): 10 driver swings. Note lead wrist position at top and impact. Cross-reference with Sportsbox X-Factor and lead arm rotation rate — they should correlate predictably.
  • 3
    Mevo third (15 min): 10 driver shots with full data. Note smash factor, lateral offset, spin rate. If dispersion exceeds Guide 41 OTT targets, trace cause using Sportsbox (path) and HackMotion (face).
  • 4
    Synthesis (5 min): Three sentences in Progress Journal: what the data showed, what it suggests technically, what coaching question it generates.

Fault Library

The eight most common movement patterns seen in plus-handicap amateur Sportsbox data — what they look like in the metrics, what they produce in ball flight, and the interventions that address each. This is the diagnostic reference that converts raw Sportsbox data into actionable training priorities.

🔬 The Elite Fault Library
Fault 1 — Insufficient Hip Separation (Low X-Factor)

What the Data Shows

Sportsbox reading: X-Factor below 40° at top of backswing. Pelvis and thorax rotating at similar rates throughout the backswing.

Ball flight signature: Loss of distance (5–15 yards below expectation for swing speed). Occasional blocked shots right due to insufficient hip lead in downswing.

Root cause: Usually thoracic or hip mobility restriction rather than a swing fault. The body cannot create separation it physically cannot achieve.

Intervention: TPI physical screen first — identify whether restriction is thoracic rotation, hip internal rotation, or lead hip mobility. Guide 06 mobility programme addresses all three. Technical work on separation before physical restriction is resolved produces minimal results.

Fault 2 — Early Pelvis Stall (Loss of Sequence)

What the Data Shows

Sportsbox reading: Pelvis velocity peaks early in the downswing (before P6 — the impact position), then decelerates. Thorax velocity peaks simultaneously with or after the pelvis rather than creating a lag-then-release pattern.

Ball flight signature: Inconsistent strike quality — some thin shots, some fats. Loss of distance. Occasional pull or pull-hook as the arms dominate through impact.

Intervention: Step drill (Guide 03) — this forces the pelvis to continue driving through impact. Ground Reaction Force work (Guide 39) — specifically the trail heel pressure drill that trains sustained hip thrust. The correction is felt as the hips "staying open longer" through impact.

Fault 3 — Insufficient Thorax Side-Bend at Impact

What the Data Shows

Sportsbox reading: Thorax side-bend below 25° at impact. Thorax remains relatively upright through the hitting zone rather than tilting toward the target.

Ball flight signature: High ball flight with reduced spin control. Thin contact pattern. Loss of compression — shots feel "floaty" rather than penetrating.

Intervention: The side-bend drill: place a headcover under the lead armpit and make half-swings without dropping it. This promotes the tilting motion required. Combine with HackMotion — insufficient side-bend often correlates with excess lead wrist extension through impact as the player unconsciously compensates.

Fault 4 — Excess Lead Shoulder Elevation at Impact

What the Data Shows

Sportsbox reading: Lead shoulder rising through impact rather than remaining stable. Often accompanied by insufficient side-bend (Fault 3).

Ball flight signature: Inconsistent strike — thin shots on well-struck swings; toe contact from compensatory shallowing. Loss of low point control.

Intervention: Wall drill — stand with trail shoulder touching a wall, make slow-motion swings feeling the trail shoulder pushing into the wall through impact. This promotes the compression of the trail side that keeps the lead shoulder down. HackMotion cross-reference: excess shoulder elevation often produces lead wrist cupping at impact.

Fault 5 — High Pelvis Sway (Lateral Drift)

What the Data Shows

Sportsbox reading: Pelvis lateral displacement exceeding 3–4 inches toward the target during the downswing, without the corresponding thrust component. Sway and thrust are different — sway moves laterally without rotation; thrust moves laterally with rotation.

Ball flight signature: Inconsistent face angle — shots go both left and right with similar swing feel. Distance loss. Fat shots when sway is excessive.

Intervention: Trail foot against a wall drill — feel the trail hip rotating around and through rather than moving sideways. GRF work (Guide 39) specifically targeting the trail leg push pattern. This fault is physical as much as technical — trail glute strength often insufficient to provide rotational drive without lateral movement.

Fault 6 — Arms-Dominant Sequence (Early Extension)

What the Data Shows

Sportsbox reading: Peak arm velocity occurring before peak thorax velocity. Body sequence inverted — arms driving ahead of the body rather than following it.

Ball flight signature: Pulls and pull-hooks from well-struck swings. Early extension (standing up through impact). Loss of depth at impact. High handicap version of this produces shanks; at elite level it produces loss of consistent face control.

Intervention: Slow-motion sequencing drills — practise the transition feeling the pelvis fire first, then thorax, then arms. The "pump drill": pause at the top, consciously move the pelvis toward the target before starting the arm downswing. Takes 4–8 weeks to internalise at competitive speed.

Fault 7 — Insufficient Lead Arm Rotation Rate

What the Data Shows

Sportsbox reading: Lead arm rotation rate below 350°/sec through impact. Arm rotating too slowly relative to body rotation — face arriving late and open.

Ball flight signature: Consistent push or push-fade. Face feels open at impact. Players with this fault typically have worked extensively on "holding the face off" and have overcorrected into insufficient rotation.

Intervention: HackMotion cross-reference first — confirm lead wrist extension at impact (the likely cause). Forearm rotation drills with a short iron: feel the lead forearm rolling over the trail forearm through impact. Do not attempt to add arm rotation before confirming the wrist position cause — addressing the symptom without addressing the cause produces inconsistency.

Fault 8 — Reverse Spine Angle

What the Data Shows

Sportsbox reading: Thorax tilting toward the target at the top of the backswing (rather than away from target). X-Factor present but created through an anatomically compromised position.

Ball flight signature: Inconsistent results with occasional excellent shots — the fault creates swing compensation chains that are unpredictable. Associated with lower back discomfort or pain (this fault is the primary biomechanical cause of amateur golfer lower back injury).

Intervention: Requires coach and physio collaboration — this is simultaneously a swing fault and a health risk. TPI screen recommended before technical intervention. The correction involves learning to coil the upper body away from the target rather than over it — a fundamental pattern change that takes 6–12 weeks minimum. Guide 13 (Injury Prevention) should be reviewed concurrently.

⭐

Priority note: If Sportsbox reveals Fault 8, address it before any other technical work. A reverse spine angle that produces occasional good ball-striking is not a performance tradeoff — it is a structural issue that compounds over thousands of swings. The injury risk alone justifies prioritising this correction above all other technical goals.
