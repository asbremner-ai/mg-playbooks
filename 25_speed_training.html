The Speed Training Framework

Club speed is the most impactful single physical variable in golf. Every additional 1 mph of driver speed adds approximately 2.5–3 yards of carry distance. Going from 95 mph to 110 mph — a realistic 12-month target — adds 37–45 yards off the tee. No technique change produces that return.

⚡ Speed = Distance = Scoring
Why Speed Training Works

The Neurological Basis of Overspeed Training

The golf swing is a neurological pattern, not just a physical movement. The brain governs how fast a pattern fires — and it places a protective speed ceiling based on what it considers safe. Overspeed training (swinging lighter implements faster than normal) teaches the nervous system to recruit more motor units and fire them more rapidly, raising the speed ceiling permanently.

The Overspeed Principle
Swing a lighter club 10–20% faster than normal
→ Nervous system adapts to higher firing rate
→ Returns to normal club with new speed ceiling
→ Speed transfers at 70–85% of overspeed gain

Example: 10 mph overspeed gain → 7–8.5 mph with driver
7 mph driver speed gain → +18–22 yards carry
Research by Dr. Sasho MacKenzie and the SuperSpeed Golf team shows average gains of 5–8% club speed after a 6-week protocol when performed 3× per week. Gains are cumulative — sustained training over 12–24 months produces compounding improvements.

Speed vs. Technique Trade-off

The Most Misunderstood Relationship in Golf Development

A common fear is that speed training will ruin technique. The evidence shows the opposite — when correctly sequenced, speed training does not disrupt technique and often improves it by forcing better kinematic sequencing. However, timing matters. Speed training done before technique is stabilised can entrench flaws.

The Science of Speed

Understanding what actually produces club speed allows you to train more effectively and avoid the common error of simply swinging harder — which produces tension, not speed.

🔬 Speed Biomechanics
The Five Speed Sources

Where Club Speed Actually Comes From

  • 1
    Ground force production (30–35% of speed): The largest single speed source. Pushing against the ground with the trail foot and then the lead foot creates vertical and horizontal ground reaction forces that drive the kinematic chain. Hip-dominant players with strong lower bodies have a structural speed advantage.
  • 2
    Hip-shoulder separation (20–25%): The rotational differential between the hips leading the shoulders into the downswing creates a rubber-band stretch through the thoracic spine. Greater separation = greater elastic recoil = more speed. This is the most trainable speed source.
  • 3
    Lead arm width (15–20%): A wide, extended lead arm in the backswing and through impact creates a longer radius and higher arc speed. Collapsing the lead arm at impact is one of the most significant speed leaks in amateur golfers.
  • 4
    Wrist lag and release timing (15–20%): Late release of wrist angles through the impact zone creates a whip effect. Early release (casting) dumps this speed before impact, reducing effective club speed by 8–15 mph.
  • 5
    Shaft flex and timing (5–10%): A correctly timed shaft flex-and-release through impact adds speed. The correct shaft stiffness profile for your speed and tempo allows the shaft to load and release optimally. Too stiff: no additional speed. Too soft: release timing is off.
Ground Reaction Forces — The Largest Speed Source Explained

What Force Plate Data Tells Us About Tour Speed

Force plate data (DST Golf, Swing Catalyst) consistently shows that vertical ground reaction force — not rotational force — is the largest single speed input in the elite golf swing. Tour players generate peak vertical forces of 1.5–2.5× their bodyweight through the lead leg at impact. This vertical push drives the kinematic chain upward, which then transfers rotationally through the thorax.

  • 1
    Trail foot push — lateral force: In the early downswing, the trail foot pushes laterally toward the target (not just rotationally). This creates the lateral ground reaction force that drives the pelvis laterally — the first phase of the correct transition. Players who spin rather than shift are not utilising this force at all.
  • 2
    Lead foot receive and vertical push: As the pelvis arrives over the lead foot, the lead leg straightens aggressively, creating a vertical push that drives the lead hip upward and outward. This vertical force is what "clears the hips" — it is not just rotation, it is a vertical drive that makes the rotation possible. This is the mechanism behind the visible lead leg straightening at impact in every elite player.
  • 3
    The flat-footed speed leak: Players who finish with the trail foot flat on the ground at impact have not expressed the trail foot ground force. The trail heel should be rising and the trail toe pushing through impact — this is evidence of the push being executed. A flat trail foot at impact = 10–20 mph of available speed left on the table, consistently.
⭐

Training implication: Jump-squat training and single-leg power development are direct ground force training for golf. A player who improves vertical force production by 10% in a jump squat will typically show measurable club speed increase within 6–8 weeks. This is why the Fitness guide's lower body power phases directly feed the speed protocol — they are training the same physical quality.

Smash Factor — Speed Without Quality Is Wasted

Turning Club Speed Into Ball Speed

Smash Factor Formula
Smash Factor = Ball Speed ÷ Club Speed

Maximum (driver): 1.50 (legal limit)
Tour average (driver): 1.48–1.49
10 HCP typical: 1.40–1.45
Poor strike: below 1.38

100 mph club speed × 1.50 = 150 mph ball speed = ~225 yds carry
100 mph club speed × 1.42 = 142 mph ball speed = ~211 yds carry
Improving smash factor from 1.42 to 1.48 at 100 mph adds 9 mph of ball speed — equivalent to 5–6 mph of additional club speed — with zero extra effort. Smash factor is improved by centring contact on the face. Speed training paired with strike quality training produces compounding gains.

The Training Protocol

The SuperSpeed Golf protocol is the most researched and validated speed training system for golfers. The core mechanism is systematic overspeed training with three clubs of different weights, progressing through a defined sequence of swing patterns.

🔄 SuperSpeed Protocol
The SuperSpeed System

Equipment and Core Mechanics

SuperSpeed Golf consists of three training clubs: green (lightest, ~20% lighter than driver), blue (neutral weight, ~10% lighter), and red (slightly heavier than driver, ~5% heavier). The progression from lightest to heaviest within each session creates maximum neurological overload followed by a speed-transfer effect when returning to the normal driver.

  • 1
    Lead hand dominant (right-handers swing left-handed): Every protocol begins with the opposite-hand swing. This neurological cross-training activates both sides of the motor cortex and has been shown to enhance speed gains by 15–20% compared to dominant-hand training alone.
  • 2
    Maximum effort on every swing: The neurological adaptation only occurs when swings are at genuine maximum effort. A 90% effort swing does not produce the neural overload required. Every swing must be an all-out attempt at maximum speed.
  • 3
    Measure after every set: Speed measurement after each set confirms the transfer effect is occurring. If speed does not increase after switching from light to heavy club, rest more between sets. The transfer is the entire mechanism — verify it every session.

Training Schedule

Sequencing speed work correctly within your overall golf training week is critical. Speed training placed on the wrong days — before a competition, during a swing-change phase, or without adequate recovery — can reduce performance rather than enhance it.

⚠️

The 48-hour rule: Speed training temporarily disrupts timing and sequence. Do not train within 48 hours of a competition. Players who speed train the day before a round consistently report timing issues — they swing faster but strike worse. The nervous system needs 48 hours to integrate the new speed stimulus before competition.

12-Month Speed Development Roadmap

Phases of Speed Progression

  • 1
    Months 1–2 (Foundation): Level 1 protocol, 3× per week. Establish baseline speed. Focus on maximum effort and correct technique (not gripping the light club too tightly). Expected gain: 3–5 mph.
  • 2
    Months 3–4 (Development): Level 2 protocol with stance variations. Add lower body activation exercises (see Fitness guide). Expected gain: additional 3–5 mph cumulative.
  • 3
    Months 5–6 (Consolidation): Reduce to 2× per week. Shift focus to smash factor improvement — speed is only valuable when contact is centred. Maintain gains while improving efficiency. Expected: gains plateau, contact improves.
  • 4
    Months 7–12 (Maintenance + Technique Integration): 1–2× per week maintenance. Work with coach on absorbing new speed into technique — turn speed potential into ball-striking reality. Expected: 8–15 mph total gain fully integrated by month 12.

When NOT to Train Speed

Speed training is powerful — and like all powerful tools, it can cause harm when applied incorrectly. These situations require pausing or deferring the speed protocol.

🚫 Stop Signals
Mandatory Pause Situations

When Speed Work Must Stop

  • 1
    Any upper body or back injury: Speed training places significant rotational stress on the lumbar spine, thoracic spine, lead hip, and trail elbow. Any pain, soreness, or active injury in these areas is an absolute contraindication. Stop immediately and consult a physio before resuming. Refer to the Injury Prevention guide.
  • 2
    Active swing reconstruction: If you are working with a coach on a fundamental change — grip, posture, takeaway, transition — defer speed training until the new movement is grooved under normal speed (approximately 4–6 weeks of consistent practice). Speed training entrenches the current pattern. Don't entrench the old one.
  • 3
    Within 48 hours of a competition: As noted above, timing disruption is temporary but real. Pause speed sessions Thursday or Friday if competing Saturday. Resume the following Monday.
  • 4
    During a heavy competition schedule: Tournament weeks with multiple rounds require full physical and neurological recovery. Speed training during a 4-round event week drains recovery resources needed for performance. Pause completely during multi-day events.
  • 5
    Fatigue or illness: Speed training on a fatigued nervous system produces negligible gains and higher injury risk. If you are tired, ill, or under-slept, the session is not worth doing. Rest and resume when recovered.
Reducing the Risk of Speed-Related Injury

The Three Non-Negotiable Prehab Requirements

  • 1
    Thoracic mobility must be adequate: Speed training amplifies rotational forces through the thoracic spine. If thoracic rotation is restricted, those forces transfer to the lumbar spine — a vulnerable area. Perform the thoracic rotation stretches from the Fitness guide daily before every speed session.
  • 2
    Lead hip mobility must be adequate: The lead hip must accept significant internal rotation force at impact under high-speed conditions. Restricted lead hip mobility is directly correlated with lead hip labrum issues in high-speed golfers. Hip 90/90 stretch daily.
  • 3
    Always warm up before speed training: Never perform overspeed swings cold. Minimum 5–10 minutes of progressive movement — arm circles, hip rotations, easy full swings with a normal club — before the first training club swing. Cold tissue under maximum rotational load is the primary injury mechanism.

Recognising and Breaking Through Plateaus

When Speed Stops Moving

  • 1
    A plateau after 4–6 weeks is normal: Neurological adaptation saturates at a given stimulus level. This is not failure — it is the signal to progress to the next level (Level 1 → Level 2, or adding stance variations). Plateau = time to change the stimulus.
  • 2
    Reduce before you increase: Paradoxically, a deload week (drop to 1× per week, half the swings) after a plateau often produces a speed breakthrough in the following week. The nervous system benefits from reduced load followed by a fresh overload stimulus.
  • 3
    Address physical limiters: If speed plateaus despite correct training, the limitation may be physical — thoracic mobility, hip mobility, or lack of lower body strength. Refer to the Fitness guide. Physical limiters cannot be overcome by training harder — only by addressing the specific restriction.
  • 4
    Check smash factor: A speed plateau sometimes masks smash factor improvement — you are hitting the same speed but with better contact, producing more distance. Track ball speed and carry distance independently from club speed. Progress may be continuing where you're not looking.
