🎯 What a Proximity Model Actually Is
Beyond Average Carry Distance

The Multi-Factor Proximity Prediction System

A carry distance (e.g. "7-iron carries 165 yards") is a single number that averages away all the complexity of a golf shot. A proximity model is a richer representation — it captures how your approach quality changes systematically with each input variable, allowing better decisions across the full range of conditions you encounter in competition.

Building Your Personal Proximity Model

The personal proximity model is built from your own Arccos data — not population averages. A 10-handicapper who is excellent at mid-iron approaches but poor from 80–100 yards has a different proximity model than a player with the reverse profile. The model needs to reflect your game, not an average player's game.

📐 The 4-Step Model Construction
Building from Arccos Data

Your Proximity Curve

  • 1
    Pull proximity data by distance bracket (30+ rounds). From Arccos, export approach shot proximity by distance bracket: 80–100, 100–125, 125–150, 150–175, 175–200 yards. Calculate the average and standard deviation of proximity in each bracket. The average is the central tendency; the SD is the dispersion (reliability).
  • 2
    Plot your proximity curve. Chart average proximity against distance. Most players have a non-linear relationship — proximity degrades faster beyond a certain distance threshold. For most 5–10 handicappers, this threshold is around 150 yards. Knowing precisely where your proximity curve bends is more valuable than knowing your average.
  • 3
    Identify your strongest distance band. The bracket where your proximity-to-benchmark ratio is highest. This is your scoring zone — the distance from which you most reliably threaten birdies. Design your course management (Guide 40) to create approach shots from this distance wherever possible.
  • 4
    Identify your most unreliable distance band. The bracket with the highest standard deviation (most inconsistent results) — not necessarily the worst average proximity. This is the distance where your shot quality is most variable and where risk-adjusted club selection matters most. From this distance, aim at the centre of the green rather than the flag, more aggressively than from your other brackets.

The Compound Condition Problem

When Multiple Adjustments Apply Simultaneously

Real approach shots often combine multiple condition adjustments — rough lie in a crosswind on an uphill slope, for example. The compound effect is not simply additive; some combinations are more destabilising than others.

  • 1
    Rank the adjustments by impact. In any compound condition, identify the most impactful single factor (usually wind strength or lie type) and apply that adjustment in full. Apply secondary adjustments at 50% — do not try to precisely calculate the interaction of three adjustments simultaneously.
  • 2
    When in doubt, widen the target. Under compound conditions, uncertainty increases. The correct response is to widen the acceptable outcome zone — aim for the fat of the green, accept a 30-foot putt as success, and reduce the ambition of the shot accordingly. Compound conditions are not the moments for pin-hunting.
  • 3
    The over-adjustment trap. A common error: applying every adjustment at full value produces an over-corrected shot that misses the other way. Under compound conditions, make one primary adjustment and be ready to accept the outcome distribution — do not try to engineer a perfect result.

Proximity Under Pressure

Approach play degrades under competitive pressure in a measurable and consistent way. Quantifying your personal pressure adjustment factor from Arccos data allows you to make better club and aim point decisions in high-stakes situations.

📉 The Pressure Proximity Gap
Measuring Your Pressure Performance Gap

How to Quantify Your Competitive Degradation

  • 1
    Split your Arccos data by event type. Tag events as: casual rounds, regular club competition, club championship/significant events. For each category, calculate average SG: APP and average proximity by distance bracket. The gap between casual and significant event performance is your personal pressure adjustment factor.
  • 2
    Typical findings: Most players lose 15–30% of approach proximity under high-pressure conditions. A player who averages 28 feet from the flag at 150 yards in casual rounds might average 34–36 feet in club championships. If you find no gap, you are either genuinely pressure-immune (rare) or not tracking event type (more likely).
  • 3
    Apply the pressure adjustment to aim point selection. In high-pressure events, shift your aim point away from the flag by an additional distance equal to your pressure proximity gap. If the pressure adjustment adds 6 feet of average proximity, move the aim point 6 feet toward the safe side — accounting for the fact that your shots will be slightly less precise than normal.
Reducing the Pressure Gap Over Time

Training That Narrows the Gap Specifically

The pressure proximity gap is not fixed — it narrows with deliberate pressure exposure over time. Three training methods specifically target it:

MethodStructureEffect
Competition simulation sessionsEvery approach shot in a 60-minute session is played with a consequence (£1 per shot over a proximity target, or shots that don't make the green cost a forfeit)Builds pressure habituation — reduces the arousal spike in real competition
One-chance drillsEvery approach shot from a given distance is played once only, with full pre-shot routine. No warm-up shots. No retakes.Replicates competition conditions — the first shot matters
Post-pressure baseline checksAfter completing a high-pressure drill sequence, immediately hit 5 baseline shots to the same target. Compare proximity to normal baseline.Measures the acute pressure effect; over time, tracks how it narrows with practice

Elite Club Gapping

Standard club gapping (covered in Guide 22) establishes carry distances per club. Elite club gapping goes further — eliminating transition distances where reliability collapses, optimising gap distribution for your most common approach distances, and using Mevo data to verify gap consistency across conditions.

⚙️ Beyond the Basic Gap Analysis
The Transition Distance Problem

Distances Where Your Reliability Collapses

Between full-swing and partial-swing territory for any club, there is a transition distance — a specific yardage where you are reluctant to hit either a full shot (too much club) or a partial shot (uncertain contact). Players who frequently face this distance from their most common approach zones lose proximity disproportionately.

  • 1
    Identify your transition distances. From Arccos, find the specific yardage brackets where your proximity standard deviation is highest relative to adjacent brackets. These are your transition distances — where reliability drops because you are neither fully committed to a full swing nor confident in the partial.
  • 2
    Reconfigure gapping to eliminate them. If you have a transition distance at 118 yards (between a full gap wedge and a three-quarter 9-iron), add a club that carries 118 yards with a comfortable full swing. This may mean adding a 50° wedge, adjusting lofts, or identifying a partial shot with a different club that carries precisely that distance reliably.
  • 3
    Verify in a Mevo session. After reconfiguration, run a Mevo session hitting 15 shots to each formerly problematic distance with the new configuration. Confirm that the standard deviation has reduced. The standard deviation — not the average — is the measure of success in gapping work.
Condition-Adjusted Gapping

Why Your Summer Gap Table Is Wrong for Winter

Club carry distances change significantly with temperature (approximately 1.5–2 yards per 10°C change), humidity, altitude, and ball compression at cold temperatures. A single gap table built on summer range sessions will produce systematic under-clubbing in cold UK winter conditions.

ConditionCarry Adjustment per ClubPractical Response
Temperature drop from 20°C to 5°C−4 to −6 yards typicalMaintain a winter gap table with all distances reduced; use it from November to March
Low humidity / dry conditions+2 to +3 yardsMinor — note it but usually within normal shot variance
High humidity / wet conditions−2 to −3 yardsNote and adjust on days with heavy rain; wet ball and club face can add up to −5 yards
Links / coastal (denser salt air)−1 to −2 yards (minimal)Less significant than temperature; primarily affects spin and trajectory, not carry significantly

Trajectory Mastery

A single trajectory per club is a limitation that becomes increasingly costly at elite level. Owning two trajectories per club — standard and knockdown — doubles the range of approach situations you can handle optimally.

🏹 The Two-Trajectory System
Standard and Knockdown for Every Club

The Minimum Trajectory Library for Elite Approach Play

TrajectorySetupWhen to UseProximity Impact
StandardBall position and hands as normal; full swingNormal conditions; receptive greens; no significant windBaseline proximity
Knockdown (−30–40% height)Ball 1–2 inches further back in stance; hands forward; abbreviated follow-through to hip heightHeadwind (15+ mph); firm greens requiring a running approach; under trees+15–20% proximity in wind vs. standard trajectory
⭐

Building the knockdown: The knockdown iron is not a natural shot for most players — it requires deliberate practice. Use the blocked acquisition protocol from Guide 38 (200 repetitions to autonomous stage) before introducing it in competition. The most common error: stopping the follow-through too abruptly, creating a jerky deceleration through impact. The follow-through should feel abbreviated but smooth, not jabbed.

The One-Shot Protocol

The Most Efficient Approach Practice Drill

  • 1
    Set up 5 targets at different distances within your priority bracket. For example, if your 125–150 yard bracket is the priority: targets at 127, 133, 138, 143, and 149 yards.
  • 2
    Play one shot to each target in sequence, with full pre-shot routine. No warm-up shots to a target before playing it. No re-hits. One shot per target — exactly as in competition.
  • 3
    Record the result (on green / off green / proximity if measurable). After completing all 5 targets, note the aggregate performance. Repeat the sequence 4 times for a 20-shot session.
  • 4
    Compare to baseline proximity. Over time, this session format tracks your real-world approach performance better than any blocked practice metric. The "one shot to each distance" protocol replicates competition conditions and gives the most honest measure of your actual approach capability.
